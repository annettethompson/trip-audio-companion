# Chapter 11: 1923: The Last of the Pack Strings
*Estimated duration: 6.5 minutes | Word count: 895*

In the spring of 1923, a man named Jack Odenbaugh was working in the machine shops of the Rio Grande Southern Railroad in Ridgway, Colorado. He had a problem to solve. The railroad was losing money. Had been losing money, in various degrees of severity, for thirty years. The silver crash of 1893 had never fully resolved for the San Juan economy. Some gold mines kept the line alive, and the railroad kept the high mountain towns alive, but the margin was always thin and getting thinner.

The automobile was coming. Roads were being improved, slowly, improbably, across terrain that had resisted wheeled vehicles since the beginning. The narrow-gauge mountain railroad, the marvel of engineering that had connected Ridgway to Telluride to Rico to Durango, was facing a competitor it could not outmaneuver: internal combustion and the expanding American road network.

Odenbaugh was not yet building the Galloping Geese in 1923; that improvisation would come in 1931. But the economic pressure that produced those famous hybrid contraptions was already acute. The railroad shops in Ridgway were already looking for ways to reduce the cost of moving a few passengers and some freight over 162 miles of mountain track.

Meanwhile, up in the high country, another technology was maintaining its usefulness in the face of all modern competition: the mule.

Pack mule strings were still operating regularly in the San Juan backcountry in 1923. The reason was simple. There were still mine camps, still high-elevation operations, still places that the railroad reached but the secondary roads from the railroad did not. A skilled mule packer in 1923 was an elite worker, commanding premium wages for a skill that took years to develop and that no machine had yet replaced. A thirty-mule string, loaded at about 250 pounds per animal, could carry 7,500 pounds of supplies, equipment, or ore up trails that no vehicle could follow. The mules moved steadily, reliably, in single file, with a bell mare at the front whose sound kept the string oriented in rough terrain or poor visibility.

The relationship between packer and mule was not sentimental in the romantic sense. It was professional. A packer knew each animal's quirks, its load tolerance, its tendency to favor a particular side, its reliability on narrow trail above a dropoff. The mules were working animals in the most serious sense: their sure-footedness and endurance in vertical terrain was something no draft horse could match, and certainly no early truck.

This is the world the 1923 television series depicts in its Yellowstone version, and it is accurate to the period across the broader mountain West. The Duttons in 1923 are navigating exactly this transition: horses and mules as practical daily technology, automobiles arriving from the cities as a disruptive force, the old economy of extraction and ranching under pressure from modernity. The cowboys in the Ridgway of 1923 would have recognized the Dutton world immediately. The tools were the same. The breeds were the same, Quarter Horse and working draft, the American Quarter Horse having been developed specifically for ranch work over the previous century and now the dominant working horse from Montana to Colorado. The relationship between rider and horse was the same: practical, daily, entirely non-romantic from the inside.

Here is one specific detail that connects Ridgway's 1923 landscape to the 1923 world of the show. By that spring, the Ute removal from the Uncompahgre was forty-two years in the past. Not ancient history. The cowboys and ranchers working the valley in 1923 had fathers and grandfathers who had lived through it. Some of the older ranch hands would have been children when the army marched the Tabeguache people out. The land being ranched had been someone else's homeland within living memory, and the legal transfer of that land had been anything but clean.

The 1923 show takes seriously this particular moral texture of the period: the settlers and ranchers are not villains in cartoon form, but they are living on land taken by force from people who are not gone, just displaced. The Duttons know this. Some of the characters grapple with it. The show is most interesting when it refuses to resolve the contradiction, because the contradiction was not resolved in the historical West either. It is still not resolved.

When you drive through the Ridgway valley and see the ranches, the irrigated hay meadows, the cottonwood-lined river bottom, you are looking at a landscape that was shaped by that removal. The agricultural infrastructure, the water rights, the land ownership patterns: all of it flows from the 1881 dispossession. The valley is beautiful. The history underneath it is not simple.

One more 1923 moment worth noting. The Silverton Narrow Gauge, which still runs today as a tourist railroad between Durango and Silverton, was in 1923 the primary supply line for Silverton. There was no paved road into Silverton until 1884, and that road, the precursor to the Million Dollar Highway, was still a rough and sometimes impassable toll road. The railroad was more reliable than the road in early spring when snowmelt made the high passes dangerous. In 1923, Silverton was as isolated as it had ever been during the long months of deep snow.

The men who kept the Rio Grande Southern and the Durango-Silverton running in that era were not just railroad workers. They were the reason small mountain towns survived.
