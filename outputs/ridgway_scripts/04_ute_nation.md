# Chapter 04: The Tabeguache People
*Estimated duration: 8.0 minutes | Word count: 1,100*

The town of Ouray is named after a man who tried harder than almost anyone in the history of American diplomacy to prevent a catastrophe he could already see coming, and who failed not for lack of intelligence or effort but because the forces arrayed against him were simply too large, too determined, and too indifferent to anything he valued.

Chief Ouray was born around 1833, and his name meant Arrow in the Ute language. He was the leader of the Tabeguache band, also called the Uncompahgre Ute, whose traditional territory centered on this valley, the Uncompahgre River drainage from its mountain headwaters down into the lower desert plateau country. His people had lived in this landscape for thousands of years. They were horse people, the first Rocky Mountain tribe to acquire horses from the Spanish in the early eighteenth century, and horses transformed their culture entirely. The Ute became extraordinarily capable hunters and warriors, ranging widely across Colorado, Utah, and New Mexico, organized into loosely affiliated bands rather than a single centralized polity.

The Uncompahgre River valley was central to their world. The hot springs at what is now Ouray and Ridgway were healing places. The valley provided winter grazing for horses. The mountains to the south offered summer game. The Ute had a sophisticated, highly mobile economy that used the landscape across all elevations and seasons, moving with the snowmelt up into the high country and retreating to the lower valleys when winter returned. They knew every spring, every game trail, every mineral seep in the San Juan country.

Ouray himself was multilingual, speaking Ute, Spanish, and English fluently. He worked as a government interpreter and negotiated treaty after treaty with Washington, always trying to preserve some portion of his people's homeland while conceding to the relentless pressure for more land. The 1868 Brunot Agreement ceded much of the San Juan high country, where the mining boom was igniting. He accepted the inevitable, hoping to preserve the valley at least.

Then Nathan Meeker arrived.

Meeker was appointed as the Indian Agent at the White River Ute agency in northwestern Colorado in 1878, and he was a man of absolute moral certainty about what Ute people needed to become. He wanted them to plow fields, grow crops, and abandon the horse culture that was the foundation of their entire identity. He began plowing up the tribe's horse pastures to plant crops. He called in the army when the Ute resisted.

On September 29, 1879, members of the White River Ute killed Meeker and nine other agency employees. On the same day, a battle erupted at Milk Creek between Ute warriors and U.S. Army troops under Major Thomas Thornburgh, killing the major and thirteen soldiers. This became known as the Meeker Massacre, though the term obscures the larger context of provocation and dispossession that preceded it.

Ouray and his wife Chipeta had no involvement in these events. The Uncompahgre Ute, his own band, had not participated. But in the political aftermath in Washington, the distinction between Ute bands mattered very little. The editorial cry that went up was blunt: "The Utes Must Go." Colorado's new newspaper headlines were not subtle about what was wanted.

Ouray worked desperately to negotiate the release of the women and children taken hostage during the White River events. He succeeded. He traveled to Washington to negotiate again, as he had so many times before. He was the most prominent Native American statesman of his era, respected even by the officials who were trying to dispossess him. But the political outcome was already decided.

Ouray died in August 1880, before the removal he had spent his life trying to prevent. He was perhaps fifty years old. He did not have to witness it. His wife Chipeta did.

On August 28, 1881, the Uncompahgre Ute were marched from their homeland under the escort of the U.S. Army, commanded by Colonel Ranald MacKenzie. They were relocated to what became the Ouray Reservation in Utah. They had been promised good land. The land they were given was inadequate and distant. The people who had lived in the Uncompahgre Valley for generations were simply gone, removed within a matter of weeks.

The town of Ouray, Colorado was named after the chief. There is something worth sitting with in that. The settlers who displaced his people named their town after him, essentially a trophy naming. Yet there is also a grudging acknowledgment in it: even the people who wanted him gone recognized that Ouray had been a person of exceptional stature. The naming is complicated in the way that so much of American Western history is complicated, honoring the individual while erasing the people.

Here is the 1923 connection that matters. By the time of the television show, by the time of the Dutton family's conflicts on Yellowstone, the Ute removal from the Uncompahgre was only forty-two years in the past. Not history. Living memory. The oldest Ute people in 1923 had been alive during the removal. Their parents had been children in this valley. The wounds were not historical abstractions; they were personal and fresh.

The ranchers and cowboys of 1923 Colorado, the people the Dutton world depicts, were farming and running cattle on land that had been someone else's home within their own parents' or grandparents' lifetimes. The legal process of dispossession was complete. The moral reckoning had barely begun. In some respects, it still has not.

The Ute Mountain Ute and the Southern Ute Tribe have reservations today in the Four Corners region of southwestern Colorado. Their relationship to the San Juan country, to the Uncompahgre Valley, is attenuated by distance and history but not severed. When you soak tonight in the waters the Tabeguache called healing waters, you are using something they understood long before the settlers arrived with their railroads and their claims.
