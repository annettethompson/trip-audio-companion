# Chapter 03: The Ancient Foundation
*Estimated duration: 7.0 minutes | Word count: 965*

Everything west of Ridgway and Ouray is old in a way the San Juan volcanics are not. Very old. The Uncompahgre Plateau, that long elevated tableland stretching northwest from Ridgway toward Grand Junction, is an exposed piece of the North American continental basement, the bedrock that everything else in Colorado was built upon or against. At its core are metamorphic and igneous rocks that crystallized 1.7 billion years ago, deep in a Precambrian mountain range that was itself eroded flat before the Cambrian Period even began. Those rocks represent nearly forty percent of Earth's entire history.

To understand the Uncompahgre Plateau, you need to understand a concept called a fault block uplift. Unlike the Laramide ranges, which were compressed and folded by horizontal pressure, the Uncompahgre Plateau rose along a system of deep faults, with one side of the fault riding up relative to the other in a tilted block. The plateau is essentially a giant slice of crust that has been tilted and raised, exposing deep rock on its eastern edge while the western edge is relatively lower. The eastern flank of the plateau, where the Uncompahgre River has carved its gorge on the approach to Ouray, is where this structural complexity becomes visually spectacular.

The river has cut down through the tilted rock layers like a saw through wood, exposing the entire stratigraphic sequence from the ancient Precambrian basement at the bottom of the gorge up through layer after layer of younger sedimentary rock to the volcanic material at the top. It is, in geological terms, a nearly complete record of half a billion years of Earth history in a single canyon cross-section.

But the rock that will catch your eye on the roadcuts, the material that gives this entire landscape its distinctive red-orange palette, is the Cutler Formation. The Cutler is a Permian-age sequence of sandstones, siltstones, and conglomerates deposited roughly 280 million years ago. The grains are the same brilliant rusty red you see in the canyon walls near Ridgway and in the roadcuts on the southern approach to the Uncompahgre Gorge. That color is hematite, iron oxide, the same compound that makes rust red, coating individual grains as the sediment was deposited.

Those red sediments have a fascinating source. During the Pennsylvanian and Permian Periods, a mountain range called the Ancestral Uncompahgre Uplift stood where the plateau stands today. This range was as high as the modern Rockies, perhaps higher, and it was composed largely of the same Precambrian crystalline rocks that now form the plateau's core. As those mountains eroded, rivers carried the sediment westward and southwestward, where it accumulated as alluvial fans hundreds of meters thick. The Cutler Formation at its type locality, the area just north of Ouray where it was first formally described, reaches over 300 meters thick.

Then the mountains subsided. The Ancestral Uncompahgre Uplift eroded almost to a flat surface during the Mesozoic, and younger sediments were deposited on top. The Triassic, Jurassic, and Cretaceous Periods each added their layers. The Colorado Plateau region accumulated thousands of meters of additional sediment.

Then the Laramide Orogeny arrived, 70 to 50 million years ago, and the entire region started rising again. And then, overlapping with the late stages of that uplift, came the volcanic cataclysm of the San Juan Volcanic Field, erupting from 35 to 25 million years ago, blanketing the region in thousands of meters of ignimbrite and volcanic debris.

What you see today around Ridgway and Ouray is the result of erosion stripping all that away. The Uncompahgre River and its tributaries have been cutting downward for the past five million years or so, accelerated by the uplift of the Colorado Plateau, removing the younger material and exposing the ancient Cutler red beds and, in the deepest parts of the Ouray canyon, the Precambrian basement itself.

At the very bottom of the Uncompahgre Gorge near Ouray, where the river has cut down to its deepest, you can see dark schist and granite that formed almost two billion years ago. Stand at the rim of the canyon above the town and you are looking through roughly 800 meters of exposed rock representing the oldest visible basement in the region. The contact between the Precambrian basement and the overlying Cambrian sediments, where the rock sequence resumes after an enormous gap, represents an unconformity of roughly 500 million years. Five hundred million years of Earth history simply missing from the record, eroded away and gone before new sediment began to accumulate.

Geologists call this an angular unconformity when the rock layers below tilt at a different angle than the layers above, and a disconformity when both packages are parallel but there is still a gap in time. At Ouray, the relationship is complex. But the gap is real. Look at the canyon walls and you are looking directly at time missing from the record, a half-billion-year absence visible in a single rock contact.

The plateau itself, viewed from a distance, looks deceptively simple: a broad elevated surface with a relatively flat top. But that surface is capped by resistant Mesozoic sandstones, the same formations that produce the canyon country landscapes to the west in Utah. Drive out onto the plateau and you enter pinyon-juniper woodland, sagebrush, eroded washes. The plateau is topographically gentle on top, dramatically eroded on its edges.

From Ridgway, driving south toward Ouray on US 550, you have the Uncompahgre Plateau rising to your right and the volcanic San Juans rising to your left, and in the valley bottom the red Cutler beds exposed in roadcuts and canyon walls. Two completely different geological stories, billions of years apart in age, visible simultaneously. It is genuinely unusual to be able to see this kind of contrast across a single valley.

Bob, watch the roadcuts as you drop into the Uncompahgre Gorge on US 550 south of Ridgway. The sequence of color changes tells you the whole story: orange-red Cutler, then darker gray-brown volcanic material above. The contact between them is the boundary between the ancient continental past and the fiery volcanic present.
