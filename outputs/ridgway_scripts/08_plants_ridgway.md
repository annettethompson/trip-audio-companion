# Chapter 08: Plants of the Mountain Transition
*Estimated duration: 7.0 minutes | Word count: 960*

The drive from Glenwood Springs to Ridgway is, among other things, a botanical journey through four entirely different plant communities, and if you are paying attention to what grows along the roadside rather than just what rises above the skyline, you will see that transition clearly.

Glenwood Canyon is a riparian world, defined by the Colorado River and the moisture it creates. The plants there, the cottonwoods and willows and river birches, the wild grape climbing the canyon walls, the box elder in the drainages, are all adapted to abundant water, deep alluvial soil, and a protected microclimate where the canyon walls moderate extremes. You covered that country yesterday.

The Crystal River Valley, where CO-133 takes you south of Carbondale, introduces the first significant shift. Narrowleaf cottonwood continues along the river itself, its leaves trembling with that distinctive silver flash in the wind, completely different from the broad-leafed plains cottonwood of lower elevations. Wild rose appears in dense thickets along the riverbank. Chokecherry forms the understory at the transition between riparian and slope. Serviceberry, Amelanchier alnifolia, begins to appear on drier slopes, a shrub with small white flowers in spring and sweet, dark blue berries in late summer that were a dietary staple for the Ute people, eaten fresh, dried, and mixed into pemmican. The berries are still edible and worth tasting if you find them later in the season.

At the middle elevations between 6,000 and 8,000 feet, Gambel oak begins to dominate large areas of south-facing and west-facing slopes. Gambel oak is one of the most ecologically important plants in the Colorado foothills and mountain shrubland zones, and it is almost always overlooked because it is not majestic. It does not grow tall. It spreads in dense clonal thickets from a single root system, the individual stems rarely reaching more than fifteen or twenty feet, often much shorter. In autumn it turns a brilliant bronze-orange that rivals aspen in saturation, but most people are looking at the aspens.

What Gambel oak provides ecologically is extraordinary habitat complexity. The dense thickets create cover that almost nothing else provides at mid-elevation. Mule deer and black bears use Gambel oak stands intensively, especially in autumn when the acorns fall. Acorns are extraordinarily calorie-dense, roughly six calories per gram, and a bear in the pre-denning hyperphagia phase of September and October will spend most of its day moving through oak thickets consuming acorns. Wild turkeys, Steller's jays, and scrub jays harvest and cache acorns, inadvertently planting new oak trees across the landscape. The relationship between oaks and corvids is one of the key seed-dispersal partnerships in Western mountain ecology.

The Ute used Gambel oak in multiple ways. The acorns were processed to remove tannins, through repeated leaching with water, and ground into a flour used for bread and mush. The bark has antimicrobial properties that were exploited in traditional medicine. Oak galls, the abnormal growths produced when certain wasps lay eggs in oak tissue, were used as a source of tannic acid for treating leather and as a dye source.

On the western side of McClure Pass and down into the North Fork Valley, the vegetation changes again as elevation drops and the climate dries. Pinyon pine begins to appear, the two-needle pinyon that produces the large, fat seeds marketed as pine nuts. Juniper comes in alongside it, the classic pinyon-juniper woodland that characterizes vast areas of the Colorado Plateau and the lower reaches of the Colorado mountains. These are drought-adapted trees, slow-growing, deeply rooted, able to persist on less than fifteen inches of annual precipitation.

But the most dramatic botanical shift comes when you drop out of the foothills entirely and into the high desert of the Montrose and Ridgway valleys. Here the dominant plant is big sagebrush, Artemisia tridentata, and it deserves far more attention than it usually receives.

Big sagebrush is the most widespread native shrub in the Intermountain West, covering more than 165 million acres of the United States. In healthy sagebrush steppe, the plants grow knee- to waist-high, gray-green, their leaves small, lobed, and covered with silvery-white hairs that reflect sunlight and reduce water loss. The three-toothed shape of the individual leaves gives the species its scientific name, tridentata: three-toothed.

The smell of sagebrush is one of the defining sensory experiences of the American West, and it is most intense when the plant is wet. After rain, or even after morning dew, the volatile oils in the leaves vaporize and the scent hits you immediately: herbal, slightly medicinal, unmistakably itself. The aromatic compounds are primarily camphor, cineole, and camphene, terpenoids produced as defenses against insect herbivory and, some researchers believe, as signals in plant-to-plant communication. Roll a leaf between your fingers at any time and you will get a concentrated hit of the scent.

The Ute and other Great Basin peoples had extensive medicinal uses for big sagebrush. The leaves and stems were boiled to make a tea used to treat colds, fevers, and coughs. The antimicrobial properties are real: sagebrush extracts have demonstrated activity against certain bacterial and fungal strains in laboratory testing. The smoke from burning sagebrush was used ceremonially and practically, for fumigation of sleeping spaces and for repelling insects.

Along the Uncompahgre River as it flows through the Ridgway valley, cottonwood gallery forest returns. These are big trees in the bottomland, their canopies creating a green corridor visible from a distance in an otherwise relatively open landscape. In late May, the cottonwoods are releasing their seeds in white cottony masses that drift on the wind, sometimes so thickly they look like unseasonal snow. It is not unusual to find a car coated in cottonwood fluff after parking near the river for an hour.

Near the Orvis property and the other springs in the immediate area, look for the plants that follow water: willows marking any seep or drainage, watercress in the cold spring inflows, cattail marshes where standing water persists. These micro-habitats are particularly productive for birds: swallows hunting insects over the water surface, red-winged blackbirds in the cattails, belted kingfishers diving from overhanging branches.

The landscape around Ridgway is not the lush canyon world of Glenwood. It is drier, more open, more muted in its colors. But it is not spare. It is simply calibrated to different terms.
