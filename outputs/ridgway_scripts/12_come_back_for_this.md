# Chapter 12: Trails, Thrills, and Tables
*Estimated duration: 5.5 minutes | Word count: 760*

The San Juan country around Ridgway and Ouray is one of the most activity-dense landscapes in Colorado, and tonight's soak is just the beginning of what this area offers. Here is what you need to put on the list before you leave.

For Bob, the top of the list has to be the Ouray Via Ferrata. Via ferrata, Italian for iron road, is a style of mountain climbing developed in the Alps where iron rungs, cables, and anchors are drilled into cliff faces to make otherwise technical routes accessible to non-technical climbers. Ouray has three separate via ferrata routes, and they were the first established via ferrata routes in the United States when the original route opened. The Gold Mountain Via Ferrata is the most ambitious: it climbs 1,200 vertical feet across twelve pitches and ends on a suspension bridge spanning a 300-foot gap above the canyon. The main Ouray Via Ferrata routes follow the Uncompahgre Gorge, with the river audible and sometimes visible hundreds of feet below as you traverse the iron rungs above it. The routes are rated moderate to moderately difficult, accessible to anyone who is comfortable with exposure and follows the safety gear requirements, which include a helmet, harness, and via ferrata lanyard. You can go unguided or hire an outfitter through Basecamp Ouray or San Juan Mountain Guides. If you have done via ferrata in Europe, these routes will feel familiar and excellent. If you have not, they are a reasonable first introduction to the form.

In winter, come back for the Ouray Ice Park. The park occupies the Uncompahgre Gorge, the same box canyon the via ferrata uses in summer, and it is the world's first man-made ice climbing park and the largest. The park began in the early 1990s when a leaky hydroelectric pipe in the gorge was creating natural ice formations that early climbers started using. Bill Whitt and Gary Wild, working to revitalize Ouray's economy after mine closures, partnered with the city to develop the concept systematically. The park now uses excess water from the city's spring-fed supply, channeled through irrigation pipes above the canyon walls, sprayed down cliff faces each night through the winter to build ice columns, curtains, and features. The park is free to climb and produces close to two miles of vertical ice terrain. It is the reason Ouray bills itself as the ice climbing capital of the world, and that claim is not exaggerated.

For hiking, the Bear Creek National Recreation Trail outside Ouray is the strongest recommendation in the immediate area. The trailhead is at the south end of Ouray on US 550, and the trail follows Bear Creek up through the canyon into the volcanic terrain, passing historic mine ruins, gaining significant elevation, and offering views into the caldera geology that are simply not visible from the road. The mining ruins along the route are intact enough to give you a sense of the physical scale of what the nineteenth-century miners actually built in this terrain: ore processing structures, tram cables, tunnel portals cut directly into the cliff face. The trail is out-and-back, roughly ten miles and significant elevation gain for the full route, but the lower section alone rewards a shorter hike.

The Jeep trails are worth a separate mention. The Ouray county four-wheel-drive road network is among the most extensive and spectacular in Colorado, reaching passes above 13,000 feet, traversing the caldera terrain, passing through the high volcanic country that is inaccessible by any other ground vehicle. Engineer Pass, Imogene Pass, and the roads around Red Mountain are well-known routes in the four-wheel-drive community. If you have a capable vehicle or are willing to rent one, these roads access landscape that most visitors never see. They also pass through the most concentrated remaining evidence of the mining era: buildings, equipment, and ore processing structures at elevations where nothing has been done to clean them up or make them accessible.

For the table, both Ridgway and Ouray have small but quality dining scenes. In Ridgway, the True Grit Cafe is the town's signature spot, a classic American diner that commits fully to its John Wayne identity and produces reliably good food in a setting that earns its tourist appeal honestly. Bella Vino brings Italian sensibility to a mountain valley setting and does it genuinely well. Colorado Boy Pub and Brewery is the local brewing operation, worth a pint and a pizza. In Ouray, Outlaw Restaurant has a strong reputation for steak and seafood, and Maggie's Kitchen is the local recommendation for a burger and a mountain view. Kami's Samis is the breakfast and lunch choice for sandwiches with a quality that punches above the town's size.

One more thing to leave yourself time for: the Ridgway Reservoir at sunrise. Set an alarm. Drive to the state park entrance before the wind comes up. The mountain reflection in the still water, the early light on the San Juan peaks, and the osprey beginning to work the reservoir surface is as good a morning scene as this part of Colorado offers. It does not require hiking boots or a reservation. It only requires getting up.
