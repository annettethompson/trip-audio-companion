# Chapter 02: Reading the Road: Glenwood to Ridgway
*Estimated duration: 7.0 minutes | Word count: 965*

The most scenic route from Glenwood Springs to Ridgway does not follow the interstate. You want Colorado 133 south from Carbondale, winding down the Crystal River Valley, over McClure Pass, through the wine country of the North Fork, across the high-desert approach to Montrose, and then south on US 550 as the San Juans rise ahead of you like a dark wall. It is roughly three hours if you do not stop. You will stop.

Leaving Carbondale, the road follows the Crystal River upstream. This is a different river character than the Colorado at Glenwood. The Crystal is narrower, colder, gin-clear, and fast. It drains the western flanks of the Elk Mountains, a range that is geologically distinct from both the older Glenwood country and the volcanic San Juans to the south. You are in a transitional zone here. The rocks around you shift from the limestone and shale of the Colorado Plateau margins toward the igneous and metamorphic rocks of the central Rockies.

About twelve miles up from Carbondale, the road passes through Redstone. This tiny village is worth slowing down for, because it contains one of the stranger chapters in Colorado labor history, and it is absolutely a 1923 story.

John Cleveland Osgood, the founder of Colorado Fuel and Iron, the largest coal company in the West, chose this narrow valley to build a coking plant starting in 1899. He constructed 250 beehive coke ovens on the west bank of the Crystal River. These are still visible from the road, a row of domed brick ovens that look like the ruins of a Roman bath complex. Osgood had grand ambitions. He also built his workers a planned community, with electricity, running water, and decent housing, unusual for the era of brutal company towns. And further up the valley from the workers' cottages, he built himself a 42-room Tudor manor house he called Cleveholm, later known as Redstone Castle. It sits above the river, stone-turreted, improbable, surrounded by mountain slopes.

By the 1920s, the Redstone enterprise was finished. Changes in steel-making economics had rendered the Crystal Valley coke unprofitable. Osgood's empire had contracted sharply. The coke ovens stood cold. The workers had left. The castle was still there, but the industrial dream that built it was already history. A traveler through this valley in 1923 would have seen exactly what you see today: the brick ovens, the empty workers' cottages, and the stubborn castle still presiding over a valley that no longer needed it.

Continuing south, the road climbs into aspen forests and the vegetation shifts abruptly. Aspen groves appear first, then Engelmann spruce moves in on the north-facing slopes. At around 8,000 feet, the snowpack lingers into June, and the aspens are just beginning to leaf. The Crystal River grows smaller as you approach its headwaters.

McClure Pass sits at 8,755 feet and marks a significant ecological and geological transition. To the east are the headwaters of the Crystal River and the Elk Mountains. To the west, the drainage reverses and flows into the North Fork of the Gunnison River. This is also where you cross from one geologic province into another. The Elk Mountains are largely intrusive igneous rock, granite and related types. The western slope of McClure Pass descends into country underlain by more diverse sedimentary and volcanic formations.

The descent into the North Fork Valley is one of the more surprising landscapes in Colorado. By the time you reach Paonia and Hotchkiss, you are at roughly 5,600 feet, in a valley that functions as one of Colorado's premier fruit-growing and wine-producing regions. The elevation and the western slope exposure create a temperature regime warm enough for Chardonnay, Riesling, and even Syrah grapes. Orchards appear. The sagebrush on the hillsides contrasts with the irrigated bottomland below. It feels more like the wine country of the Pacific Northwest than what most people picture when they think of Colorado.

From Hotchkiss the drive continues west to Delta, then south on US 550 into the Uncompahgre Valley. Here the landscape opens dramatically. The Uncompahgre Plateau rises to your west, a long elevated tableland whose cliff-edges catch the afternoon light and glow orange in the late hours. Ahead of you, gaining height with every mile, the San Juan peaks. They are darker than the mountains behind you. They have a different profile, more jagged and vertical, with cliff bands that seem almost too steep to be natural.

That darkness is partly the rock itself. The welded tuffs and volcanic breccias of the San Juan Volcanic Field do not reflect light the way limestone and sandstone do. They absorb it. The peaks look somber even in full sun.

The first roadcuts you encounter south of Montrose show the Cutler Formation, the brilliant red-orange sandstones and conglomerates that were deposited during the Permian Period roughly 280 million years ago. The red color comes from hematite, iron oxide, coating individual sand grains. These sediments eroded off the Ancestral Uncompahgre Uplift, a mountain range that stood where the plateau is now, taller than the modern Rockies. The Cutler is the calling card of that ancient landscape.

As you approach Ridgway on US 550, Ridgway Reservoir appears to your right, a wide blue sheet of water with the San Juan peaks rising behind it. On a calm morning the reflection in the reservoir is almost perfect, a photographic cliche that deserves every picture taken of it. This is the view that photographers position themselves for before dawn with tripods.

Beyond the reservoir, the road drops into the Ridgway valley, the Uncompahgre River threading through cottonwood bottoms, the town itself small and quiet in the foreground. Behind it, the mountain wall.

You have arrived at a very old and very particular place.
