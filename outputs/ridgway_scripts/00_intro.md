# Chapter 00: The Arrival Hook
*Estimated duration: 2.5 minutes | Word count: 345*

You are leaving a canyon carved by a river working for sixty million years. Behind you, Glenwood Canyon drops away, its limestone walls still warm in your mirrors, and the Colorado River bends back toward the Rockies. Yesterday you soaked in the sodium-rich waters of the Yampa and the Iron Mountain pools, watched travertine build itself shelf by shelf, and touched rock that was once the floor of a shallow tropical sea.

Today is different. Today you drive south into mountains that were not lifted up. They were blown out.

The San Juan Mountains ahead of you are not a product of the Laramide uplift or the patient pressure of continental collision. They are the eroded shell of what geologists call a supervolcanic field, the remnant of eruptions so enormous that individual events buried millions of square kilometers under glowing ash flows hundreds of feet deep. The Fish Canyon eruption, centered roughly where you are driving, is the largest known explosive volcanic event in North American geological history. Yellowstone, famous as a supervolcano, produced eruptions roughly half the size.

This changes how you see every mountain ahead. Those jagged peaks, those dark canyon walls, the way the rock layers look almost chaotic rather than neatly horizontal, all of it is the story of collapse and explosion and the slow work of rivers cutting back down through the aftermath.

And at the end of today's drive, tucked into the foothills just north of those mountains, is a hot spring that has been fed by this same volcanic heat for longer than humans have kept records. The Tabeguache band of the Ute people soaked in it for generations, calling this valley home. Chief Ouray, one of the most capable diplomatic minds in nineteenth-century American history, knew these waters. The town named after him is twelve miles south.

Today you drive through a transition. High desert opens around you. Sagebrush replaces the lush riparian canyon you left at Glenwood. The rock turns red, then gray, then the dark volcanic gray-brown that tells you the San Juans are taking over. Watch the roadcuts. The land itself is about to tell you everything.
