# Chapter 01: The San Juan Volcanic Field
*Estimated duration: 9.0 minutes | Word count: 1,240*

To understand the San Juan Mountains, you have to abandon the usual story of how mountains get made.

Most mountain ranges form the way the Rockies formed: continents collide, plates subduct, the crust buckles and thickens, and over tens of millions of years the rock gets pushed upward like a rug crumpling against a wall. The Laramide Orogeny, the event that built most of the modern Rockies, is that story. Glenwood Canyon and its magnificent stacked limestone is largely that story.

The San Juans are something else entirely.

What you are driving toward is a graveyard of volcanoes. Not individual peaks of the Kilauea variety, where lava flows steadily from a central vent. We are talking about something far more violent: a supervolcanic field, one of the largest concentrations of caldera-forming eruptions ever documented on this planet. The San Juan Volcanic Field was active from roughly 35 million years ago to about 25 million years ago, with the most catastrophic eruptions clustered between 28 and 30 million years ago. During that roughly five-million-year window, southwestern Colorado was one of the most geologically violent places on Earth.

Here is what a caldera eruption actually is, because the word caldera gets thrown around loosely. Deep in the crust, an enormous chamber of silica-rich magma accumulates over thousands or millions of years. Unlike basaltic magma, which tends to flow, silicic magma is viscous, full of dissolved gases, and under extraordinary pressure. When conditions change, when the magma chamber roof weakens or new material is injected below, the result is catastrophic decompression. The dissolved gases flash to vapor almost instantaneously. The magma becomes a suspension of superheated gas and molten rock fragments that erupts not from a single vent but from a ring of fractures encircling the entire magma chamber. The material, called pyroclastic density current or ignimbrite, does not rise into the atmosphere the way a classic volcanic eruption does. It flows, at hurricane speeds, outward from the eruption site, scouring the landscape, burying everything, and traveling sometimes over a hundred kilometers before coming to rest. When the eruption empties the magma chamber, the roof of rock above it, sometimes twenty to forty kilometers across, collapses inward. That depression is the caldera.

The San Juan Volcanic Field contains at least twenty-two confirmed caldera-forming eruptions. Twenty-two. Each one would have been a civilization-ending event by any human scale. But the one that stands above all others in the geological record is the La Garita eruption, which occurred approximately 28 million years ago.

The La Garita Caldera is in the southeastern San Juans, roughly in the area of present-day Creede, Colorado. The eruption produced what geologists call the Fish Canyon Tuff. The volume of material ejected: more than 5,000 cubic kilometers. To put that in perspective, the 1980 eruption of Mount St. Helens produced roughly one cubic kilometer. Yellowstone's most famous supereruption, the Huckleberry Ridge event of 2.1 million years ago, produced about 2,500 cubic kilometers. The Fish Canyon Tuff is roughly twice the volume of Yellowstone's largest eruption on record, and it is the largest known explosive volcanic event in Earth's geological record since the Cretaceous Period. The pyroclastic flows covered an area greater than 28,000 square kilometers, roughly the size of Maryland and Delaware combined, under deposits averaging 100 meters thick. In some places the tuff piled to 300 meters.

That material is still here. When you see those brownish-gray, almost compacted-looking cliff faces in the San Juan country, the rock that looks slightly glassy or welded, that is ignimbrite. The heat of the flow was so intense, often exceeding 800 degrees Celsius, that the individual ash particles fused together under their own weight and residual heat, creating what geologists call welded tuff. It is extraordinarily hard rock. It weathers into dramatic cliff faces. The canyon walls around Silverton, around Ouray, much of the dramatic vertical terrain in this entire region, is welded ignimbrite from these ancient eruptions.

Now here is the part that connects directly to why this part of Colorado is historically famous, and why the mountains south of you glitter with old mine shafts and slag heaps. The relationship between caldera volcanism and ore deposits is direct and profound.

During a large caldera eruption, the magma chamber does not empty completely. Hot, mineral-laden hydrothermal fluids remain in the rock, circulating through fractures and fault zones at temperatures of several hundred degrees. These fluids carry dissolved metals: gold, silver, copper, lead, zinc, tellurium, and a suite of trace elements. As the fluids move through cooler rock, they precipitate their mineral cargo in the pore spaces and fractures. Over millions of years, these concentrations build up. The result is an ore deposit.

The San Juan caldera complex created one of the richest mining districts in North American history. The veins of gold and silver that drew tens of thousands of prospectors into these mountains starting in the 1870s formed directly as a consequence of those violent eruptions 28 to 33 million years ago. The caldera system essentially pre-loaded the mountains with treasure. The miners stumbling up these canyons with their picks and mules were, without knowing it, harvesting the precipitation products of a supervolcanic event older than the Himalayan mountain range.

The Silverton Caldera complex, which is one of the major systems you will drive near if you venture south toward Silverton on another trip, is particularly instructive. The ore deposits of the Silverton, Ouray, and Telluride districts all sit within or immediately adjacent to the boundaries of old calderas or along the ring fractures that formed during caldera collapse. The fractures created by the roof collapse became the plumbing system through which hydrothermal fluids moved. The richer the ore zone, the closer it typically is to those ancient fractures.

One more piece of this picture deserves attention. The volcanic field is not cold. The geothermal gradient through the San Juan region remains elevated compared to the continental average, a lingering consequence of the heat that drove those eruptions. That elevated geothermal gradient is part of what warms the groundwater that feeds hot springs throughout the region, including the springs at Ouray and the ones you will soak in tonight at Orvis. The volcano may be 25 million years dead. Its heat, radiating upward through thousands of meters of rock, still reaches the surface.

When you look at the San Juans and see sharp, jagged, apparently chaotic peaks rather than the smooth domed profiles of the older Rockies, you are seeing the differential erosion of volcanic materials. The welded tuff stands in vertical faces. The less-resistant ash fall and volcanic breccia erodes away faster, creating the gullies and couloirs between. Rivers have cut deeply into this material. The entire mountain range is an erosional remnant of a collapsed volcanic field, slowly being dismantled by water and ice, piece by piece.

You are about to drive right up to the edge of it.
