# Chapter 09: Animals of the San Juan Foothills
*Estimated duration: 6.0 minutes | Word count: 820*

Late May in the Ridgway area is an exceptional time for wildlife. The winter has released its grip, the high country snowpack is still deep enough to keep large animals at lower elevations, and the breeding and birthing seasons are in full swing. If you are paying attention in the right places, there is a lot to see.

The bighorn sheep colony at Ouray is the most remarkable wildlife story in this immediate area. Bighorn sheep are native to the steep, rocky terrain of the mountain West, and the cliff walls around Ouray are ideal habitat: the right elevation, the right rock type for footing, the right exposure. A herd has established itself in the Ouray amphitheater canyon and has been present long enough that individual animals have largely habituated to the town below them.

The sheep use the canyon walls for protection, the way the species has always used cliffs: as an escape terrain, a place where their primary predators cannot follow. Mountain lions will pursue bighorns, but they cannot match them on truly vertical rock. Coyotes cannot follow them at all. By staying in terrain that no predator can negotiate, the sheep trade access to richer forage for security. In the Ouray canyon, they have apparently decided that the food and security balance works.

What makes them worth stopping for specifically is the lambs. In late May, the lambs born in April and early May are at that particular developmental stage where they are large enough to be mobile but young enough to still have that impossibly delicate quality. They follow their mothers across rock faces that seem to have no purchase for a hoof. The ewes navigate the canyon walls with complete confidence, and the lambs follow them, sometimes hesitating at a gap, then committing and leaping to the next ledge with a precision that looks entirely disproportionate to their age. Watch for lambs on the north-facing walls above the Uncompahgre River. Scan with binoculars and you may see entire family groups moving across terrain that looks impassable from below.

Mule deer are present throughout the Ridgway valley, especially in the transitional zone between the sagebrush flats and the cottonwood river bottoms. In late May, the does are fawning, and you are unlikely to see the fawns, which are hidden in dense cover for their first weeks of life. The mother does not stay with the fawn during the day; she visits to nurse and then leaves deliberately, reducing the trail of scent that leads predators to the hidden young. If you see a doe that seems alert but is not running, she may have a fawn concealed nearby. Give her space.

Prairie dogs are a feature of the higher, drier sagebrush flats north and west of Ridgway, and specifically in the Montrose area you may have driven through. Black-tailed prairie dogs form colonial towns, sometimes covering dozens of acres, and the sociality of these animals is genuinely fascinating. They have one of the most complex vocal communication systems documented in any North American rodent, including calls that apparently encode information about the size, shape, and color of approaching predators. Research by Con Slobodikoff at Northern Arizona University suggested that prairie dog alarm calls contain descriptors that function almost like adjectives, distinguishing a tall human from a short one, or a red shirt from a blue one.

Prairie dog colonies are ecological anchor points. The burrow systems they excavate create habitat for burrowing owls, which cannot dig their own holes and depend almost entirely on prairie dog infrastructure. Ferruginous hawks, the largest American hawk, hunt prairie dogs. Coyotes hunt them. Black-footed ferrets, one of the most endangered mammals in North America, depend on prairie dogs as their exclusive prey and their burrows as their dens. Healthy prairie dog colonies are an index of healthy grassland ecosystems.

Ridgway Reservoir, just north of town on CO-62, holds an osprey nesting pair that is typically present and actively fishing by May. Osprey are spectacular to watch: they hunt by plunging feet-first into water from heights of up to thirty meters, reaching speeds of fifty miles per hour, and they succeed on roughly a quarter of their dives. The feet have reversible outer toes and specialized spiny pad scales that help grip slippery fish. Watch for them hovering over the reservoir before the plunge.

Golden eagles soar over the Uncompahgre Plateau to the west of Ridgway, using the thermal lift along the plateau's eastern edge. They are often mistaken for hawks at distance, but the wingspan gives them away: golden eagles reach six to seven and a half feet across. They hunt primarily jackrabbits and prairie dogs, but will take marmots, young deer, and occasionally raptors. They mate for life and often use the same cliff nest site, called an eyrie, for decades, adding material each year until the nest can weigh hundreds of kilograms.

Black bears emerge from their winter dens in May, typically moving to lower elevations first to feed on early green vegetation, then following the snowmelt upward as the season advances. In late May around Ridgway, bears may be visible in the oak brush zones and along drainages. They are coming off winter torpor and are hungry, focused almost entirely on caloric replacement. They are not dangerous in the normal course of a roadside encounter, but they are large, and any bear that has learned to associate humans with food is a problem for both the bear and the humans. Keep your vehicle clean and your attention wide.

The Uncompahgre Plateau mule deer herd is one of the largest in Colorado, occupying the plateau through summer before migrating to lower elevations in autumn. In late May they are still in the valley and foothills.

Watch everything on this drive. The valley is full.
