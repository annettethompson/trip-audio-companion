# Chapter 05: The Science of the Soak
*Estimated duration: 7.5 minutes | Word count: 1,035*

Orvis Hot Springs sits just outside Ridgway at the foot of the San Juan foothills, and what makes it immediately different from the hot springs you experienced at Glenwood is not just the setting. It is the water chemistry.

The springs at Glenwood and Iron Mountain are driven by the Paradox Formation brines, deep sodium-bicarbonate waters that have percolated through evaporite deposits laid down when a Devonian sea evaporated 370 million years ago. That chemistry gives Glenwood's water its particular quality, slightly sulfurous, strongly mineralized with sodium and calcium.

Orvis produces something different. The water here is notably rich in lithium, an alkali metal that occurs in relatively few hot springs in the American West at concentrations significant enough to be remarked upon. Lithium in natural water is not the same as pharmaceutical lithium carbonate, and the concentrations in a hot spring are nowhere near clinical therapeutic doses. But the presence of lithium in groundwater has been the subject of genuine scientific interest. Population studies in Japan and in Texas found inverse correlations between lithium levels in drinking water and rates of suicide and certain mood disorders. The research is preliminary, the mechanisms are debated, and the lithium in a hot spring soak is absorbed transdermally at low and variable rates. What you can say with confidence is that the Orvis water profile is genuinely unusual, not just marketing language. The geochemistry of the San Juan volcanic terrain, with its long history of hydrothermal activity and mineral-rich groundwater, produces a water signature you are not going to find coming out of a limestone formation.

The springs emerge from the ground at approximately 127 degrees Fahrenheit at their hottest, at a source point on the highest part of the property they call simply the Crater. Gravity distributes this water through the facility. The water is never pumped, never heated, and never chemically treated. What comes out of the ground is what goes into the pools, diluted only by mixing between different temperature outlets. The result is a cascade of different temperatures across the property's ten soaking areas: from the main pond at around 100 to 106 degrees, to the Lobster Pot at 108 to 114 degrees for those who want an intense experience, to a cold plunge at 62 degrees that provides the cardiovascular contrast the body responds to so strongly, to the indoor pool at a gentler 98 degrees.

The pool design at Orvis is not pool design in any conventional sense. The ponds were shaped from the natural landscape, with stones and organic forms rather than concrete walls. The setting is open to the sky. In every direction, the San Juan peaks are visible, or the gentler profile of the Uncompahgre Plateau to the west. On a clear night, the soaking happens under stars without light pollution, because Ridgway is small and the horizon is wide.

The clothing-optional nature of the springs often becomes the lead detail in casual conversation about Orvis, which is a bit like noting that a Beethoven concert allows you to sit down. It is true, it is relevant to whether you go, but it is not the thing. The cultural context is worth a moment, though. European mineral springs, from Iceland's Blue Lagoon to the thermal baths of Hungary and Germany, have operated clothing-optional or even clothing-required-off for centuries, and visitors from those cultures find the American discomfort with the practice somewhat puzzling. Japanese onsen culture is explicitly clothing-free; the practice is deeply embedded in the culture as relaxation, social bonding, and a kind of physical honesty. The absence of swimsuits is not incidental to these traditions. The removal of social signifiers, of the garments that indicate status and fashion, is part of the experience. You are just people in water.

Orvis has operated as a clothing-optional facility since its modern iteration opened, and the visitor reviews consistently note that the atmosphere is less charged than newcomers expect. Families soak in the warm pond. Couples sit in the cooler north pool. People read books. The mountain views are the main event.

Now, since you spent yesterday learning the physiology of hot spring soaking in general terms at Glenwood, what can you add here at Orvis specifically?

The multi-pool format invites something that a single large pool does not: deliberate thermal contrast cycling. Moving from a hot pool to a cold plunge and back, repeatedly, produces a different physiological response than simply sitting in warm water. The cold plunge triggers immediate vasoconstriction, a flood of norepinephrine, and a sharp increase in metabolic rate. When you return to the hot pool, the vasodilation rebounds even more powerfully than it would have from heat alone. The contrast cycling is what Japanese spa practitioners call noboriburo, ascending bath, and it is also closely related to the Scandinavian sauna-to-ice protocol. Research on this specific pattern is more robust than the research on simple heat immersion, with documented improvements in post-exercise recovery, reduction in delayed-onset muscle soreness, and measurable shifts in autonomic nervous system tone toward the parasympathetic side.

In plain terms: alternating hot and cold pools, done three to five cycles, produces a deeper relaxation than sitting in one warm pool for an hour. The body thinks it has gone somewhere and done something. It reacts accordingly.

The Tabeguache Ute soaked in the spring that predated this facility. The exact location of the original spring they used may or may not be on the Orvis property; hot spring systems often have multiple outlets across an area. But the hydrothermal system that heats this water is the same one that has been active in this landscape for millions of years, a consequence of the elevated geothermal gradient left by the San Juan volcanic episode and enhanced by the deep fault systems that allow groundwater to reach heated rock and return to the surface.

The spring itself is ancient. The awareness of what it does to the human body is equally ancient. Tonight is a very old activity, in a very old place.
