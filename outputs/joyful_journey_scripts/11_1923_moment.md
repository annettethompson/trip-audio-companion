Taylor Sheridan's 1923, the show you and Bob have been watching, is set in Montana's Paradise Valley, where the Dutton family is fighting to hold a cattle ranch through drought, disease, and the encroachments of a changing West. In 1923, the world that family represents, the world of the open range and the horse-powered ranch, is already ending.

The San Luis Valley in 1923 was a compressed version of that same transition, playing out at 7,600 feet elevation between two mountain ranges, but in many ways more complicated than the Dutton family's version because the people who lived here had already been through several rounds of dispossession and adaptation before the early automobile age arrived to change things again.

By 1923, the valley's agricultural economy had been through three distinct phases in the previous 70 years. The first was the original Hispano acequia phase, the hand-dug ditches and vara-strip farms around San Luis and the Culebra Creek settlements, which you heard about in the oldest-town chapter, running since 1851. This system was still in operation in 1923, still governed by its traditional mayordomo structure, still irrigating the same family farms that had been laid out by the original settlers. Four and five generations into it, the families along Culebra Creek were farming ground that their great-grandparents had turned with wooden plows. The acequia did not change because it worked.

The second phase was the artesian well boom. Starting in the late 1880s, drilling technology made it possible to tap the confined aquifer beneath the valley floor, the one that had been described in the geology chapter this morning, and the wells flowed without pumping because of the artesian pressure. By 1900, thousands of artesian wells had been drilled across the valley. Fields that had been too far from a creek to be irrigated by an acequia could now be farmed. The valley's agricultural footprint expanded dramatically. Potato production, which Colorado's high-altitude, well-drained soils are particularly suited for, became the valley's signature crop. By the 1920s, Colorado potatoes were the state's most valuable agricultural crop, and a significant share of them came from the San Luis Valley.

The third phase was the Anglo homesteader wave. The Homestead Act and its successor legislation, combined with the railroad's arrival in the valley in the 1870s and 1880s, brought waves of Anglo-American settlers attempting to dry-farm the flatter, more exposed parts of the valley. Dry farming, agriculture without irrigation relying solely on rainfall, is marginal at best in a climate that averages six to eight inches of precipitation per year and frosts in every month. Most of the Anglo homesteader ventures in the San Luis Valley from the 1890s through the 1920s failed. The land they had tried to cultivate reverted to scrub. The physical evidence of those failures was everywhere in 1923: abandoned homestead cabins, overgrown claim boundaries, fields gone back to saltbush and rabbitbrush.

In the same decade that the homesteaders were abandoning their claims, a new agricultural community was arriving. Japanese-American families, many of them coming west from farming communities in California and Colorado's Front Range, established themselves in the San Luis Valley in the 1920s and introduced intensive truck farming: vegetables grown for sale in town markets and railroad-car shipment, requiring the kind of daily labor and technical skill that the short growing season and high-altitude conditions demanded. The Japanese-American community in the valley would later be one of the communities forced into internment camps during World War II, a loss to the valley's agricultural knowledge and community fabric that was not fully recovered.

In 1923, the horse was still the engine of valley farming. The transition to gasoline tractors that was already underway in the Great Plains had barely started here. The potato fields, the grain fields, the hay meadows: all worked by horse-drawn equipment, by the large draft horses that the valley's farming families bred and traded, that looked recognizable to anyone who had been watching the 1923 Dutton family manage their own ranch across the mountain ranges to the north. A team of Percherons pulling a potato harvester down a San Luis Valley field in October 1923 would have been utterly familiar to Jacob Dutton.

Fort Garland, which you heard about this morning, was by 1923 an abandoned adobe ruin at the foot of La Veta Pass, forty years since its Army garrison had left. The Ute people whose presence in the valley it had been partly built to manage were forty years gone, removed to their reservation in southwestern Colorado in the 1879-1881 period covered in earlier episodes. The valley by 1923 had settled into its post-removal demographic pattern: Hispano families on the old acequia farms in the south, Anglo ranchers and potato farmers in the middle and north, small towns at the railroad junctions of Alamosa, Monte Vista, and Del Norte. The land that had been Ute territory in the memories of the oldest living valley residents was now divided into private holdings, water rights, and irrigation company shares.

What would have struck anyone arriving in the San Luis Valley in 1923, from anywhere else, was the size of it and the silence of it. No interstate highway. No developed ski industry drawing traffic through. Just flat, high-altitude agricultural land between two mountain ranges, the wind off the Sangres carrying the cold of elevation even in August, the night sky uninterrupted by light pollution for 50 miles in every direction. The Dutton family, if they had turned south from Montana and driven the ruts down through Alamosa, would have recognized it immediately: a valley where the land was larger than the people on it, and where holding on required a specific combination of knowledge, stubbornness, and water.

The water is still here. The acequia still flows. The Sangres are in exactly the same position they were in 1923. Some of the families are too.
