From the south end of the San Luis Valley on a clear day, a mountain massif rises at the southeastern corner of the Sangre de Cristo range that seems out of proportion to everything around it. It is broader at the base, higher at the summit, and more complex in its ridgelines than its neighbors, and in the right morning light it carries a quality that is hard to name: something between authority and presence.

This is Blanca Peak, at 14,345 feet the highest summit in the Sangre de Cristo Mountains and the fourth highest peak in Colorado. Geologically, it is the core of a Precambrian massif, the same ancient basement rock, nearly 1.7 billion years old, that forms the deep foundation of the Rocky Mountains across the region. While the Laramide Orogeny, the mountain-building event you heard about in the Ridgway episodes, uplifted the surrounding sedimentary layers, the rock of Blanca Peak is far older: it is the basement that the sedimentary layers sat on top of before the mountains existed. The Rio Grande Rift fault system, which you heard about in this morning's chapter, has lifted the entire Sangre de Cristo block while the valley beside it dropped, exposing these Precambrian rocks at elevations where most of North America's ancient basement is buried miles below the surface.

But the geological story of Blanca Peak is, in this case, not the most important thing about it.

The Navajo people, Diné, whose traditional homeland spans the Four Corners region of the American Southwest, organize their world geographically around four sacred mountains, one at each cardinal direction, that define the boundaries of Dinétah, the traditional homeland. These mountains are not metaphors. They are specific physical places, identified by name and story, that establish where the Navajo world begins and ends and what it means to be within it.

The eastern boundary mountain is Tsisnaasjini', which translates approximately as Dawn or White Shell Mountain. It is Blanca Peak.

In Diné Bahané, the Navajo creation narrative, the Holy People placed the Diné on land between four sacred mountains. Tsisnaasjini' was dressed with white shell and fastened to the earth with a bolt of lightning. Because it stands in the east, the direction of the rising sun, it carries particular significance for the daily orientation of Navajo life: traditionally, hogans, the Navajo home, face east toward Tsisnaasjini', so that the first light of day enters the door aligned with the mountain's sacred direction. The mountain determines the orientation of a person's mind and intentions at the start of each day.

The other three sacred mountains of the Navajo complete the cardinal framework. Tsoodzil, or Mount Taylor near Grants, New Mexico, is the southern mountain, associated with turquoise and summer rains. Dook'o'oosliid, the San Francisco Peaks near Flagstaff, Arizona, is the western mountain, associated with abalone shell and autumn. Dibé Nitsaa, Hesperus Mountain in the La Plata Mountains near Durango, is the northern mountain, associated with obsidian and winter. Together, these four mountains define a territory of roughly 150,000 square miles in which the Navajo homeland is contained: southeastern Utah, northeastern Arizona, northwestern New Mexico, and southwestern Colorado.

Blanca Peak has never been within the political boundaries of any reservation. The original Navajo reservation established in 1868, in the treaty that ended the Long Walk imprisonment you heard about in the Fort Garland chapter, was drawn well south and west of Blanca's position. The mountain sits today in Costilla County, Colorado, on a combination of private ranching land and national forest. The Navajo Nation has maintained a formal interest in access to Tsisnaasjini' for ceremonial purposes, and the relationship between the Navajo people and this mountain has continued, through federal land ownership changes and the complexities of cross-state jurisdiction, because a sacred mountain does not become less sacred when a political boundary is drawn past it.

The Ute people also held the Sangre de Cristo range as significant territory, the source of game, hunting land, seasonal movement routes, and the high-country that their ancestors had worked for centuries before any settler arrived in the valley. The specific ceremonial significance of individual Sangre de Cristo peaks in Ute tradition is less fully documented in the written record than the Navajo four-mountains framework, in part because Ute oral and ceremonial knowledge was not systematically recorded by outside researchers in the 19th century in the same way some other traditions were. What is clear is that the Mouache and Capote Ute, the valley's prior inhabitants, regarded the eastern mountain front as homeland in the deepest sense of that word, not just territory but the landscape of origin.

The Sangre de Cristo range is still geologically active. The fault system at its western base, the same fault that the rift mechanics produced, has displaced Precambrian basement rock by nearly 20,000 feet relative to the valley floor, and the fault continues to move. Microseismic surveys in the region record small earthquakes along the Sangre de Cristo fault system regularly, and paleoseismic work on fault trench excavations shows surface-rupturing earthquakes in the last 10,000 years. The mountains are still rising, imperceptibly by human timescales, driven by the same extension that opened the valley. The geology and the sacred significance are, in a sense, the same observation made in different languages: this is a place of power, where the forces that shaped the world are still working, still visible, still present in a way that most landscapes do not offer.

Look east. The mountain is there. It has been there for ten million years in roughly its current form, and will be there long after every structure in this valley has dissolved back into sediment and been buried under whatever the rift deposits next.
