The San Luis Valley and Great Sand Dunes are worth a dedicated trip. What you are doing today, the drive-through soaking visit, barely scratches what is here. Here is what to plan for.

**Great Sand Dunes National Park**

The park is about 30 miles east of Moffat on CO-150, roughly 40 minutes from Joyful Journey. If you have any time this afternoon or want to build a return trip around it, here is what matters.

The main hike is up High Dune, 699 feet of elevation gain on sand, which is significantly harder than the numbers suggest. Sand gives underfoot. Your calves will burn. Plan two to four hours round-trip depending on pace, and go early in the day. By afternoon in summer, the surface of the dunes can reach 140 degrees Fahrenheit and burn bare feet through shoes. Boots are better than sandals, and starting at 7 or 8 in the morning before the surface heats is strongly recommended.

Star Dune, the tallest at 750 feet, is deeper into the dunefield, requiring a longer approach across the dune floor. The reward is solitude: most visitors never make it past High Dune. Plan a full day for Star Dune and bring more water than you think you need.

Medano Creek, the seasonal creek at the base of the dunes, runs in spring and early summer, typically April through early July. When it is flowing, families wade in it, the surge flow phenomenon makes it pulse in waves, and it is one of the more unexpectedly fun experiences in any national park. The creek disappears by midsummer as snowmelt diminishes. Check NPS.gov before planning a Medano Creek visit.

The Mosca Pass Trail climbs from the visitor center up through the Sangre de Cristo range to Mosca Pass at 9,737 feet, a 3.5-mile one-way hike with 1,550 feet of elevation gain. It passes through the transition from the dune environment into the mountain forest, and the views back across the dunefield and valley from the upper switchbacks are genuinely exceptional. Moderate difficulty. Good wildflower hiking in June and July.

Zapata Falls, about 9 miles south of the main park entrance on CO-150, is a short hike to a waterfall in a slot canyon, the last 50 yards in a creek bed requiring wading through cold water and ducking under a rock overhang. Not deep, not dangerous for healthy adults, but wet shoes are certain. The falls themselves are 30 feet high inside a narrow granite canyon. Under-visited relative to the main park, and worth it.

**Come back in March for the cranes.** The Monte Vista Crane Festival runs on the first weekend of March annually. The festival is small, the crowds are manageable, and the crane numbers are extraordinary, as covered in the wildlife chapter earlier today. Book ahead; local lodging in Monte Vista and Alamosa fills for that weekend. Show up at the Refuge at sunrise and find a field margin where a few thousand birds are lifting off in the morning light, and you will understand why people build trips around this.

**Joyful Journey return tips.** Weekday visits are significantly quieter than weekends, especially summer. The facility has yurts and glamping accommodations if you want to make it a two-night stay with evening and morning soaks when the pools are nearly empty and the Sangre de Cristo dawn light is doing what it does. Clothing-optional hours are designated; check their website for the current schedule.

**Restaurants**

Alamosa is the valley's dining center. True Grits Steakhouse is a local institution, old-school western steakhouse in downtown Alamosa, a reliable choice for something substantial after a day of hot spring soaking. Calvillo's Mexican Restaurant has been feeding the Alamosa community for decades, good regional New Mexico-influenced cooking. Milagros Coffee House in downtown Alamosa is the local café and gathering spot.

In Monte Vista, the San Luis Valley Brewing Company has been the area's craft beer anchor for years, good food alongside Colorado-brewed beers in a solid downtown space.

For a full San Luis Valley experience: drive south to San Luis, the oldest town in Colorado you heard about earlier. The Covered Wagon Restaurant is a local institution. The town's restaurants are modest and authentic, the kind of cooking that has been feeding the valley's Hispano farming families for generations rather than performing for tourists.

Salida, about 50 miles north of Moffat, is a more fully developed food and arts town if you want a broader selection. But the San Luis Valley has its own flavor, and Alamosa and San Luis give it to you more honestly.
