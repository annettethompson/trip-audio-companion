Fort Garland sits in the southeastern San Luis Valley, at the foot of La Veta Pass, where the valley floor meets the mountain front. It was established in 1858 to replace Fort Massachusetts, an earlier and badly sited post about six miles north that had been plagued by cold, flooding, and poor visibility of the approaches from the mountains. Fort Garland was better positioned, built of adobe in the traditional Southwestern style, and it served as the central military presence in the San Luis Valley for 25 years.

The Army's mission at Fort Garland was a complicated one, characteristic of the period. It was there to protect Hispano settlers from Ute raiding parties. It was there to protect Ute bands from settler encroachment and violence. It was there to enforce a set of treaty arrangements that kept changing as the United States decided it wanted more of the valley, then more of the mountains, then more of everything. The soldiers at Fort Garland were simultaneously peacekeepers, enforcers, and scouts in a territory where the legal ground shifted under everyone's feet every few years.

The Mouache and Capote Ute were the bands whose territory included the San Luis Valley and the eastern slope of the Sangre de Cristo range. Their seasonal movements through the valley had been part of the landscape for generations before the Hispano settlers arrived in 1851. The early decades of settlement were not a simple story of conflict: there were periods of trading, economic interdependence, and local cooperation alongside the raids, counter-raids, and diplomatic crises that dominate the written record. The Hispano community and the Ute communities had a relationship that was sometimes violent and sometimes something more like neighbors managing a difficult coexistence on the same land.

Fort Garland's most famous commander arrived in May 1866.

Kit Carson is one of those historical figures who becomes larger and more uncomfortable the more carefully you look at him. By 1866 he was 56 years old, a former trapper who had been in the mountains since the 1820s, a scout who had guided Fremont's western expeditions, a rancher, an Indian agent, a Civil War officer, and, most controversially, the man who commanded the forced removal of the Navajo people in 1863 and 1864, an event known as the Long Walk, in which somewhere between 8,000 and 10,000 Navajo people were marched hundreds of miles to Bosque Redondo in eastern New Mexico, where they were held in terrible conditions for four years. Roughly 2,000 Navajo people died during the Long Walk and the Bosque Redondo imprisonment.

Captain Albert Pfeiffer, whose knife duel at Pagosa Springs you heard about in yesterday's episode, was Carson's most trusted officer and participated in the same Long Walk campaign.

Carson arrived at Fort Garland already ill with what would prove to be a terminal aortic aneurysm, and he commanded for only about 18 months before leaving in October 1867. But in those 18 months he accomplished the diplomatic work that defined the fort's lasting significance. In September 1866, he persuaded Chief Ouray, whose role in Ute history you heard about in the Ridgway episode, to bring his band to Fort Garland to negotiate.

The negotiations that followed were the foundation for the 1868 Ute Treaty, which established a formal reservation in western Colorado, a large one by the standards of the era: roughly the western third of what would become the state of Colorado. The treaty guaranteed Ute access to hunting lands, annuity payments, and the government's recognition of Ute sovereignty over a defined territory.

Carson died less than a year after leaving Fort Garland, in May 1868, at Fort Lyon, Colorado. He was 58. He never saw the 1868 treaty finalized, though his negotiations had made it possible. He also never saw how quickly it unraveled, as the silver and gold discoveries in the San Juan Mountains in the 1870s made the United States government decide that the Ute's guaranteed territory was inconveniently located over valuable ore deposits. The Ute removal from most of Colorado followed in 1879 and 1881, the full story of which was covered in the Durango episode.

Fort Garland continued operating until 1883 under various commands. The Buffalo Soldiers, the all-Black 9th and 10th Cavalry regiments established after the Civil War, served rotations at Fort Garland, as they did at many western posts. The 9th Cavalry spent time at the fort in the early 1870s, part of the Army's deployment of Black troops in the West during the same era when they were being systematically excluded from most civilian opportunities in the post-war South.

The fort was abandoned in 1883 as the Ute removal made the original peacekeeping mission obsolete. The adobe buildings were partially maintained and partially left to decay for decades. In 1945, the State of Colorado acquired the site, and Fort Garland is now a Colorado state museum, one of the better-preserved examples of a mid-19th century western Army post in the region.

The Kit Carson question is one that historians and Indigenous communities continue to navigate: was he a man of his time, acting within the moral framework available to him, or was the Long Walk an atrocity that deserves to mark him regardless of the context? Both answers are too simple. What is clear is that Carson was, by the end of his life, genuinely committed to the Ute people's survival as a distinct community, that his negotiations at Fort Garland were conducted in good faith as he understood it, and that the protections he helped secure were dismantled by his own government within a decade of his death.

The museum at Fort Garland is small and serious. Worth an hour if you have it.
