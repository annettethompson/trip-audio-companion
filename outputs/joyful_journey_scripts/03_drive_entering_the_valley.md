You left Pagosa Springs heading east on US-160, and for the first part of this drive you are still in the forest. The Wolf Creek Pass corridor, which you heard about yesterday, is behind you. The road through South Fork follows the Rio Grande upstream through a narrowing canyon, cottonwoods and willows crowding the riverbanks, pine and spruce on the slopes above.

Then the canyon opens.

There is no subtle transition. The walls just stop. The forest thins out, the road levels, and suddenly the horizon is so far away it does not look real. This is the moment the San Luis Valley introduces itself, and it does it by making everything you have seen for the last several days of mountain driving feel small.

Resist the urge to explain what you are seeing while it is happening. Just look. Fifty miles of valley floor, perfectly flat, stretching east until it ends against a mountain wall that goes straight up without foothills. That eastern wall is the Sangre de Cristo range, and you are going to learn a great deal about it today. For now, just notice how abrupt the transition is: valley floor, then mountains, with almost nothing in between. No gradual slope, no piedmont foothills. Rock and flat, with a hard edge between them.

The town of South Fork is behind you now. Keep heading east toward Monte Vista.

This stretch of US-160 across the western portion of the valley floor takes you through some of the most productive agricultural land in Colorado at the highest elevation in the world where that statement makes sense. You are at roughly 7,600 feet above sea level, and the fields around you grow potatoes. The San Luis Valley produces the majority of Colorado's potato crop, and a significant portion of the nation's, from soil that sits over one of the most extraordinary underground water systems in the American West. The flat you see is also the aquifer, and the aquifer is part of the geology that you heard about in the opening chapter today.

Watch the road shoulder and the field margins as you drive. The vegetation is not grass. Not the blue grama and fescue of a normal Colorado meadow. What you are seeing on the drier, saltier margins is a different community of plants entirely, low-growing and silver-gray, adapted to alkaline soil and eight inches of rainfall per year. That is the ecology chapter's territory, and we will get there.

Monte Vista. The Monte Vista National Wildlife Refuge is just south of town. In early March, this area hosts up to 27,000 sandhill cranes staging for their migration north to breeding grounds in the Yellowstone ecosystem. By late May when you are driving through, most of the major crane flocks have passed through. But during spring migration, this quiet agricultural valley becomes one of the premier crane spectacles in North America. That story is coming in the wildlife chapter.

East of Monte Vista, US-160 runs into Alamosa, the valley's largest town, at the junction of the Rio Grande and the valley's main road network. Turn left. You are heading north on CO-17, which runs parallel to the Sangre de Cristo mountain front on your right.

This is the drive. CO-17 north, mountains to the east, valley floor to the west. For the next 30 miles or so, you will have an uninterrupted view of the Sangre de Cristo range rising on your right. These peaks top out above 14,000 feet. The range runs nearly 220 miles from Salida, Colorado, down into New Mexico, making it the longest subrange in the Rocky Mountains. The name means Blood of Christ in Spanish, a reference attributed to the red light the peaks catch at sunrise and sunset, though the origin story of the name is debated.

Scan the base of the mountains as you drive north. At some point in the next 30 miles, you will see something that makes no sense: sand. Not a sandy wash, not a gravel bar, but actual tan-colored dunes pressed against the base of the mountains, visible as a lighter-colored wedge rising toward the rock. That is the Great Sand Dunes. You are seeing them from the highway before you arrive. The chapter on how they got there runs after this one.

Moffat is a small community about 50 miles north of Alamosa. You are there before you realize the town started. Joyful Journey is a short drive off CO-17 toward the mountains. The parking area sits at the foot of the Sangres, the pools visible on the hillside.

Find your suit, your towel, and some water. This afternoon, you are getting in.
