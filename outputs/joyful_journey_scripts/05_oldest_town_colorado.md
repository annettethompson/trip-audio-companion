San Luis was founded in April 1851. Colorado became a state in 1876. The town is 25 years older than the state it sits in, and it has never stopped.

That sentence requires a moment. The people who established San Luis were not Americans. They were Hispano settlers from Taos, New Mexico, moving north into the San Luis Valley on the authority of the Sangre de Cristo Land Grant, a tract of land that had been granted by the Mexican government in 1843 and whose legitimacy they believed carried over when the United States acquired the territory after the Mexican-American War ended in 1848. They were wrong about the law, as it turned out. But in April 1851, none of that had been adjudicated. They had a grant. They had a valley. They had a creek.

They built on Culebra Creek, a stream that runs off the western face of the Sangre de Cristo Mountains, and they built in the pattern that generations of Spanish colonial settlement had established in the Southwest: homes arranged around a central plaza, the church defining the eastern edge, fields laid out in long narrow strips running perpendicular to the creek. These strips were measured in varas, a Spanish colonial unit, and they were designed to give each family access to both the creek water and the road, with strips narrow enough that neighbors were within calling distance across the width of their fields. It was a social geometry as much as an agricultural one.

Within a year of founding San Luis, the settlers dug the San Luis People's Ditch. This is the first recorded water right in the state of Colorado, pre-dating statehood by nearly 25 years, and it is still in operation today. The ditch was hand-dug using the technology available in 1852: wooden shovels, leather buckets, human labor. It diverts water from Culebra Creek into a network of smaller channels that distribute it across the farm strips on both sides of the creek. The San Luis People's Ditch is 170-plus years old and still irrigates the same fields it was built for.

The larger system it belongs to is the acequia. An acequia is a community irrigation ditch, an institution that came to the Southwest from Moorish Spain via colonial Mexico over a span of centuries. The word is Arabic in origin, from the Spanish transliteration of the Moorish term for the channel, and the water management philosophy it embodies is genuinely different from the individual property rights approach that Anglo-American water law developed in the West. Under acequia governance, water is a shared community resource. The ditch is maintained collectively. Water is distributed according to need and tradition, with a parciante, a ditch member, receiving allocation proportional to the size of their fields and the current flow available. In drought years, everyone gets less. In good years, the allocation expands. The acequia is governed by a mayordomo, a watermaster, elected annually by the parciantes, with authority to manage the seasonal distribution and resolve disputes.

The Hispano settlers brought acequia governance north from New Mexico, and in the San Luis Valley it took root in a way that it did not in most of the rest of Colorado, where Anglo-American prior appropriation water law came to dominate. The San Luis People's Ditch and the network of Culebra-area acequias that were established in the 1850s and 1860s operated under traditional acequia governance for their entire history, and in the 1980s and 1990s, when the Sangre de Cristo Land Grant question came back before the courts in a complicated legal fight over timber and grazing rights on the mountains above the valley, the acequia community was a central part of the argument about what the land grant meant and who still held rights under it.

The land grant story is long and not entirely resolved. The Sangre de Cristo grant was confirmed by Congress in 1860, but the specific question of whether the original grant included traditional grazing rights on the mountain common lands above the deeded private parcels went through Colorado courts for decades. The heirs of the original Hispano settlers argued that the grant included the right to continue grazing cattle and gathering firewood on the mountain slopes, as their families had done for a century. The corporate owners of the mountain lands argued it did not. In 2002, the Colorado Supreme Court ruled in favor of the heirs on the question of grazing rights, a decision that acknowledged a 150-year-old community relationship with the land that Anglo property law had tried to extinguish.

San Luis today has a population of about 600 people, most of them descendants of the original Hispano settlers, in a village that looks recognizably like the plaza town that was established in 1851. The San Luis Museum and Cultural Center documents this history, and the Stations of the Cross Shrine above the town, a series of life-sized bronze figures set in the hillside above the valley, is a landmark that has become as associated with the town as the water ditch. The church in the central plaza has been in continuous operation since 1862.

The Capote and Mouache Ute, the bands who lived in and traveled through this part of the San Luis Valley before and during the settlement period, are the earlier chapter's territory. What matters here is that the Hispano settlers arrived in a valley that was not empty, that the relationships between settler families and Ute bands in the early settlement period were complex and locally variable, and that the Ute removal from the northern valley in the 1870s and 1880s came decades after San Luis was already an established community.

By 1923, the year of the show you and Bob have been watching, San Luis looked roughly as it does today: a small plaza town at the end of a network of irrigated farm strips, the People's Ditch running through it as it had since 1852, the same families who had been here for three or four generations still farming the same vara strips their great-grandparents had laid out by hand. The railroad had reached Alamosa in 1878 and the San Luis Valley branches extended further by the 1880s. The early 20th century brought new Anglo homesteaders to the valley attempting dry farming on the flats with mixed results, and Japanese-American families arrived in the 1920s introducing truck farming. But San Luis stayed San Luis: the oldest continuously occupied town in Colorado, held together by ditch water and community governance and a faith that what was planted was worth tending.
