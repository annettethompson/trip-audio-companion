There is a moment, somewhere on US-160 east of Pagosa Springs, when the mountains stop and the world opens up in a way that Colorado does not usually allow.

You have been driving through forests and canyons for days. Elevation and compression. Rock walls close enough to touch. Then the San Juan Mountains pull back, and in front of you is a flat, high-altitude basin so large it has its own weather system, its own sky, its own atmosphere. Fifty miles wide, a hundred and twenty miles long. You can see mountains on both sides at once, and they are so far away they look like a painted backdrop.

This is the San Luis Valley. And today, it is yours.

The valley sits at 7,664 feet above sea level, and it has no right to be here. Geologically speaking, this gap in the Rocky Mountains is an accident of violence, a place where the earth pulled apart along fault lines that still move, where 30,000 feet of sediment filled a hole so deep that the ground beneath you is one of the most complex underground aquifer systems in the American West. The mechanism that made it is called the Rio Grande Rift, and it is a story Bob is going to love.

At the far eastern edge of the valley, held against the Sangre de Cristo Mountains by opposing winds, is something even more improbable: the tallest sand dunes in North America. Not in Arizona. Not in Utah. Here, at 8,200 feet, at the foot of peaks that exceed 14,000 feet, sand piled so high it takes four hours to climb. We will tell you exactly how it got there.

The hot spring you are heading toward today sits right in the middle of all of this, in a town called Moffat that most Coloradans have never heard of. Joyful Journey Hot Springs is artesian water rising through the same fault system that built this valley, carrying minerals that have not seen sunlight since they sank into the earth long before any human walked this ground.

And from the pools at Joyful Journey, if the afternoon clouds hold off, you will look east and see Blanca Peak: 14,345 feet, and one of the four sacred mountains of the Navajo people. You will hear about why.

Along the way, there are cranes that have been stopping in this valley for longer than any written history of it. There is the oldest town in Colorado, and an acequia irrigation system still flowing after 170 years. There is a man who brought the United Nations to a cattle ranch, and an idea he had about what the land could become.

The valley is waiting. Let's go in.
