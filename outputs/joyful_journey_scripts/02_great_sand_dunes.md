If you had to design the worst possible place in Colorado to grow sand dunes, you would probably pick the foot of a 14,000-foot mountain range, in an alpine valley at 7,600 feet elevation, in the middle of a rift basin that receives less than eight inches of rain a year. You would pick, in other words, almost exactly where the Great Sand Dunes are.

The dunes do not make intuitive sense. They are too big for where they are, too desert-feeling for a landscape surrounded by snow-covered peaks, too concentrated in one specific spot when the valley floor in every other direction is flat scrubland and potato fields. And yet there they are: 30 square miles of sand piled 750 feet high, the tallest dunes on the continent, pressed against the Sangre de Cristo Mountains like something the valley floor is trying to push through the rock.

To understand how they got there, you need to think about the valley's history over the last half million years.

The San Luis Valley, as you heard in the geology chapter, is a rift graben: a dropped section of crust that has been collecting sediment for tens of millions of years. About 440,000 years ago, something specific happened here. A combination of snowmelt, river drainage, and the valley's enclosed basin geometry created a large lake geologists call Lake Alamosa. It was not a small seasonal pond. Lake Alamosa covered much of the southern San Luis Valley and persisted for perhaps hundreds of thousands of years, accumulating thick layers of fine-grained lake sediments on its floor.

Then, around 440,000 years ago, the lake broke through its southern rim and drained. Where a deep lake had been, there was now a vast flat plain of dry lake-bottom sediment exposed to the open sky and the relentless winds of the valley.

The sand on that lake floor came from two mountain ranges. The San Juan Mountains to the west contributed sediment that the ancient ancestors of today's Rio Grande and its tributaries had eroded and carried eastward into the basin over millions of years. The Sangre de Cristo Mountains to the east contributed material eroded from their own western faces. The lake had concentrated and sorted this sediment. When it dried, the valley floor was covered with sand and silt waiting to be moved.

The primary wind in the San Luis Valley blows from the southwest. Standing on the valley floor, wind comes off the San Juan Mountains, crosses the flat basin, and runs into the abrupt wall of the Sangre de Cristo range on the eastern edge. There are three low passes through the Sangres along the valley's eastern boundary: Music Pass, Medano Pass, and Mosca Pass. The wind funnels toward these passes, and where it hits the mountain front and loses energy, it drops its sand load.

For hundreds of thousands of years, that southwest wind has been pushing sand toward the Sangre de Cristo front, and the sand has been accumulating at the exact spots where the wind slows down. The result is a dunefield pinned against the mountains, trapped between the prevailing wind from one direction and the rock wall of the Sangres from the other.

But why don't the dunes keep moving? Why haven't they climbed through the passes and scattered across the eastern slope? The answer is two creeks, and they are one of the more elegant recycling systems in North American geology.

Medano Creek flows down from Medano Pass seasonally, crossing the eastern face of the dunefield before disappearing into the valley floor. Sand Creek does the same on the southern edge. Both creeks cross at the base of the dunes, capturing sand blown off the front edge by the southwest wind, and carrying it back toward the valley floor in their flow. When the creeks run dry and sink into the sand and gravel of the valley, the sand they were carrying settles out and is available to be picked up by the next southwest wind and pushed back toward the mountains.

Sand goes up into the dunes from the valley, is pushed toward and through the passes by prevailing winds during major storm events, is captured by the creeks on the eastern flanks, is carried back down to the valley floor, and is then blown back up again. The dunefield is in dynamic equilibrium, recycling the same material over and over, growing taller because the wind from the opposite direction, coming through the mountain passes during storms, pushes back just hard enough to prevent the dunes from migrating east.

The physics of this system created the specific geometry you will see: the highest, most massive dunes concentrated right at the point where opposing winds cancel each other out. Star Dune, the tallest individual dune in the system, rises 750 feet from its base to its summit. High Dune, the one most hikers target because it is closest to the parking area, stands 699 feet. For scale: the Empire State Building is 1,454 feet to the roof. You are looking at a pile of sand roughly half as tall as the most recognizable skyscraper in America, and it is made entirely from eroded mountain rock and dried lake sediment, moved here grain by grain on the wind.

The sand is also not static in texture. Walk to the creek edge on the right day in spring or early summer, when Medano Creek is running, and you will see a phenomenon called surge flow: pulses of water moving down the creek in waves, the result of sand dams forming and releasing upstream. Children play in it. The water rises to ankle depth, then falls, then rises again, in rhythmic pulses that look like something a child programmed and are actually the natural hydraulics of a creek heavily loaded with sand. No tides. No pumps. Just sand and water working out their physics.

The dunes exist here, and are as tall as they are, because three things happened to coincide: the rift basin created a flat, enclosed catchment area for sediment; the mountain range on the eastern edge provided a physical barrier for wind; and two creeks developed exactly where they needed to be to keep the sand from escaping. Remove any one of those three elements and you have an interesting wind deposit at most. With all three, you have something that makes first-time visitors stop their cars and just stand there for a while, trying to figure out where they are.
