There is a particular quality that the best hot springs in the West share, something that the resort brochures try to describe and mostly fail: the sense that the place is not performing for you, that it would be here whether you came or not, and that you are the visitor in a relationship it did not ask for and does not depend on. Joyful Journey Hot Springs has that quality.

The facility is modest in scale, which in this case means honest. Three main outdoor soaking pools at different temperatures step up a gentle hillside at the foot of the Sangre de Cristo Mountains. A cooler pool at 98 degrees, a mid-temperature pool at 104 degrees, and the view pool at 108 degrees, the hottest. All three are mineral water, artesian, fed from springs that rise through the fault structures of the Rio Grande Rift, the same geological system you heard about in the opening chapter today. From the upper pools, the Sangre de Cristo peaks fill the eastern horizon, and the valley floor spreads west for miles, the San Juan Mountains marking the far wall on clear days.

The spring produces no sulfur smell. That is the first thing visitors notice, especially after the Springs Resort in Pagosa yesterday, which announces itself with the distinctive hydrogen sulfide odor of a sulfate-dominated water chemistry. Joyful Journey's water chemistry reads differently: sodium bicarbonate as the base type, which you encountered at Steamboat Springs on Day 1, but with a mineral profile that is distinct from anything you have soaked in this week.

The water at Joyful Journey is described by the facility as carrying boron, calcium, lithium, magnesium, and a range of other trace minerals. Let us talk about two of those specifically, because they have not come up in earlier episodes.

Boron is a trace element found in geothermal waters derived from deep crustal sources, and the San Luis Valley's high geothermal gradient means deep fluid circulation. Boron in hot spring water is primarily absorbed through skin, and research on boron and human biology shows measurable effects on bone density, hormonal regulation, and inflammatory markers at consistent dosing levels. You are not taking a boron supplement in the pools. The concentrations are low. But boron is a biologically active trace mineral that appears in geothermal waters precisely because it comes from rock types associated with deep crustal heat, and the San Luis Valley's rift geology delivers it in quantities that distinguish this water from simpler surface-heated springs.

Lithium has been in the news in recent years as a potential micronutrient with effects on mood regulation and cognitive function. Hot spring bathing and lithium absorption through skin is a subject of ongoing research, and the concentrations involved are a fraction of pharmaceutical lithium doses. But natural lithium in spring water is associated with the same deep geological processes as boron: old, deep water that has been in contact with granite and pegmatite at high temperatures. Strawberry Park in Steamboat, where you soaked on Day 2, was also lithium-rich. Joyful Journey's water sits in a similar category of deep-sourced geothermal chemistry.

The artesian nature of the spring is worth a moment. Artesian water rises under its own pressure without pumping because it comes from a confined aquifer, one sealed above by an impermeable clay layer, with the recharge zone at a higher elevation than the outlet. Water entering the aquifer from snowmelt on the Sangre de Cristo peaks, at elevations of 10,000 to 14,000 feet, flows downward through the aquifer under gravity. Where the land surface is lower than the recharge zone and the clay seal has been broken by a fault, that pressure pushes the water upward. The Joyful Journey spring rises at the fault zone on the eastern edge of the valley because the structural geology of the Rio Grande Rift created exactly the break in the clay layer that artesian pressure needs to escape.

The springs emerge at 120 to 140 degrees Fahrenheit and are cooled in the distribution system before entering the pools. Unlike pools fed by recirculated and chemically treated city water, artesian hot spring pools are continuously refreshed by new spring water. The overflow drains away, taking with it whatever minerals have been deposited and whatever the bathers contributed. The water in the pool is not static; it is a flowing system, and the mineral content you are soaking in is essentially unchanged from what came out of the ground.

The clothing-optional designation applies to specific areas. The facility is clothing-required in the main pools unless your visit falls on a designated clothing-optional session. The general atmosphere is one of quiet adult enjoyment rather than resort activity, which is appropriate for a location that is, at its core, an artesian spring on an alkaline flat at the foot of one of the most geologically dramatic mountain fronts in North America.

The Sangre de Cristo view from the pools is not incidental. On a clear afternoon in late May, the peaks will still carry significant snowpack. Blanca Peak, at 14,345 feet the highest point in the range, is visible from the facility on clear days approximately 40 miles south. The visual combination of spring-hot pool water and snowy peaks is one of those juxtapositions that Colorado specializes in and that photographs never quite get right. You are at 7,664 feet. The mountains behind you rise nearly 7,000 feet above that in roughly 10 horizontal miles.

The vasodilation and cardiovascular effects of hot spring soaking were covered in the Pagosa episode's physiology chapter. At the altitude and spring chemistry you are dealing with today, those effects apply here. What is different at Joyful Journey is the boron and lithium chemistry and the artesian water quality, both of which make this a genuinely distinct soaking experience from anything earlier in the trip.

Stay as long as your body wants to. The valley is not going anywhere.
