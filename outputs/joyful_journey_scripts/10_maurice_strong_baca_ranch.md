In 1978, a Canadian oil and mineral businessman named Maurice Strong acquired a 200,000-acre ranch in the San Luis Valley of Colorado, about 30 miles northeast of where you are sitting at Joyful Journey Hot Springs, at the foot of the Sangre de Cristo Mountains in Saguache County. Strong was not a rancher. He had no particular experience with Colorado. What he had was money, a vision, and an almost inexhaustible network of global connections.

Maurice Frederick Strong, born in Manitoba in 1929, had built his career in two parallel tracks that seemed, to many observers, to contradict each other. Track one was oil and minerals. He began in the Alberta oil patch in the late 1940s, made enough money by his early 30s to acquire significant interests in Canadian energy and financial companies, and eventually became a major figure in Canadian corporate finance, connected to the Power Corporation of Canada, Petro-Canada, and a range of international resource ventures. By the 1970s he was a wealthy man by any measure.

Track two was the United Nations and international environmentalism. In 1972, Strong organized and served as Secretary General of the United Nations Conference on the Human Environment in Stockholm, the first major international gathering that put environmental issues on the global political agenda. He then became the first executive director of the United Nations Environment Programme. Twenty years later, in 1992, he organized the Earth Summit in Rio de Janeiro, the largest international environmental conference in history to that point, which produced the Rio Declaration, the Convention on Biological Diversity, and the framework that eventually became the Kyoto Protocol on climate change. Strong was, by reasonable assessment, the single most influential person in establishing international environmental governance as a field.

He also owned a cattle ranch in Colorado, and he had a plan for it.

The land that Strong acquired, a combination of ranch properties that totaled roughly 200,000 acres at various points, was built around the core of the Luis Maria Baca Land Grant No. 4. This land grant has a history as tangled as any piece of land in the American West. The original Baca grant was made by the Mexican government to Luis Maria Baca in 1821, confirmed in 1826, and validated by the United States Congress in 1860 as compensation to the Baca family heirs after a surveying conflict invalidated their original New Mexico land claim. The Baca family's attorney at the time chose to claim five separate parcels of equivalent acreage in unoccupied public lands, rather than one contiguous piece; this particular parcel, Grant No. 4, landed in Saguache County, Colorado, at approximately 99,000 acres, pressed against the western base of the Sangre de Cristo range.

The land passed through several private owners over the following century, eventually to the Newhall Land and Farming Company and then to the Arizona-Colorado Land and Cattle Company. Strong assembled the core of the grant and surrounding properties into what he called the Baca Ranch.

His plan for the land was, by the standards of the 1970s, unusual. Strong and his wife Hanne were interested in what they described as a model of sustainable land use that combined traditional agriculture, spiritual practice, and ecological stewardship. On a strip of land along the base of the Sangres near the town of Crestone, Strong's nonprofit Manitou Foundation began offering parcels of land, at nominal cost, to established spiritual and contemplative organizations from different traditions: a Tibetan Buddhist community, a Carmelite monastery, a Zen center, a Hindu ashram, a Jewish renewal group, and a variety of other intentional communities. By the time Strong sold the ranch in 1995, Crestone had become one of the most concentrated collections of interfaith spiritual communities in North America, a development that continues today. The town of Crestone, about 40 miles northeast of Moffat, is to this day a notable destination for contemplative practice, with perhaps a dozen active communities operating in the foothills of the Sangres.

The Baca Ranch story also includes one of the more significant water rights controversies in Colorado history. In 1986, Strong founded American Water Development Incorporated and filed an application to pump water from the San Luis Valley's deep artesian aquifer, the same confined underground water system described in this morning's geology chapter, and sell it to front-range urban water districts. The application proposed pumping at rates that the Colorado Department of Natural Resources, the National Park Service, local water conservation districts, and acequia water rights holders, the community-managed irrigation systems you heard about in the San Luis chapter, argued would permanently damage the valley's water table and harm agricultural users who had established rights going back to the 1850s. After years of legal proceedings, the Colorado Water Court denied the application in 1994, a ruling that has been cited as a landmark in western water law.

Strong sold the ranch in 1995. The subsequent legal proceedings, the Nature Conservancy acquisition, and the eventual transfer to the federal government resulted in the Baca National Wildlife Refuge, a 78,000-acre refuge established in 2000 and now managed by the U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service adjacent to Great Sand Dunes National Park.

Maurice Strong died in Beijing in 2015 at age 86. His legacy is genuinely complex: the man who arguably did more than anyone else to put international environmental governance on the world stage owned a piece of the American West that he tried to profit from by selling its water. He also gave away land for interfaith communities, and the land he once owned is now one of the larger wildlife refuges in Colorado.

The view from Joyful Journey toward the Sangres includes, in the northern part of the range, the general vicinity of the Baca refuge and the Crestone communities. The mountain front looks the same as it did when the Baca family's heirs first claimed it in 1860. What has happened on and under that ground since is the kind of story that sounds made up and is entirely documented.
