The crane is the oldest bird in the fossil record. Not a crane in the way we see them today, exactly, but a Gruidae, the crane family, recognizable in fossil beds dating back 47 to 54 million years. The sandhill crane that stages at Monte Vista every spring in numbers that turn the sky gray and the fields into moving carpets of gray feathers is, in a meaningful biological sense, one of the oldest continuous success stories in vertebrate evolution. While most of what was alive in the Eocene is unrecognizable to us now, the crane has been doing exactly this, more or less, for longer than the Rocky Mountains have existed in their current form.

That fact may not impress you while you are stuck in March traffic on CO-17 because 20,000 cranes are crossing the road, which has happened. But it is worth holding onto.

The Greater Sandhill Crane, Antigone canadensis tabida, is the subspecies that stages in the San Luis Valley. Subspecies designation in birds can get taxonomically complicated, but the Rocky Mountain population of Greater Sandhill Cranes is distinct enough in its breeding geography and migration route that it is tracked and managed separately. This population winters near Bosque del Apache National Wildlife Refuge in central New Mexico, along the Rio Grande. In late February and March, as days lengthen and temperatures rise, the cranes begin moving north. The San Luis Valley is their primary staging area for the Rocky Mountain leg of this migration, and the Monte Vista National Wildlife Refuge is at the center of it.

Here are the numbers: during peak migration, typically the first week of March, the Monte Vista refuge and surrounding private agricultural land host roughly 95 percent of the entire Rocky Mountain population of Greater Sandhill Cranes. That translates to between 18,000 and 21,000 birds. Additionally, 5,000 to 6,000 Lesser Sandhill Cranes pass through, a smaller subspecies with a more northerly breeding range. Total crane numbers in the valley at peak can reach 27,000.

The refuge manages this migration through active habitat work. The San Luis Valley in late winter and early spring offers little natural crane food: most wetlands are frozen, grain is not yet in the fields, and the frost-cracked ground gives up few insects or invertebrates. The refuge floods wetland cells using stored water, and managers mow grain fields to expose the leftover grain from the previous year's harvest. Without this management, the staging area would support a fraction of the birds. The cranes are entirely dependent on the combination of natural wetland habitat and intentional agricultural management to use the San Luis Valley successfully.

The staging behavior itself is worth describing. Cranes are not just passing through. They are rebuilding fuel reserves for the next leg of their migration north to breeding grounds primarily in the Yellowstone Ecosystem, Idaho, and Montana. A crane arriving at Monte Vista after the New Mexico winter may be carrying minimal fat reserves. It needs weeks of intensive feeding on grain, insects, waste crops, and invertebrates before it has the energy to continue north to breed. The staging period can last from late February well into April, and the birds are eating almost constantly when not roosting.

Roosting happens in the wetland cells, in standing water where predators cannot approach silently. Thousands of cranes standing in a few inches of water overnight, their calls producing a sound that has been described, not inaccurately, as something between a French horn and a dinosaur. The unison call, a coordinated duet that bonded pairs perform, involves the male giving two calls for every one the female gives, the timing so precise and practiced that paired birds can hold it together even in the chaos of a flock of thousands. Crane pairs mate for life, or near enough to it that the distinction may not matter practically. They begin breeding at somewhere between three and five years of age, and Greater Sandhill Cranes regularly live into their 20s and 30s in the wild.

The crane is a large bird by any reasonable measure. Standing 4 feet tall with a wingspan that can reach 7 feet, weighing 8 to 12 pounds, with a bright red patch of bare skin on the crown that intensifies during breeding display. The gray plumage is actually partly stained: cranes frequently preen with iron-rich mud, painting their feathers a rusty brown during the warmer months, then molting into cleaner gray feather in fall. In March at Monte Vista, you are seeing them in transition.

The dancing behavior that cranes are famous for, a coordinated sequence of jumps, wing spreads, bows, and calls that serves both pair bonding and flock communication functions, is on full display at Monte Vista during the staging period. It is not exclusive to courting birds. Established pairs dance. Juveniles dance with each other in apparent play. Researchers believe the behavior also functions as a social sorting mechanism within the flock, reinforcing hierarchies and pair bonds under the compressed social conditions of a large staging aggregation.

By late May, when you are driving through, the peak staging is long past. Most of the Rocky Mountain cranes are already on their northern breeding grounds. But a smaller resident population of Greater Sandhill Cranes nests in the San Luis Valley itself, in the wetland margins and wet meadows along the valley's rivers and ditches. If the season is right and the timing is good, you may see small family groups or individual birds in the fields along CO-17. A crane standing alone in a potato field at 7,600 feet elevation, 150 miles from the nearest ocean, visible against the Sangre de Cristo backdrop, is one of those images of North American landscape that does not need any interpretation.

Watch for them. They are easy to miss if you are looking at the mountains. They are impossible to miss once you see one.
