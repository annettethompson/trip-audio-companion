Your body is going to be doing several things simultaneously while you are in these pools, and most of them are measurable.

The most immediately noticeable is heat immersion. Immersing in water above body temperature triggers a cascade of thermoregulatory responses. Blood vessels near the skin surface dilate to release excess heat, which is why hot spring soakers turn visibly pink. Heart rate increases slightly. Cardiac output rises as the heart moves more blood to the skin periphery. This vasodilation response is one of the physiological mechanisms that gives hot spring soaking its effect on blood pressure: the peripheral dilation temporarily drops resistance in the circulatory system, giving the heart an easier workload and often producing a measurable drop in blood pressure readings for 30 to 60 minutes after a soak.

The sulfur chemistry at Pagosa adds a dimension that the bicarbonate springs earlier in this trip do not have. The key compound is hydrogen sulfide, the molecule responsible for the smell. Dissolved hydrogen sulfide in hot spring water at the concentrations found at Pagosa crosses the skin barrier into the bloodstream in measurable quantities. This is not speculation: skin absorption of dissolved gases and small molecules from hot water is well documented in the physiological literature. Once absorbed, hydrogen sulfide acts as a vasodilator, relaxing smooth muscle in blood vessel walls through a mechanism involving nitric oxide signaling. The effect parallels what nitrates in leafy vegetables do via a different biochemical pathway: blood vessels open up. More blood gets through. The sensation is relaxation, warmth, and a kind of muscular loosening that is genuinely different from what a heated bathtub produces.

Research published in the early 2000s established hydrogen sulfide as an endogenous gasotransmitter in humans, meaning the human body produces small amounts of it naturally as part of normal cell signaling. The concentrations in sulfur spring water are higher than the body produces on its own, but they engage receptors and pathways that already exist. You are not introducing a foreign compound. You are engaging a native system at elevated intensity.

The mineral content beyond hydrogen sulfide has additional documented effects. Magnesium, present in significant concentrations in Pagosa spring water, is another vasodilator and also acts on muscle relaxation at the cellular level. Calcium has structural effects on skin barrier function. Silica in high concentrations in spring water has been associated in multiple peer-reviewed studies with improved skin elasticity and hydration. The sulfate ions that define Pagosa's water chemistry are the same sulfate chemistry that makes Epsom salt soaking effective, though Epsom salt is specifically magnesium sulfate and Pagosa's water delivers a broader mineral profile simultaneously.

The specific effect of soaking at altitude adds a layer. You are at Pagosa at an elevation of 7,079 feet. At altitude, the body adapts by carrying more oxygen per unit of blood volume, a process you heard about in detail in the Ouray physiology chapter. The vasodilation from heat and sulfur immersion at altitude produces a more pronounced cardiovascular response than it would at sea level, because the cardiovascular system is already running at a slightly elevated baseline effort. This is why experienced altitude hot spring visitors recommend shorter initial soaks: twenty minutes in a 102-degree pool at 7,000 feet produces roughly the equivalent cardiovascular effect as thirty-five to forty minutes would at sea level. Hydration before soaking matters more at altitude than at lower elevations, where baseline fluid loss through respiration is already elevated.

The Springs Resort has staged its pools at temperatures ranging from roughly 95 degrees Fahrenheit in the river pools at the lower end to around 113 degrees in the upper pools. This gradient is not an accident of plumbing. It exists so that visitors can self-regulate their exposure, moving to cooler water when the cardiovascular response becomes too intense, then returning to warmer pools as they acclimatize. The standard recommendation from the resort staff is to start at a lower pool, ascend gradually toward hotter water, and always have a cooler pool accessible for recovery.

The source pool at the top of the resort, which sits at approximately 153 degrees, is off-limits for swimming. It is accessible to observe at close range and is worth doing: the volume of steam rising from it, the visible mineral deposits around the edges, the way the surrounding concrete is stained and textured by decades of mineral deposition, are all evidence of what the earth is doing a thousand feet below where you are standing. The pool looks like something that belongs in a different category of natural phenomenon than a swimming pool. It is a geological feature that has been dressed up with a railing and a viewing area.

Two other things worth knowing before you get in: the sulfur smell fades after about five minutes of immersion because the olfactory system habituates. The smell returns when you get out. And your skin will feel different after soaking: slightly slippery, slightly smoother. That is the sulfate and silica content working on your skin barrier. The effect is noticeable for several hours after you dry off.
