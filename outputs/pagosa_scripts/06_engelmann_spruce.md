Above 9,500 feet in the San Juan Mountains, the ponderosa pines whose story you heard in the Durango episode stop, and a different forest begins. The trees are shorter. The spacing is tighter. The canopy is darker. The crowns point upward like spires rather than rounding like umbrellas. You are entering the subalpine zone, and its signature species are the Engelmann spruce and the subalpine fir.

Engelmann spruce, named for the Missouri botanist George Engelmann who first described it to science in 1863, grows from New Mexico north into Canada and east to the Rocky Mountain front range, always at high elevation, always in cool, moist, relatively sheltered sites. It is a slow-growing tree: a trunk 18 inches in diameter may represent 150 to 200 years of growth. It is shade-tolerant, meaning it can establish and grow slowly under the canopy of other trees before working its way upward, rather than requiring full sunlight to survive its seedling years. This shade tolerance makes Engelmann spruce the dominant climax species of the subalpine forest: given enough time and undisturbed conditions, spruce will eventually outcompete most other species for canopy position.

The bark is thin, which makes Engelmann spruce highly vulnerable to fire. A crown fire at the right wind speed and temperature will kill an Engelmann spruce forest outright, with no regeneration from surviving root systems the way you see in aspen or Gambel oak. Recovery after fire in the subalpine zone requires reseeding from intact forest edges and is measured in centuries, not decades. This vulnerability is why land managers treat fire behavior in subalpine forests fundamentally differently from fire behavior in ponderosa pine forests, where low-intensity historical ground fires maintained a parklike open structure.

The companion species, subalpine fir, is formally Abies lasiocarpa, and it grows alongside Engelmann spruce throughout the subalpine zone. You can distinguish them by touch: spruce needles are sharp enough to prick your fingers if you grab a branch firmly; fir needles are soft and flat. The bark of a young subalpine fir is smooth and gray with resin blisters. Spruce bark is scaly and brownish-gray. In the forest around Wolf Creek Pass, you will see both species growing so closely intermingled that experienced foresters use the abbreviation SE/AF for what they call spruce-fir forest: the two species so consistently co-occurring that naming only one is ecologically incomplete.

The Wolf Creek area has a character that distinguishes it from most subalpine forests in Colorado: it is extraordinarily wet. Wolf Creek Pass receives an average of 400 to 450 inches of snow per year in a typical season, driven by Pacific moisture systems that stall against the Continental Divide at this specific geographic notch. Some years the total exceeds 600 inches. Individual winters on record at the pass have exceeded 800 inches. There are ski areas in the Alps that do not see those totals. The Wolf Creek Ski Area, which sits just west of the summit, operates on a snow depth that resort operations elsewhere in Colorado monitor with open envy.

What extreme snowfall means for the spruce-fir forest is a growing season compressed almost beyond belief. At the summit zone, snow does not fully melt until June and begins accumulating again in September. The trees have roughly ten to twelve weeks in which to conduct the photosynthesis, root growth, and seed production that must sustain them for the remaining forty weeks. Engelmann spruce responds by growing almost nothing in bad years: a cold, wet summer with a late snowmelt can produce annual growth rings so thin they require magnification to count. A core pulled from a mature Engelmann spruce at Wolf Creek Pass would show decades of years where essentially nothing happened above ground, recorded only as a line barely visible under a hand lens.

At the upper limits of the subalpine zone, usually around 11,500 feet in the San Juans, the trees begin to lose their upright form. They shrink, bend, grow horizontally away from the prevailing wind, cluster together for thermal mass in the same way animals huddle. This is the krummholz, from the German for crooked wood, and it is not a different species. It is Engelmann spruce and subalpine fir expressing what it costs to exist in a place where winter storms last ten months and the soil freezes solid five feet deep. Krummholz trees that are waist-high at the edge of a trail may be a hundred years old. The one that looks like a shrub may have started from a seed when the Civil War was being fought.

Above the krummholz, there are no trees. What comes next is tundra, and what comes after that is rock.
