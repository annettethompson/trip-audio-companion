# Chapter 00: The Smell Arrives First
*Estimated duration: 2.8 minutes | Word count: 406*

Something is going to hit you before Pagosa Springs hits the visual. If the windows are down at all, or even if they are not, somewhere around mile seven on US-160 heading west from Wolf Creek Pass, the air inside the car is going to change. Not dramatically. Not like something went wrong. Just a faint mineral edge, a sulfur note so light it is more suggestion than smell, and for a moment you will wonder if you imagined it.

You did not. You are smelling the earth.

The Great Pagosa Hot Spring sits in the middle of Pagosa Springs, Colorado, and it is arguably the deepest known geothermal hot spring on the planet. The water that flows through it has traveled from somewhere well below 1,000 feet down, possibly much deeper, through rock that reaches temperatures that would cook anything organic. It emerges at the surface carrying dissolved hydrogen sulfide, 23 distinct minerals, and a temperature at the source of approximately 153 degrees Fahrenheit. You cannot get in the source pool. You would not survive it.

But the resort has engineered a cascade of pools down to the San Juan River's edge, each one cooled to a soakable temperature, each one fed from the same water. Twenty-five pools. River pools at 95 degrees. Rooftop pools with views of the San Juan Mountains. And behind all of it, the source spring: a small, steaming, frankly alarming body of water that has been producing this flow continuously since long before any human being had words for what it was.

The Ute had words. Their word was pagosa. It meant healing waters or stinking waters, depending on which scholar you ask, and both translations are probably accurate. The Ute fought for these springs. So did the Jicarilla Apache. The contest over who owned the pagosa is one of the more dramatic stories in Colorado history, and it involves a Civil War veteran, a knife duel, and the kind of single combat that sounds like legend but is verifiably documented.

Today you will hear the geology that produces one of Earth's most extraordinary geothermal systems. You will cross Wolf Creek Pass, one of the most storied mountain crossings in Colorado. You will learn what sulfur-rich water does to the human body that bicarbonate water does not. And you will meet an animal that most people associate with Canada or Alaska but has been quietly thriving in the forests around this valley for forty years.

Pagosa is waiting. You can already smell it.
