If you are returning to Pagosa Springs for a dedicated visit, here is what is worth planning around.

**Trails**

The Piedra River Canyon is the most rewarding hiking destination accessible from Pagosa without committing to a backpacking trip. The Lower Piedra Trail follows the river through a sandstone canyon, with walls that show the same sedimentary layering of the southern San Juan geology. The trail stays along the river bottom for five to six miles before the terrain opens into wider forest. The trailhead is off County Road 600 about 25 miles northwest of town on the way to Chimney Rock. Moderate difficulty, minimal elevation gain, bring water filtration.

The East Fork Trail out of South Fork, about 30 miles northeast of Pagosa, climbs into the Weminuche Wilderness and reaches the Continental Divide in roughly 12 miles. This is a full-day or overnight route, starting at 8,400 feet and gaining significant elevation through spruce-fir forest before breaking into the alpine meadows above treeline. The trailhead is accessible in a standard vehicle with adequate clearance.

For a short hike with immediate scenery, the Hot Springs Boulevard Riverwalk follows the San Juan River through town and passes the source spring, which is observable from the path at close enough range to understand why the pools are staggered down from it at cooler temperatures. Two miles round trip, flat, entirely paved. Worth doing in the evening when the steam off the source pool is most visible in cool air.

The Continental Divide National Scenic Trail has a trailhead at Wolf Creek Pass summit. A day hike north along the divide takes you above treeline within two miles and puts you on the watershed roof with views east into the San Luis Valley and west across the Weminuche. You start at 10,857 feet and climb from there. This is not for people who are still acclimatizing to altitude.

**Extreme Sports and Adventure**

The San Juan River through Pagosa runs Class II and III whitewater during peak snowmelt runoff, which runs from late May through early July most years. Local outfitters run half-day guided trips on the upper river; it is appropriate for beginners with a guide in medium flows and demands prior experience in high water. Pagosa Outside and Pagosa Rafting Outfitters both operate on this section.

Fly fishing on the San Juan River above Pagosa and on the Piedra River is serious fishing. The lower San Juan below Navajo Dam in New Mexico is a world-class tailwater fishery, but the upper San Juan near Pagosa is a wild river with native cutthroat in productive holding water. Late May through early June means high, fast water: plan for early mornings before peak daily flow, or fish the smaller tributaries where flows are more manageable. Late summer, when the river drops and clears, is technically the easier season, but the late-May fishing has its advocates.

Mountain biking in the Pagosa area has grown substantially in recent years. The Turkey Springs trail system, accessible from town, offers roughly 40 miles of purpose-built singletrack ranging from beginner to technically demanding intermediate riding. The system sits in the ponderosa-spruce transition zone, which means views and forest variety rather than the sustained alpine exposure of higher elevation systems.

Wolf Creek Ski Area, six miles northwest of town, opens around Thanksgiving and typically operates into April. It receives more natural snow than any resort in Colorado most years, with the avalanche data to prove it, and charges lift ticket prices that reflect its position off the major resort corridor. Weekday skiing there in a high-snow January or February is among the better values in the state.

**Restaurants**

Pagosa Brewing and Grill is the local institution: a brewpub occupying a building on the river with a kitchen that takes itself seriously. The beer list includes seasonals, and the food is substantially better than brewery food needs to be. The green chile pork is worth ordering if it is on the current menu. Price point is reasonable for a mountain town.

Alley House Grille is the upscale option in town: a small dining room with a seasonal menu and a wine list better than you expect from a community of 2,000. Reservations matter from June through August.

For breakfast before a trail day, Higher Grounds Coffee and Bakery opens early, makes good pastry, and understands what altitude does to baking. The location is walkable from the springs if you are staying at the resort.

The Springs Resort's own restaurant, Waterfall Restaurant, has improved its kitchen in recent years. Eating on the rooftop deck in the evening while steam rises from the source pool below is an experience specific to this one place on earth. The setting would carry mediocre food. The food is better than mediocre.
