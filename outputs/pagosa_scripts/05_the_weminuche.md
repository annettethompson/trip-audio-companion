Colorado has 41 designated wilderness areas. The Weminuche is the largest of them, and it is not close.

At 499,771 acres, the Weminuche Wilderness covers just under 800 square miles of the San Juan Mountains in the southern part of the state. It encompasses portions of the Gunnison and San Juan National Forests, stretches from the Continental Divide westward to the Needle Mountains, and includes the headwaters of several major river systems including the San Juan, the Piedra, and the Los Pinos. In practical terms, it is a roadless alpine landscape the size of a small state, with no motorized access inside its boundaries and essentially no human infrastructure beyond trails and a small number of permitted outfitter camps.

The Weminuche Wilderness was designated by Congress in 1975 and expanded in 1980 and 1993. Its name comes from the Weminuche band of the Southern Ute people, whose ancestral territory covered much of this high country before the removals of the 1870s and 1880s that you heard about in the Durango episode. It is a rare case where an American wilderness area carries the name of the Indigenous people who inhabited it, and the communities of the southern San Juans have generally treated this as meaningful rather than merely nominal.

The wilderness sits at high elevation throughout. The majority of the interior is above 10,000 feet, with peaks exceeding 14,000 feet in the Needle Mountains section. The Needle Mountains, the most remote and rugged part of the wilderness, contain some of the most technically challenging terrain in Colorado: Sunlight Peak, Windom Peak, and Eolus Peak are among the state's more demanding fourteeners, requiring either a long approach on foot or access via the Durango and Silverton Narrow Gauge Railroad, which makes request stops at Needleton and Elk Park for wilderness users, as you heard in the Durango episode.

The Continental Divide Trail, which runs 3,100 miles from the Mexican border to Canada, passes through the Weminuche for approximately 100 miles. This stretch is among the most exposed and demanding on the entire trail. No roads cross it. Resupply options are minimal. Weather above treeline in June and July produces afternoon thunderstorms that arrive faster than inexperienced hikers expect. The CDT through the Weminuche is serious backcountry, and the people who traverse it tend to know that before they start.

From Pagosa Springs, the most accessible entry points into the Weminuche are the Wolf Creek and South Fork corridors to the north, and the Piedra River canyon to the west. The Piedra River area is notably wilder in character than the trails closer to the pass: lower visitation, denser forest, and one of the few undammed river corridors in the entire San Juan drainage. The Piedra is designated a Wild and Scenic River, which gives it statutory protection against impoundment and commercial development, and the canyon it has cut through southern San Juan sandstones is one of the less-visited rewards accessible from Pagosa.

The wilderness ethic that governs the Weminuche is the same ethic that governs all wilderness areas under the 1964 Wilderness Act: no mechanized transport, no permanent structures, Leave No Trace. In the Weminuche specifically, with its high visitation at certain entry points, this extends to fire restrictions during dry periods, rules about camping within 300 feet of lakes and streams, and a practical reality that the area receives more than 100,000 visitor-days per year by some estimates. The combination of high visitation and genuine wildness means that the more popular routes, like the Vallecito and Pine River trails, require the kind of discipline that the more remote Piedra corridors do not demand of the few people who find their way there.

What the Weminuche preserves, more than any specific trail or summit, is consequence. Not the difficulty of the terrain, though that is real and should be respected. The consequence of the sky. When you are above treeline in the Weminuche and a storm builds over the divide, there is nothing between you and it. The mountains are not threatening. They are simply indifferent to human preference, and that indifference, in an era when most natural places have been made manageable for visitors, is one of the more valuable things the wilderness designation has managed to keep intact.

The designation is 50 years old in 2025. The wilderness that Aldo Leopold argued for in the 1920s and Howard Zahniser wrote into law in 1964 has been protected here long enough that a tree that was a seedling when the bill passed is now a mature spruce with roots that have grown through four decades of Weminuche winters. That is not a long time in geological terms. In conservation terms, it is almost everything.
