There are places where history is merely documented and places where it happened with enough intensity that the ground still seems to hold it. The hot springs at Pagosa are the second kind.

The Ute people had known the springs for generations before any other group contested them. The word pagosa itself comes from the Ute language: a rough translation falls somewhere between stinking water and healing water, and the same source gives us both because the two meanings were not contradictory to the people who used them. The water stank. It also healed. Both things were true and both were aspects of the same power.

The Ute treated the springs as a sacred site with practical purposes simultaneously. Soaking in hot mineral water relieves muscular pain and joint inflammation, effects that were understood experientially long before any physiology characterized them scientifically. The sulfur content in particular has documented effects on skin conditions and bacterial infections that traditional users in many cultures observed over centuries. The Ute came to the pagosa not as a recreation destination but as a place of healing and ceremony that happened to be controlled, like most significant resources in the West, by whoever was strong enough to hold it.

The Jicarilla Apache, a southwestern Apache group whose territory covered much of northern New Mexico and southern Colorado, also claimed rights to the springs. The Jicarilla and the Ute were frequently in conflict over territory and resources in this region, and the pagosa was among the most contested points of that boundary. There is documented oral history from both groups of conflicts at the spring site, and the archaeology of the region shows evidence of long-term multiple-group use with periods of violent interruption.

The single most dramatic confrontation over the springs that entered the written historical record involves a man named Albert H. Pfeiffer.

Pfeiffer was a German immigrant who arrived in New Mexico in the 1840s and spent the next two decades becoming one of the most complex and dangerous figures in the frontier Southwest. He served as an Indian agent for the Ute in the 1850s, fought in the Civil War as a New Mexico Volunteer officer, and was the closest personal friend of Kit Carson, who described Pfeiffer as the most fearless man he had ever encountered. Carson's threshold for fearlessness was high. He had spent forty years in the mountains.

In 1863 and 1864, Pfeiffer participated alongside Carson in the Long Walk: the forced removal of the Navajo people from their ancestral lands to Bosque Redondo in eastern New Mexico. The scale and brutality of that removal are now recognized as one of the defining atrocities of the American Southwest. Pfeiffer's role was substantial, and it is not a flattering chapter of his biography.

But before and after the Long Walk, Pfeiffer maintained a relationship with the Ute that was, by the standards of the time, genuinely unusual. He had learned the Ute language as a younger man and spoke it fluently. His wife, a Mexican woman named Antonita, was killed by an Apache raiding party in 1863, an event that intensified both his alignment with the Ute and his deep antagonism toward the Apache groups of the region.

The incident at Pagosa Springs took place in 1866. The specific details come from Pfeiffer's own account and from Ute oral history that was recorded by later ethnographers, and the accounts align closely enough to be credible. A Jicarilla Apache party challenged the Ute claim to the springs through the mechanism of single combat: one representative from each group to fight until one side yielded the ground. The Ute, who needed a fighter willing to go up against a selected Apache warrior, asked Pfeiffer.

Pfeiffer accepted. The duel was conducted with Bowie knives. Pfeiffer, who was by this point in his fifties and had fought enough men in enough circumstances to know exactly what he was doing, wounded his Apache opponent within a short engagement. The Apache party withdrew. The springs remained in Ute territory for another fifteen years, until the removal of the Weeminuche and other Ute bands in the early 1880s that you heard about in the Durango episode cleared them from this ground entirely.

An American Army camp was briefly established at the spring site in 1878, partly to manage the tense boundary between the remaining Ute and the growing American settler population. Soldiers bathed in the sulfur water and used it for treating skin conditions, a practical adaptation of exactly the same knowledge the Ute had held for generations. The camp became one of several precursors to the first Fort Lewis, whose full history and subsequent move northward was covered in the Durango episode.

Within a decade of the Ute removal, private commercial development of the springs began. A bathhouse was operating by 1880. The springs changed hands several times over the following decades, operating as a modest resort and health destination. The Ute who had fought over these waters within living memory of the first American owners were by then on their reservation in the southwest corner of the state, 80 miles to the west.

Albert Pfeiffer died in 1881 at Abiquiu, New Mexico, the same year the people he had defended at Pagosa were removed from their land. He was buried with his second wife's family. His biography is a genuinely uncomfortable historical document: a man who defended the Ute with his own body in 1866 and who also participated in the Long Walk against the Navajo three years earlier. The frontier Southwest does not often produce simple heroes or straightforward villains, and Pfeiffer is one of the clearer examples of why.

The springs he helped the Ute hold have been running ever since.
