In 1923, the road over Wolf Creek Pass existed. But calling it a road requires some generosity of definition.

The Colorado State Highway Commission had taken over what was previously a county wagon road in 1916 and was in the process of converting it from a rough dirt track suitable for pack mules and the most determined early automobiles into something a regular passenger car could use reliably. By 1923, the pass was technically open to automobile traffic. The specific conditions of that traffic, however, need description.

The grade on the eastern approach was 11 percent in places. Most 1923 automobiles could barely manage 7 or 8 percent grades without overheating on warm days. The road surface was unpaved, which in mountain conditions meant alternately muddy and deeply rutted when wet, and layered with loose dust that could reduce visibility to a few yards when dry. There were no guardrails. The drop on the western side of the pass in certain sections was 300 to 400 feet to the canyon bottom below. The road was single-lane on most of the upper grades, with occasional turnouts where opposing traffic could pass when two vehicles met going different directions. There was no guaranteed towing service if your car broke down, which cars of 1923 did with some regularity. The radiator of a 1923 automobile at 10,000 feet did not have a good day on a steep July climb.

The people who crossed Wolf Creek Pass in 1923 were participating in one of the great adventures of the early automobile era, and many of them knew it. The Rocky Mountain Motor Tour Guides published route descriptions and timing estimates for passes like Wolf Creek: allow six to eight hours from Durango to Alamosa, bring extra radiator water, plan to negotiate the grade in low gear, do not attempt in rain if avoidable. This was aspirational advice. Road conditions often made the timeline academic.

The cars crossing in 1923 were Model T Fords, early Dodge Brothers trucks, Studebakers, and the occasional heavier touring car with enough engine to manage the grade without boiling over. Their drivers wore goggles against the dust, tied luggage to the running boards, and carried tire patching kits because flat tires on unpaved mountain roads were routine rather than exceptional. Crossing Wolf Creek in 1923 was a mechanical achievement as well as a geographic one.

Pagosa Springs in 1923 was a ranching, logging, and sheep-grazing community of perhaps 1,500 people in town and the surrounding valley. The Piedra Valley and the upper San Juan corridor were sheep country: hundreds of thousands of animals were trailed up to national forest grazing allotments each summer under permits that set local economic fortunes. Wool markets in Denver determined whether a sheep ranching family had a good year or a hard one. A good shearing plus a good wool price meant another operating season; a bad year on either front could break a family operation that had been built over a generation.

The hot springs had been in commercial operation since 1880, when the first bathhouse was built at the site. By 1923, the facility had gone through several owners and was operating as a modest health resort, attracting visitors with arthritis, skin conditions, and respiratory ailments who believed, with some medical backing of the era, that mineral water immersion had therapeutic effects. They were not entirely wrong, though the physiological mechanism was different from what 1923 medicine imagined. A traveler arriving after the pass descent would have been ready for the sulfur pools in a way that specifically physical and dusty travel makes you ready for them.

This is the world that Taylor Sheridan's 1923 series is set in. The show is geographically Montana and Wyoming, not Colorado, but the economic and social texture is accurate for this region too. The ranching economy dependent on a single commodity market. The remoteness that made every supply run a logistics operation. The mountain passes that separated communities in winter not just inconveniently but completely. The springs as both practical resource and modest luxury for people who worked extremely hard with their bodies and had limited access to the comforts that urban Americans were beginning to take for granted.

What changed in the 15 years after 1923 was primarily the road. By 1938, Wolf Creek Pass had been graded and improved enough to hold pavement. By the mid-1950s, it was a recognizable version of what it is today: paved, properly graded, with at least nominal safety features. The crossing that took six to eight hours in 1923 now takes 45 minutes. The valley on the other side has not changed. The springs have not changed. The smell of the earth below Pagosa is the same smell a Model T driver with a steaming radiator noticed when the descent finally put him close enough to the river to roll the windows down.
