If you see a large dark animal browsing in a willow thicket along the San Juan River this morning, and your first instinct is that it seems enormous, and your second instinct is that it looks somehow wrong for this landscape, you are probably looking at a moose, and both instincts are correct.

Moose are not native to Colorado. They were introduced. The first animals arrived in 1978 when the Colorado Division of Wildlife brought twelve moose from Utah and released them near North Park in the Walden area of the state. A second introduction followed in 1987 and 1988, bringing animals from Wyoming. A third release targeted the Laramie River valley in 1991. The animals established breeding populations, expanded their range, and began moving south and west on their own. By the late 1990s, moose had reached the San Juan Mountains under their own locomotion.

They have been thriving here ever since.

The reason the Pagosa Springs area works for moose is the combination of two habitat elements that moose require above almost everything else: willow thickets along stream corridors and cold, dense forest. Moose are obligate willow browsers. A mature bull in peak condition needs roughly 50 to 60 pounds of browse per day in summer, and the San Juan River corridor, the Piedra River bottoms, the meadow margins along Wolf Creek, and the tributary streams feeding the upper San Juan drainage provide exactly the willow-rich habitat that Colorado wildlife managers were targeting when they selected introduction sites in the 1980s. The Pagosa region has among the highest moose densities in Colorado.

This matters because late May is one of the best possible times to encounter them.

Female moose, called cows, calve in late May and early June. A cow will separate from her calf of the previous year just before giving birth, which means that yearlings, roughly the size of a large horse, are suddenly moving around on their own, often confused and visible near roads and river banks. The new calf arrives days later, usually a single but occasionally twins. A newborn moose is roughly 30 to 35 pounds at birth, which is both enormous for a newborn and absurdly small relative to a mother who weighs up to 1,000 pounds. The calf is long-legged and wobbly for the first few days and stays very close to the cow.

Male moose, called bulls, are in late-May antler velvet. The racks that will reach full size by August begin growing in April and can add up to an inch of growth per day during peak season, making moose antlers among the fastest-growing tissues in any mammal. The blood vessels running through growing velvet antlers are extensive and visible from a distance: if you photograph a bull in velvet and zoom in, you can see the vascular pattern through the fur. The antlers are warm to the touch. A mature bull's rack can reach 60 to 70 inches across at full size; in late May it is still several months from its finished dimensions, covered in dense fur, and visually quite different from the polished bone of a September bull.

The contrast with elk, whose ecology you heard about in the Ridgway episode, is instructive. Both are large ungulates with significant presence in the San Juan region. An adult bull moose, however, outweighs even a large elk by 400 to 600 pounds and stands two to four feet taller at the shoulder. A moose does not move the way an elk moves: it is heavier, less elegant, more deliberate. Where an elk herd moves through a meadow with a kind of fluid coordination, a moose tends to move alone through its territory, stopping to browse, standing in streams, indifferent to observation.

The danger question requires directness. Cow moose in calving season are among the most dangerous large mammals in North America by incident count. More people are injured by moose in North America each year than by any other large mammal, including bears. A cow with a new calf will charge anything she perceives as a threat, including people, dogs, horses, vehicles, and in documented cases, black bears considerably larger than her calf. The charge, when it comes, is fast, and it does not involve warning behavior beyond flattening the ears and raising the hackles on the neck. The cow does not look threatening at rest: she looks like a very large, somewhat odd-proportioned animal doing nothing. The charge is the surprise.

If you encounter a cow with a calf on foot or from a vehicle, give her a wide berth. Do not get between her and the calf under any circumstances. If you are on foot and she charges, get behind something solid: a large tree, a vehicle, a building. The standard bear behavior of making yourself large and speaking calmly does not work with a cow moose with a calf. She is not bluffing, and she will follow through.

Watch for moose in willow thickets along any stream course from the Wolf Creek descent down through Pagosa and along the San Juan River corridor. Dawn and dusk are peak activity hours, but moose near Pagosa have become habituated enough to human presence that midday sightings near town are not rare. The specific combination of willow, spruce-fir forest, and cold clear water that describes this valley is what moose managers mean when they say high-quality habitat. The animals here are among the most well-established introduced population in the American Rockies, in a landscape where, by every ecological measure, they belong.
