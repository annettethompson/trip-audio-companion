You are about to cross one of the great mountain passes in Colorado, and you are doing it on a US highway, which means it is paved, two-laned, and has a gas station at the summit. This is a substantial improvement over historical standards. Until 1916, there was no road over Wolf Creek Pass at all. Until the 1930s, crossing it in anything but settled weather required substantial conviction. Even today, in winter, Wolf Creek Pass closes more often than any other pass on the US highway system in Colorado, which is saying something given the competition.

US-160 climbs from Durango to the pass at 10,857 feet above sea level, making it one of the highest paved US highways in the country. The route follows the general path of one of the oldest trading corridors in the Southwest. The Ute traveled it for centuries. Spanish explorers documented the route in the late 18th century. When American settlers began arriving in the 1870s, they pushed wagon roads over the same grade the Ute had used long before.

Watch on the way up for the transition in forest type. Below about 9,000 feet, you are still in ponderosa pine and Gambel oak territory, the same parklike open forest familiar from the Durango area, the full story of which you heard in the ponderosa chapter. As you gain elevation, the trees get shorter and denser. Around 9,500 feet, Engelmann spruce begins to appear, first scattered among the pines, then increasingly dominant. By the time you reach the pass zone, you are in full subalpine forest: the dense, dark, spire-crowned spruce and fir that define Colorado above the lower subalpine boundary. The full story of those trees is in the chapter ahead.

At the summit, if you have time to pull over at the overlook, look north. You are standing on the Continental Divide. Water that falls on the western slope of this ridge flows west and south, eventually reaching the Colorado River and then the Gulf of California. Water that falls on the eastern slope drains into the Rio Grande system, eventually crossing New Mexico before reaching the Gulf of Mexico. Every drop that lands on the west side of where you are standing right now is on a different ocean track than every drop on the east side.

The San Juan River, which runs through Pagosa Springs, begins its life on this divide and immediately east of it. You will hear the full story of the San Juan and where it goes in the river chapter.

The scale of the Weminuche Wilderness becomes visible from the summit zone. The dark, continuous forest stretching west and north from the pass, rising to bare rocky peaks above treeline, is the edge of Colorado's largest wilderness area. It extends for nearly 500,000 acres from here westward across the San Juan high country. You will hear more about the Weminuche in the chapter dedicated to it.

Descending the eastern side of the pass, the valley opens. This is the upper San Juan River basin: a broad agricultural and ranching valley backed by the continuous wall of the Continental Divide peaks. The snowpack on those peaks above you is still releasing into the river system now, in late May, which is why the San Juan through Pagosa will be running high and fast today.

The distance from Wolf Creek Pass summit to Pagosa Springs is 17 miles downhill. The town will appear on your right along the San Juan River. Before you see it, if the windows are down, you will likely smell it. The sulfur note in the air is subtle at first and unmistakable once you know what it is. It is the Great Pagosa Hot Spring, announcing itself the way it has been announcing itself for centuries: through the nose before the eyes.
