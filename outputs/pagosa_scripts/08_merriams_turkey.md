The sound will reach you before the bird does. It is a loud, cascading gobble that carries considerably farther than you would expect from any animal smaller than a large mammal, a rolling declaration that echoes off canyon walls and carries across meadow edges in the cool morning air of the San Juan foothills. If you hear it anywhere between Wolf Creek Pass and the valley floor on a May morning, you are listening to a Merriam's turkey announcing itself to the world with the urgency it feels every spring from April through early June.

Wild turkeys in Colorado are Merriam's turkeys, a Rocky Mountain subspecies of the North American wild turkey that differs in meaningful ways from the eastern wild turkeys most people picture from the standard Thanksgiving image. The Merriam's is a bird of ponderosa pine and mixed mountain forest rather than the deciduous woodland of the eastern seaboard. Its tail fan and wing tips are whiter than the eastern subspecies, giving it a striking contrast between the iridescent bronze of the body feathers and the bright white and buff margins of the spread tail. It lives at elevations that would be physiologically difficult for eastern wild turkeys: in Colorado, Merriam's turkeys regularly winter at 7,000 to 8,000 feet and move up to 9,500 feet and higher during summer.

Late May is the back end of turkey breeding season, and the adult male birds, called gobblers or toms, are at their most vocal. The gobble is the primary long-distance location call, used to attract hens and establish dominance with competing males. Gobblers also produce a range of quieter sounds: yelps, clucks, purrs, and the spit-and-drum, a rapid puff of expelled air followed by a low-frequency vibration produced by fanning the wings against the chest, which has to be experienced at close range to be believed as something a bird is actually making.

The full breeding display of a mature Merriam's gobbler, with the tail fanned, the beard dragging the ground, the head caruncles flushed red and blue, and the body feathers puffed to maximum width, is one of the more spectacular behavioral performances in the bird world. The beard, which is the modified feather cluster hanging from the chest, grows throughout a tom's life: a two-year-old bird may carry a beard of 8 to 10 inches; a four or five-year-old bird can have a beard exceeding 12 inches. The beard drags the ground during the strut, leaving a distinctive mark in soft soil that experienced turkey hunters can use to estimate a bird's age.

Pagosa Springs has a reputation among western turkey hunters that exceeds its geographic profile. The region consistently produces gobblers with significant age and body weight, which in turkey hunting terms is the combination that creates the most impressive beard length and spur development. The spurs, the bony protrusions on the rear of the legs, also grow throughout a tom's life. Older birds have long, curved spurs that are one of the standard measures of a trophy turkey. The ponderosa pine and mixed forest surrounding the Pagosa plateau, combined with the spruce-fir forest at higher elevation and the open grassland and agricultural edges in the valley floor, creates a mosaic of habitat types that Merriam's turkeys use differently by season: high forests in summer, lower open areas in winter, and in spring specifically the structural edges where forest meets meadow, where gobblers can be visible to distant hens while escape cover is always within 50 yards.

The relationship between Merriam's turkey and the forest type is worth pausing on. In the ponderosa pine zone below about 9,000 feet, turkeys find a combination that suits them precisely: open enough for visual communication and display, with large seed crops and invertebrate foraging in the needle duff, and roost trees with accessible limbs. The subalpine spruce-fir forest above 9,500 feet is less used by turkeys because the dense canopy and deep snow reduce the foraging options that define turkey habitat. The transition zone, exactly where the ponderosa gives way to the first Engelmann spruce, is where turkey density peaks in the Pagosa area.

The domestic turkey that appears on American holiday tables is descended from the South Mexican subspecies of wild turkey, brought to Europe by Spanish explorers in the early 1500s and selected over generations for breast size, rapid weight gain, and a calm temperament suited to confinement. The result is biologically related to but essentially unrecognizable as the same genus as the Merriam's you might see on the forest edge outside Pagosa. A domestic broad-breasted white turkey cannot fly, cannot navigate terrain more complex than a flat pen, and requires active human management to survive. A wild Merriam's turkey can fly a quarter mile at a time, roosts in trees every night, can cover ten miles of rugged terrain in a day, and will live 10 to 12 years in the wild. Same species. Unrecognizably different animals.

If you want to see turkeys on this drive, look at the edges. Not deep in the forest, not in the open field: at the margins where the two habitats meet. A flock of hens moving through open grass in the early morning, often with one or two gobblers still displaying at the rear of the group. Later in the day, as warmth builds, the birds move into shade and the morning acoustic environment quiets. If the morning produces nothing, come back at dusk.
