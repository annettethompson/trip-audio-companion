The water flowing through Pagosa Springs today started as snow on the Continental Divide above Wolf Creek Pass. It fell last winter, accumulated through months of cold and compression, and has been releasing gradually since the snowmelt began in April. What moves through the town now is part snowmelt river and part geothermal spring, because the Great Pagosa Hot Spring has been contributing its flow to the San Juan River continuously for as long as the river has existed in its current geological form.

The San Juan River is one of the major rivers of the Colorado Plateau system. It rises on the eastern flanks of the San Juan Mountains near the Continental Divide, flows west through Pagosa Springs, continues across northwestern New Mexico, is impounded at Navajo Lake, released into canyon country west of Farmington, and eventually joins the Colorado River at the Utah-Arizona border in what is now Lake Powell. Total length: approximately 383 miles. Total watershed: roughly 24,000 square miles spanning parts of Colorado, New Mexico, Utah, and Arizona.

At Pagosa Springs, the river is young and fast. The gradient from the upper watershed, where it collects snowmelt from peaks above 13,000 feet, produces a current that carries significant volume in late May and early June. This is peak runoff timing. The winter snowpack on the Continental Divide above town is releasing through the river system now, and the San Juan near Pagosa in late May runs higher and faster than it will by August. For kayakers and whitewater paddlers, the upper San Juan in peak runoff is a different river from the low-water mid-summer version.

The riparian corridor along the San Juan through Pagosa supports a distinct ecological community separate from the dry ponderosa forests on the surrounding slopes. Cottonwood, willow, alder, and water birch line the banks, creating dense shade and root systems that stabilize the channel and produce the invertebrate habitat that forms the base of the aquatic food chain. Aquatic insects, stoneflies, mayflies, and caddisflies are present in the upper San Juan in healthy numbers. All three are indicator species for water quality: they tolerate cold, oxygen-rich, clean water and are absent from degraded systems. Their presence in the upper San Juan above Pagosa is a water quality statement.

The geothermal input from the Great Pagosa Hot Spring raises the local river temperature measurably downstream from the source. In winter, this effect is visible: the river below the spring does not freeze as quickly as it does above. In summer, the temperature contribution is diluted rapidly by the cold snowmelt volume, but the mineral signature of the spring persists farther downstream. The elevated sulfate and silica concentrations from the spring have been measured in river chemistry studies well into New Mexico, traceable in the water column as a chemical fingerprint of what the earth is adding at Pagosa.

The Navajo Nation, whose treaty territory the lower San Juan crosses in New Mexico, has documented relationships with the river stretching back centuries. The river's lower course runs through land that has been Dine homeland for generations, and the river's health is not an abstract environmental metric to communities that draw from it directly. The Southern Ute tribe's water rights in the San Juan system are legally active and have been the subject of ongoing tribal negotiations and federal adjudications since the 1970s. Water rights in the American West are always a legal landscape as well as a physical one, and the San Juan is no exception: the allocation of its flow between Colorado, New Mexico, Utah, Arizona, and the Navajo Nation reflects treaties, court orders, and compacts stretching back to the 1920s.

The water quality of the San Juan downstream of Pagosa has been complicated by the same regional mining legacy that affected the Animas River, whose story you heard in the Durango episode. The specific Gold King Mine event is that story; the broader pattern of acid mine drainage into San Juan system tributaries is an ongoing management challenge that involves the EPA, the states, and tribal nations simultaneously.

What runs through this town is both geological and atmospheric: snowmelt and geothermal water mixing at the place where the Continental Divide meets the first broad valley. The river runs away from the mountains carrying both kinds of water and a mineral signature that the earth began producing long before any human used the word Pagosa to describe it.
