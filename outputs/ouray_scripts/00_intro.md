# Chapter 00: Introduction — Ice, Fire, and the Road That Shouldn't Exist
*Estimated duration: 2.8 minutes | Word count: 385*

This is Day 3 of the Colorado Hot Springs Road Trip Audio Companion. If you heard Day 2, you know where you are: Ridgway, Colorado, in the shadow of the San Juan Mountains, and you slept last night with volcanic ash millions of years old beneath your feet and lithium-rich spring water still warm in your bones. Today everything gets bigger.

Between Ridgway and Silverton, you will drive what many people call the most spectacular highway in North America. You will climb from high mountain valley to an 11,018-foot pass where the air carries only two-thirds the oxygen you breathe at sea level. You will see rock faces turned red and orange and yellow and white by chemistry that has nothing to do with the Cutler Formation's ancient iron. You will look up at cliff walls and understand, for the first time, that something enormous once filled this valley from wall to wall. Not water. Ice.

The San Juan Mountains were heavily glaciated. During the Pleistocene, ice sheets hundreds of feet thick carved the U-shaped valleys you will drive through today. They left cirques, the high bowl-shaped theaters carved into peaks by ice that gathered and scraped and moved with geologic patience. They left hanging valleys, side valleys that were carved by smaller glaciers and now terminate abruptly at a cliff face, their floors suspended hundreds of feet above the main valley floor. When you see a waterfall pouring from a clifftop with no visible canyon above it, you are looking at a hanging valley. There are several of them today.

We will give you the specific formations visible from the Million Dollar Highway, and we will tell you what the engineers who built it were working with. We will explain why Red Mountain is red in a way that is entirely different from why the Cutler Formation is red. We will introduce you to the people and plants and animals that live at the edge of what plants and animals can survive. And we will place you in a Model T, summer of 1921, inching around a switchback with no guardrail and a 400-foot drop, white-knuckling a steering wheel while a horse pulls a freight wagon toward you from the other direction.

Ridgway is waking up. Let's drive.
