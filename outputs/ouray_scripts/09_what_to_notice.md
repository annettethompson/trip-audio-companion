# Chapter 09: Eyes on the Road — What to Notice on Today's Drive
*Estimated duration: 6.0 minutes | Word count: 825*

This chapter does not explain anything. That is the job of the other chapters. This chapter points.

Start in Ridgway. Before you reach the car, look south from wherever you are. The peaks directly ahead of you, the jagged skyline on the horizon, are the Sneffels Range. Mount Sneffels itself, at 14,150 feet, is the prominent summit slightly right of center with its distinctive pyramidal profile. The reason the peaks look so jagged, so improbably knife-edged, is covered in the geology chapters. For now: look and notice how different this skyline is from the rounded peaks of the Front Range or the gentler profiles of the Elk Mountains you saw on the drive from Glenwood. The San Juans have a different character. The glaciation chapter will tell you why.

Leaving Ridgway south on US-550, the Uncompahgre River valley begins to narrow. Notice how the valley walls change height and character as you drive south. In the first five miles, the walls are relatively low and graded, foothills-scale. By the time you reach the outskirts of Ouray, they are canyon walls. This is not a gradual transition. The narrowing is abrupt, and the reason is geological structure: you are crossing from the relatively wide valley carved in sedimentary and volcanic material to the canyon carved through the hard Precambrian basement. Different rock, different canyon character.

Ouray announces itself with noise before it announces itself with signs. The sound of the Uncompahgre River, amplified by the narrow canyon, reaches you through the car windows even if the windows are closed. Roll them down when you hear it. The town sits at the base of cliffs on three sides, which gives it an enclosed quality unlike any other town on this trip. Look up. The canyon rim is hundreds of feet above the townsite on the east and west walls. Waterfalls are visible during late spring snowmelt from the canyon walls near town.

On the drive south from Ouray toward Red Mountain Pass, watch for several specific things. First, the rock color change in the roadcut on your right. Within the first two miles south of Ouray, the roadcut exposes the contact between the dark grey Precambrian metamorphic rocks and the overlying volcanic sequence. The contact is not always sharp in exposure, but the color change is visible: from dark grey and maroon to lighter, more varied volcanic rock. Watch for it.

Second, watch for the hanging valley waterfalls on the canyon walls to your right and left. These are not at stream level. They pour from the cliff face, from a height that makes no sense if you are imagining a river canyon, only sense if you are imagining a glacier. The falls themselves may be running hard in late May from snowmelt. Some of them only run in spring and are dry by August.

Third, look at the canyon bottom where the road crosses the stream. The water color tells you something. Clear, blue-green water indicates relatively clean drainage. Orange-stained water, particularly at tributary creek confluences, indicates acid mine drainage from the Red Mountain district above. This is not a historical artifact; it is current chemistry, and some of the tributaries run orange year-round.

At the hairpin switchbacks approaching Red Mountain Pass, slow down if traffic allows and look at the exposed road fills on the outside edge of the switchbacks. You are looking at the constructed fill material that holds the road on the cliff face, and in some places it is visible as exactly what it is: crushed rock, mixed gravel, blasted material placed against the cliff. The road is not sitting on a natural ledge. The ledge was built.

At Red Mountain Pass itself, pull over if the lot is accessible. The view north back down toward Ouray shows the full U-shaped valley carved by glaciation, discussed in Chapter 01. The view south toward Silverton shows a different character: the valley opens up somewhat as you cross the drainage divide, and the mine tailings piles of the Red Mountain district are visible on multiple flanks above the road. The colors in those tailings, the yellows and oranges and reds and occasional greens, are the iron sulfide chemistry covered in Chapter 03. From this viewpoint you can see the whole color palette at once.

Descending from Red Mountain Pass toward Silverton, the road enters a stretch that in winter is avalanche country. Watch for the avalanche paths: wide swaths cut through the spruce forest where nothing taller than young brush grows. These are not old clearcuts. They are recurring avalanche tracks where the snow removes everything every few years and the vegetation restarts from scratch. The avalanche chutes have their own ecology: specific plants colonize them, including subalpine meadow species that grow faster than trees and can recover from the next slide. Watch for the transition between the avalanche chute vegetation and the undisturbed mature spruce forest. The boundary is sharp.

The Animas River appears at the bottom of the descent. Follow it: you are looking at the drainage that connects Silverton to Durango, the canyon that carries the narrow gauge railroad, the river that the miners had to figure out before they could move ore in volume. It is running fast in late May. The color should be green-grey. If it runs orange or brown near any tributary, the mine drainage from above Silverton is finding its way into the mainstem.

Silverton appears ahead as the valley widens. It will look smaller than you expect. That is because it is smaller than the legends suggest. There were never more than about 2,000 people here even in the boom years. The town that survived everything is exactly this size.
