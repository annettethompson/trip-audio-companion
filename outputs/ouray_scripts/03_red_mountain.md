# Chapter 03: The Red That Is Not the Same Red — Red Mountain Pass
*Estimated duration: 9.0 minutes | Word count: 1,240*

You have already heard, in the Day 2 episode, why the Cutler Formation is red. The Cutler is a Pennsylvanian sedimentary rock unit, roughly 300 million years old, and its red color comes from hematite, iron oxide in the ferric form, essentially rust coating the individual sand and gravel grains. The Cutler red is old red, sedimentary red, the color of a continent that was eroded and deposited and lithified into rock. It is iron in its most stable, fully oxidized state.

The red at Red Mountain Pass is entirely different. Different mineralogy, different chemistry, different process, different age. They look similar from a moving car, which is exactly why understanding the difference matters. When Bob explains to you tonight why the pass is red, he will want to have the right answer.

Red Mountain Pass sits at 11,018 feet at the crest of a divide between the Uncompahgre River drainage to the north and the Uncompahgre River's tributary drainages to the south. The pass itself sits at the center of the Red Mountain mining district, a cluster of three volcanic mountains whose surfaces are brilliantly colored in shades of red, orange, ochre, and rust. Red Mountain No. 1 is 12,218 feet. Red Mountain No. 2 is 12,216 feet. Red Mountain No. 3 is 12,237 feet. They sit close together, and from certain viewpoints their red flanks dominate the skyline in a way that makes them look like they are on fire.

The red comes from iron sulfide minerals in the process of oxidizing. The key mineral is pyrite, iron disulfide, the classic fool's gold. Pyrite forms in enormous quantities in hydrothermal ore deposits, and the Red Mountain district was one of the most intensely mineralized zones in the San Juans. When hydrothermal fluids moved through fractures and deposited their ore load, they deposited far more pyrite than silver or gold. The silver and gold were the economically valuable minority. The pyrite was everywhere.

Pyrite is chemically unstable at the surface. In the presence of water and oxygen, it begins to oxidize. The chemistry unfolds in steps. Pyrite (FeS2) oxidizes to form ferrous sulfate and sulfuric acid. The ferrous iron then oxidizes further to ferric iron, and the ferric iron precipitates as iron oxyhydroxide minerals: goethite, which is yellow-brown to orange, and jarosite, which is bright yellow. Further oxidation and dehydration converts these to hematite, the stable red oxide that is also present in the Cutler Formation. But at Red Mountain, you are seeing all stages of this process simultaneously, not just the final stable hematite. You are seeing the reaction in progress.

This matters for the color palette. Jarosite is sulfur yellow. Goethite is yellow-orange to orange-brown. Hematite is red to deep red. The multi-stage oxidation of pyrite gives you a spectrum: yellow at the base of the reaction, orange in the middle, red where the process has run to completion. Look at the hillsides around the pass and you can often see all three colors in a single slope, sometimes in distinct bands. This is oxidation chemistry written at landscape scale, visible from a moving vehicle.

The sulfuric acid produced by pyrite oxidation is not a minor byproduct. It is a major product, and it does significant work. The acid dissolves other minerals in the surrounding rock, leaching the rock of its carbonate cements and leaving it friable and crumbly, easily eroded. The acid also reacts with other metal sulfides, if present, producing more complex mineral suites. Where copper sulfides were present, the acid leaching produces the green malachite and blue azurite you saw in the canyon walls. Where zinc sulfides were present, you get white or pale minerals. Each mineral in the tailings and natural outcrops is a record of what was dissolved and reprecipitated, in what sequence.

Mine tailings add a second layer of color to the landscape at Red Mountain. Tailings are the crushed rock waste left after ore processing. In the early mining era, ore processing involved crushing the rock and using water and sometimes chemicals to separate the valuable minerals from the waste rock. The tailings piles, the crushed waste, were often dumped directly into creek drainages or spread on hillsides near the mills. They contain the same mix of sulfide minerals as the undisturbed rock, but at much smaller particle size, which means the oxidation chemistry happens faster and more completely.

Active tailings piles can produce what mine drainage researchers call acid rock drainage: acidic, metal-laden water that colors streams orange and yellow and suppresses aquatic life. The Uncompahgre River and many of its tributaries in this district have been significantly impacted by acid mine drainage. Environmental remediation work has been ongoing for decades in the Red Mountain district. Some streams that were lifeless for much of the 20th century are beginning to recover.

Stand at Red Mountain Pass and look at the mine tailings visible from the road. You will see a color palette that tells you exactly what was mined. The yellow-white is jarosite from pyrite oxidation. The orange is goethite, the first stable iron oxyhydroxide. The red is hematite. The green-blue-green is copper mineral alteration: malachite and azurite. The white or pale material is often calcium sulfate, gypsum, or zinc minerals. If you see brown-black, that is manganese oxide, a secondary mineral that precipitates when manganese is present in the original ore.

The pass itself is bare of vegetation on the steepest slopes not because of altitude alone, though altitude is a factor. The acid rock drainage creates soils too acidic for most plants to establish. The characteristic vegetation of Red Mountain's most impacted slopes is often sparse to absent, and what does grow tends to be acidophilic species tolerant of low pH and high metal concentrations. This is why the flanks of the Red Mountains look bare compared to equally high but unmineralized slopes nearby. The mountain is not just colored by its chemistry; it is shaped by it.

The naming of the mountains is satisfyingly unimaginative. Red Mountains 1, 2, and 3, numbered in the order they were encountered. The miners who named them were practical people. What else would you call them.
