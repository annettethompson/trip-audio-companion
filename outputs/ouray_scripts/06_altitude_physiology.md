# Chapter 06: What Your Body Does at 11,018 Feet — Altitude Physiology
*Estimated duration: 10.5 minutes | Word count: 1,445*

Annette, this chapter is yours. We are going to do real altitude medicine, not the summary version.

You have been at elevation since you arrived in Colorado. Glenwood Springs sits at 5,756 feet. The Day 1 episode covered the basics of altitude physiology there: barometric pressure drops with altitude, the fractional oxygen content of the air stays the same at 20.9% everywhere from sea level to Everest base camp, but the partial pressure of oxygen drops proportionally with barometric pressure. At Glenwood, you were breathing air at a barometric pressure of roughly 625 mmHg, compared to 760 mmHg at sea level. The alveolar partial pressure of oxygen at Glenwood is meaningfully reduced. This is covered in the first episode and we are not going to repeat it.

What we are going to do is go further, because Red Mountain Pass is 11,018 feet, and at 11,018 feet the physiology is genuinely different from what happens at 5,756.

The standard barometric pressure at 11,018 feet is approximately 510 mmHg, compared to 760 at sea level. This is 67% of sea-level barometric pressure. The partial pressure of inspired oxygen, after accounting for water vapor pressure in the airways, is approximately 100 mmHg at sea level and approximately 65 mmHg at 11,018 feet. That is a 35% reduction in the driving pressure for oxygen to move from your lungs into your blood.

Now let's go through the alveolar gas equation, which tells us the actual oxygen pressure in your alveoli, the tiny air sacs where gas exchange happens. The simplified alveolar gas equation is: PAO2 equals FiO2 times (Patm minus PH2O) minus (PaCO2 divided by R). Let's translate that. PAO2 is alveolar oxygen partial pressure. FiO2 is 0.209, the fractional concentration of oxygen in air. Patm is atmospheric pressure. PH2O is water vapor pressure in the airways, which is 47 mmHg regardless of altitude, because the airways are always saturated with water vapor at body temperature. PaCO2 is arterial carbon dioxide partial pressure, approximately 40 mmHg at sea level. R is the respiratory quotient, approximately 0.8 on a mixed diet.

At sea level: PAO2 equals 0.209 times (760 minus 47) minus (40 divided by 0.8). This gives approximately 100 mmHg. At 11,018 feet: PAO2 equals 0.209 times (510 minus 47) minus (40 divided by 0.8). This gives approximately 47 mmHg. Not 100. Forty-seven. The driving pressure for oxygen to cross from your alveoli into your capillaries is less than half of what it is at sea level.

Hemoglobin is designed to load oxygen at the alveolar partial pressure and deliver it to tissues. But hemoglobin's oxygen-saturation relationship is not linear. It is a sigmoid curve, the oxyhemoglobin dissociation curve, and the important feature of its shape is that it has a flat upper portion. At sea level, with PAO2 around 100 mmHg, hemoglobin is approximately 98% saturated with oxygen. The flat upper portion of the curve means that hemoglobin saturation is relatively insensitive to drops in partial pressure at the high end. Even if PAO2 drops to 70 or 80 mmHg, saturation stays near 95%.

But at 11,018 feet, you are not on the flat upper portion of the curve anymore. An alveolar PAO2 of 47 mmHg puts you on the steep portion of the sigmoid. At that partial pressure, hemoglobin saturation is roughly 85 to 88%. Some of your hemoglobin is arriving at your tissues only partially loaded with oxygen.

Your body's first response to this is hypoxic ventilatory response: you breathe more. This is involuntary. The carotid body chemoreceptors, tiny clusters of cells at the bifurcation of the carotid arteries in your neck, detect the drop in arterial oxygen and send signals to the respiratory center in the brainstem to increase breathing rate and depth. This is fast, happening within seconds of the drop in oxygen, and it is significant: breathing rate at altitude can increase 20 to 30% even in the first hour.

The increased breathing does two things simultaneously. It helps: more breathing brings more fresh air into the alveoli per minute, which partially compensates for the lower partial pressure by increasing alveolar ventilation. But it also causes a problem: it blows off carbon dioxide. CO2 is an acid, and when you exhale more of it, your blood becomes less acidic. The kidneys respond to this alkalosis by excreting bicarbonate to restore acid-base balance, but this process takes 24 to 48 hours. In the meantime, the respiratory alkalosis partially blunts the hypoxic ventilatory response, because a less acidic blood environment tells the chemoreceptors to ease off a little.

This is why acclimatization takes time. The full ventilatory response requires the kidneys to correct the blood pH, which takes days. The kidneys also have another acclimatization role: they increase production of erythropoietin, EPO, which signals the bone marrow to produce more red blood cells. This is the performance-enhancing adaptation that altitude athletes are trying to stimulate by sleeping at altitude. More red blood cells means more hemoglobin molecules, which means more oxygen transport capacity even at reduced saturation. But erythropoiesis takes weeks to manifest in meaningfully increased hematocrit. Full acclimatization to altitude is a process of weeks, not hours.

You are not fully acclimatized. You have been in Colorado for three days. Your kidneys have begun excreting bicarbonate. Your breathing rate has increased. Your heart rate is slightly elevated, doing extra work to pump the same cardiac output through a system where each liter of blood is carrying slightly less oxygen. But your hematocrit has not meaningfully changed, and the full acclimatization response is weeks away.

One molecular detail that does begin within hours: the concentration of 2,3-diphosphoglycerate, or 2,3-DPG, inside your red blood cells increases at altitude. 2,3-DPG is a metabolic intermediate in red blood cell glycolysis, and it has a specific effect on hemoglobin: it binds to the protein and shifts the oxyhemoglobin dissociation curve to the right. A right shift means hemoglobin releases oxygen more readily at any given partial pressure. At altitude, where you need to extract as much oxygen as possible from your partially saturated hemoglobin, this right shift is beneficial. Your tissues get more oxygen per delivery cycle. The 2,3-DPG increase begins within hours of altitude exposure and is one of the early acclimatization responses that is working in your favor already.

What this means practically for your day at Red Mountain Pass: at 11,018 feet, your breathing will feel slightly labored with exertion. Hiking or climbing stairs will feel harder than it did in Ridgway. Headache is the most common symptom of acute mountain sickness, and it can appear at elevations above 8,000 feet in susceptible individuals. Nausea, fatigue, and sleep disruption are other common symptoms. Most people who feel fine in Ridgway do well on Red Mountain Pass for a brief visit, because they are there for minutes to hours, not overnight. Staying overnight at the pass would produce more significant symptoms in unacclimatized people.

UV radiation at 11,018 feet is significantly higher than at sea level. The atmosphere at high altitude is thinner and contains less ozone and water vapor, both of which absorb UV. UV radiation intensity increases approximately 4 to 6% per 1,000 feet of elevation gain. At 11,018 feet, you are receiving roughly 50% more UV than at sea level. If the snowpack around the pass is reflecting sunlight upward, the effect compounds. Sunscreen is not optional in late May at this elevation.

The air temperature at the pass in late May is variable. Even on a warm day in Ridgway, the pass can be cold and windy. The environmental lapse rate, the decrease in temperature with altitude, is approximately 3.5 to 5 degrees Fahrenheit per 1,000 feet. You have gained roughly 4,000 feet from Ridgway to Red Mountain Pass. That is 14 to 20 degrees colder at the pass than in the valley, before accounting for wind. Wind chill at exposed alpine passes can be severe even in late May.

Enjoy the view. And then get back to the car.
