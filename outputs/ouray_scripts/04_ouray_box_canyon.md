# Chapter 04: The Oldest Rock in the Canyon — Box Canyon Falls
*Estimated duration: 7.5 minutes | Word count: 1,030*

Walk to the edge of the Box Canyon gorge and look down, and you are looking at something that took 1.7 billion years to set up and a few thousand years of post-glacial water to reveal.

Box Canyon Falls is located just outside the town of Ouray, a short walk from the main street, and it is one of the most visually striking waterfalls in Colorado. Not because of its height, though it falls about 285 feet through the gorge. And not because of its volume, though the spring snowmelt flow is substantial. What makes Box Canyon remarkable is the gorge itself: a slot canyon carved through Precambrian schist, walls nearly vertical, barely 20 feet wide at the bottom, dropping to a chamber that the sky barely reaches.

The rock in the Box Canyon gorge is the Uncompahgre metamorphic suite, Precambrian basement rocks that are among the oldest exposed bedrock in Colorado. The metamorphic history of these rocks is complex. They were originally sedimentary and volcanic rocks deposited on the early ocean floor, then deeply buried, heated, and compressed during mountain-building events that predated the Rocky Mountains entirely, predated the dinosaurs entirely, predated most of the fossil record. The intense heat and pressure recrystallized the original minerals into a new set of metamorphic minerals: mica, amphibole, feldspar, and quartz in a layered, foliated fabric that gives the rock its characteristic streaky appearance.

The result of this metamorphic history is a rock that is extremely hard and extremely resistant to erosion. Schist and gneiss are not easily dissolved or mechanically broken down. They require either the sustained abrasion of a river carrying sand and gravel, the grinding force of glacial ice, or the crack propagation of freezing water. Box Canyon Creek has been doing all three in the gorge since the glaciers melted.

Box Canyon Creek is a small drainage by the standards of the San Juan Mountains. Under normal conditions, it carries a modest flow. But it occupies a narrow fault-controlled channel in the Precambrian rock, and faults in hard metamorphic rock create zones of weakness: crushed and fractured rock that erodes faster than the intact walls on either side. The gorge is, in part, a fault trace, a line of weakness that the creek has been exploiting since the Pleistocene glaciers uncovered this landscape.

The upper walls of the gorge are vertical or even slightly overhanging in places. This geometry develops in hard rock when erosion progresses faster vertically than laterally: the crack deepens faster than it widens. The result is a slot canyon, and slot canyons in hard Precambrian rock are relatively rare. Most slot canyons in the American Southwest form in sandstone, which is soft enough to be dissolved and abraded quickly. Box Canyon's schist slot is geologically distinctive.

The waterfall at the base of the gorge drops into a plunge pool, a rounded basin scoured by the impact of the falling water. Plunge pools in hard rock form when the turbulence of the falling water creates sufficient hydraulic energy to abrade the rock floor. The plunge pool at Box Canyon is visible from the bridge that spans the gorge above it, and at high water the spray from the falls fills the chamber.

There is a viewing area reached by a tunnel through the rock, which is worth noting not just for the view but as an experience of the rock itself. The tunnel walls are unlined, polished metamorphic schist. You can put your hand on rock that was formed under conditions that would vaporize human tissue, more than ten miles below the surface, at temperatures above 1,300 degrees Fahrenheit. The heat and pressure required to produce the mineral assemblage in the tunnel walls did not exist anywhere accessible to human experience until geology exposed it here. This is what billions of years of uplift and erosion have delivered to you: the deep interior, served at the surface.

The depth of the gorge increases as you go downstream, not upstream. This seems counterintuitive. Most canyons cut headward, deepening at the upper end as the waterfall retreats. Box Canyon does cut headward, but the gorge is more impressive at its lower end because the resistant Precambrian rock contains a vertical gradient in strength. The lowermost exposure, where the creek emerges from the gorge, is in the most intensely metamorphosed, hardest rock. The upper portion is in slightly less resistant rock where the original sedimentary layering is more intact. The gorge geometry reflects these differences.

There is a story worth knowing here about the town and its waterfall. Ouray was founded in 1876, the year of Colorado statehood, and its founders quickly recognized that Box Canyon was not just geology but tourism. By 1890, Ouray was marketing itself as the Switzerland of America, a phrase that every mountain town in Colorado also uses, and Box Canyon was part of the pitch. The falls were gated for an admission fee as early as 1904. The fee has not changed in ambition if not in amount: it has always been modest, the canyon has always been worth it, and the Precambrian schist at the bottom has never looked any different than it does today, because 1.7 billion years of compression do not respond to a tourist season.
