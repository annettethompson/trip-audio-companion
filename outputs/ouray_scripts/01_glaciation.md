# Chapter 01: The Ice That Built What You See — Glaciation in the San Juans
*Estimated duration: 10.5 minutes | Word count: 1,445*

Look at the valley you are driving through and think about what it would take to carve it.

The Uncompahgre River occupies the bottom. A river that size, in the time available, would carve a V. Deep and narrow and steep-walled, like Glenwood Canyon, where the Roaring Fork cut through the Pennsylvanian rock over millions of years and left you those spectacular layered walls. Rivers cut V-shaped canyons. That is what rivers do.

But the valley between Ridgway and Ouray is not a V. It is a U. The walls rise nearly vertical for hundreds of feet and then the valley floor spreads wide and relatively flat. The valley bottom is spacious. The walls are sheer. This shape has one explanation: ice.

During the Pleistocene epoch, which lasted from approximately 2.6 million years ago to around 11,700 years ago, global temperatures were on average significantly cooler than today. In the high mountains of Colorado, this manifested as glaciation. Not the continental ice sheets of Greenland and Antarctica, but valley glaciers: rivers of ice that originated in high mountain basins, grew fat on winter snowfall that never fully melted in summer, and flowed slowly downhill under their own weight.

The mechanics of a valley glacier are different from a river in almost every important way. A river cuts with the energy of moving water, and it cuts primarily at the bottom of its channel, carving deeper but not much wider. A glacier is a solid, and it moves as a solid. It fills the entire valley cross-section from wall to wall, and it presses against every surface it touches with thousands of pounds of ice per square foot. It plucks rock from the valley walls. It grinds rock into flour. It pushes house-sized boulders as casually as a current pushes a leaf. And because it fills the valley from wall to wall, it erodes not just the bottom but the walls, widening and deepening simultaneously. The result is not a V but a U.

The San Juan glaciers were substantial. At their maximum extent, roughly 20,000 years ago during the Last Glacial Maximum, valley glaciers in the San Juans extended significant distances below their source basins. The Uncompahgre glacier extended well into what is now the upper portion of the valley. In some San Juan valleys, glacier ice was estimated to have been 800 to 1,000 feet thick. That is nearly 200 feet taller than the Eiffel Tower, occupying the valley as a solid moving wall of ice.

Now look up. High above the tree line, near the peaks, you will see the cirques. A cirque is a bowl carved into a mountainside by a glacier's origin zone. The upper portion of a glacier is where snow accumulates and compacts into ice. The ice does not simply flow forward at its origin; it also rotates in a process called rotational sliding, which means the base of the accumulation zone scours backward and downward into the mountain. Over tens of thousands of years, this rotational erosion carves a concave amphitheater-shaped bowl into the mountain face. When the glacier eventually melts away, the cirque remains. It is the birthplace of the glacier, preserved in rock.

Many cirques hold small lakes called tarns. These are among the most beautiful bodies of water in the high country: small, intensely blue, cradled in rock bowls above treeline, fed by snowmelt and glacial meltwater. The rock around them is striated, scratched by the moving ice that once filled the cirque to the brim.

Where two glaciers carved cirques on opposite sides of a ridge, they eroded toward each other until only a knife-edged spine of rock remained between them. This is an arete, and the high ridgelines of the San Juans are full of them. They look impossibly sharp, like the serrated edge of a bread knife frozen at thousands of feet of elevation. The Sneffels Range to your west, which you have been watching since Ridgway, has aretes visible on its skyline.

Now look for the hanging valleys. This is perhaps the most dramatic glacial feature visible from the road today. A hanging valley is a side valley that was carved by a smaller, tributary glacier, while the main valley was simultaneously carved by a much larger glacier. Because the main glacier was larger and more erosive, it deepened the main valley faster than the tributary glacier could keep up. When both glaciers melted, the side valley was left with its floor hundreds of feet above the main valley floor. The tributary valley simply does not connect to the main valley at the same elevation anymore; it is stranded in the air.

The result is what you will see today: waterfalls that appear to emerge from nowhere, pouring off a cliff face with no canyon visible above them. The cascade is the drainage from the hanging valley, making the leap to the main valley floor. It is not a spring and it is not a broken pipeline. It is geography, left over from the ice age. Some of the most spectacular waterfalls in the San Juans pour from hanging valleys. Box Canyon Falls in Ouray, which we will discuss separately, has its own geological story, but many of the other cascades visible from US-550 are hanging valley drainage.

One more glacial feature to understand before you reach Ouray: the moraine. When a glacier advances, it pushes debris in front of it and to its sides. Rocks, boulders, gravel, and glacial flour all accumulate at the margins of the moving ice. When the glacier retreats, all of this material is left in place, deposited in long ridges called moraines. A terminal moraine marks the furthest advance of the glacier. A lateral moraine marks the sides of the glacier's path. Recessional moraines mark pause points during retreat.

Moraines can be surprisingly large. Some terminal moraines in the San Juans are hundreds of feet high and stretch for miles. They are the physical signature of the glacier's maximum reach, left in the landscape like a tide line on a beach, marking how far the ice came before it turned around.

The glacial episode in the San Juans was not a single event. There were multiple glacial advances and retreats over the course of the Pleistocene. Evidence for at least four distinct glacial periods exists in the Colorado high country, though the exact number and timing are subjects of ongoing geological research. The landscape you are driving through today reflects primarily the last two major glacial advances, Bull Lake glaciation roughly 130,000 years ago and Pinedale glaciation ending roughly 12,000 years ago. Pinedale is the one that carved most of what you see most clearly: the sharpest cirques, the most dramatic aretes, the freshest moraines.

Twelve thousand years is not very long in geological time. These glaciers retreated from this landscape roughly when the first permanent human settlements appeared in the Fertile Crescent of the Middle East. The San Juan glaciation and the invention of agriculture happened at approximately the same moment in earth's history.

The ice is gone, but it is not entirely absent. At the highest elevations, permanent snowfields persist through summer in sheltered north-facing cirques. These are not glaciers in the technical sense; they do not move and they do not significantly erode. But they are ice that survives the summer, a remnant of the Pleistocene world that made this valley. Climate researchers have been documenting the shrinkage of these permanent snowfields over recent decades. The cirques that once generated glaciers are still there, still bowl-shaped, still perfectly designed to collect and hold snow. What they are missing is enough cold to turn that snow into something that moves.

What you are driving through today is the aftermath of an ice age, readable in the shape of every valley wall, every sharp ridge, every suspended waterfall.
