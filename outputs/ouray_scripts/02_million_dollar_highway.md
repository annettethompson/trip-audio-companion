# Chapter 02: The Road That Shouldn't Exist — The Million Dollar Highway
*Estimated duration: 10.0 minutes | Word count: 1,375*

There are guardrails on most of it now. That single sentence requires emphasis because when you see what the road traverses, the guardrails will seem optimistic.

US Highway 550 between Ouray and Silverton is known as the Million Dollar Highway, and it crosses Red Mountain Pass at 11,018 feet through terrain that most engineers in the 19th century would have considered impossible to build a road through. The canyon walls drop hundreds of feet directly below the pavement. The switchbacks are tight enough that large vehicles must make three-point turns. The roadbed in places is blasted directly into a nearly vertical cliff face, and the cliff is below you as often as it is above you.

The stretch from Ouray south to Silverton is 24 miles. The road climbs 3,300 feet from Ouray, which sits at 7,792 feet, to Red Mountain Pass at 11,018 feet, and then descends into the Animas River valley where Silverton sits at 9,318 feet. Most of that elevation gain happens in the first 12 miles, which means the grade is serious and the switchbacks are relentless.

The man who made this road was Otto Mears, who has some claim to being the most consequential infrastructure builder in Colorado history. Mears was born in Russia in 1840, orphaned young, made his way to the United States as a teenager, and ended up in Colorado during the 1860s, where he recognized before almost anyone else that the key to wealth in the mountain mining economy was not the mines themselves but the roads between them. He began building toll roads through the San Juan Mountains starting in the 1870s, and by the time he was done, he had built more than 300 miles of wagon roads over terrain that seemed to resist roads entirely.

The toll road between Ouray and Silverton was his most audacious project. The route through the canyon below Red Mountain Pass had been used by pack animals for years, but it was barely a trail. Mears graded it into a wagon road in the early 1880s, allowing ore wagons to carry the silver and gold coming out of the Red Mountain district north to Ouray and ultimately to the smelters. It was a toll road, and he charged by weight: so much per ton of ore, so much per wagon, so much per animal. The toll road was enormously profitable. The Red Mountain district was producing enormous quantities of silver ore in the 1880s and 1890s, and every pound of it went out over Mears's road.

There are several theories about why it is called the Million Dollar Highway. The most poetic is that when the road was paved in the 1920s using fill material sourced from old mine tailings and glacial gravel in the canyon, the gravel happened to contain enough residual gold and silver to be worth a million dollars. This story is almost certainly apocryphal, but it is the one people love. A more prosaic explanation is that the paving project in the mid-1920s cost approximately a million dollars, which was a staggering sum for a 24-mile road at that time. A third theory is that an early tourist, looking at the scenery and the engineering together, simply declared the whole thing worth a million dollars. All three versions get told in Ouray, sometimes in the same conversation.

The paving in 1924 transformed the road from a wagon track to something automobiles could navigate, but it did not make them comfortable navigating it. The pavement was narrow, the switchbacks were the same switchbacks, and the drop was the same drop. What changed was the type of vehicle attempting it. Horses had instincts that served them well on mountain trails. Automobiles had none.

Now let's talk about what you are actually looking at on the canyon walls as you drive.

As you leave Ouray heading south, the canyon walls initially expose the Precambrian basement rocks, the ancient granites and metamorphic rocks that underlie everything in the San Juans. These are among the oldest rocks you will see on this trip, formed more than 1.7 billion years ago. They are dark grey to pink, massive, fractured in great vertical joints. They form the towering walls of the Uncompahgre Canyon immediately south of Ouray, where the canyon is so narrow and the walls so high that the town is shaded for part of the afternoon.

As you climb, the Precambrian rocks give way to volcanic sequences, the thick piles of lava flows, tuff, and breccia erupted from the San Juan volcanic field during the Oligocene and Miocene epochs, roughly 35 to 20 million years ago. These are the rocks that made the mining wealth possible. The volcanic rocks that filled and overflowed the ancient calderas contained the hydrothermal ore deposits that the miners were chasing. The specific mechanism of ore deposition, the hydrothermal fluids rising through fractures and precipitating minerals as they cool, operates the same way described in previous episodes. What is new here is the scale of ore production and the specific minerals involved.

The ore bodies of the Red Mountain district, the cluster of mines that Otto Mears's road served, were primarily silver with significant gold and some copper. The silver was bound in minerals like argentite, native silver, and various silver sulfosalts. The gold occurred both native and associated with pyrite. The copper came as chalcopyrite and bornite, the peacock ore that old miners loved for its iridescent blue-purple surface.

The colors in the rock change as you climb. Pay attention to the cliff faces and roadcuts. Some sections show rust red and orange, which we will explain in detail in the Red Mountain chapter. Other sections show green and blue-green, the characteristic colors of copper mineral alteration: malachite is bright green, azurite is vivid blue, and chrysocolla ranges from pale blue to blue-green. Where you see green in the rock, copper was present. The mountain knows what it held.

One engineering detail worth understanding: significant portions of the Million Dollar Highway cling to the cliff face not because the road builders found a natural ledge there, but because they blasted and built the ledge. The road is in many places supported on fill material on its downhill side, which means the pavement you are driving on is sitting on built-up material that extends down the cliff face. This was accomplished with substantial amounts of dynamite and an extraordinary amount of hand labor, much of it performed by workers hanging from ropes on the cliff faces to drill and load the blast holes.

The snowsheds you will pass, if you drive this route in winter or very early spring, are another engineering response to the geography. Red Mountain Pass receives enormous snowfall, and avalanche paths cross the highway in several places. The snowsheds are permanent concrete and steel roofs built over the road to carry the avalanche debris over the pavement rather than burying it. They are not beautiful, but they represent the engineering acknowledgment that in this landscape, you do not fight the mountain. You duck.

The road receives avalanche path closures multiple times each winter. Colorado Department of Transportation forecasters monitor the snowpack and trigger artificial avalanche releases to control when slides happen. The Million Dollar Highway is, in this sense, actively managed terrain. The mountain is not tame; it is watched.

Twenty-four miles of road between Ouray and Silverton represents more than a century of continuous effort to maintain access across terrain that does not want to be accessed. Drive it slowly. It is worth the time.
