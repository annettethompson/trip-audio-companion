# Chapter 11: The Second Morning — Ridgway Reservoir at Dawn
*Estimated duration: 7.0 minutes | Word count: 960*

There is a particular quality to a second morning at a place you have already been.

The first morning, you are still locating yourself: which direction is north, where does the coffee come from, what is that mountain. The first morning has the slightly frantic quality of arrival, even when arrival was the day before. You are still catching up to where you are.

The second morning, you are already there.

You know which direction is north because the light came from behind the Sneffels Range and you watched it move. You know where the coffee comes from. You do not need to ask about the mountain; you spent yesterday looking at it and it spent yesterday looking at you and you have reached the informal understanding that passes for familiarity between a person and a 14,000-foot peak.

Orvis Hot Springs, where you have been soaking, is a modest property. It does not dress itself up. The pools are functional, the water is genuinely therapeutic, and the people who come to it tend to be people who found it by looking rather than people who ended up there because a hotel app surfaced it. On the second morning, if you are staying on the grounds, there is a ritual available to anyone who wakes early enough: the pools before anyone else gets there.

The Orvis pools in the early morning, in late May, with the air temperature in the 30s and the water temperature in the upper 90s to low 100s, are a specific experience. The steam lifts straight up in still air. The mountains to the south are grey-blue silhouettes against a sky that is just beginning to differentiate from black into dark blue. The Sneffels Range is still fully dark. The water holds you, and the cold air above the water is on your face, and nothing is moving except the steam.

This is the contrast cycling that Orvis's multiple pools are designed for, but the specific version available only at dawn has the full sky as part of the contrast. The cold is the open air. The warmth is the spring-fed pool. The ceiling is the stars going out one by one as the sky lightens from the east.

The light that reaches Ridgway from the east in late May comes over a lower ridgeline than the high peaks to the south, which means it arrives earlier than it might seem like it should. By 6 AM in late May, there is enough light to see color: the first brown-grey of the willow thickets along the Uncompahgre, the sage going silver, the outline of the Dallas Divide to the northwest becoming distinct. The San Juans to the south hold their darkness longer. They are in shadow while the valley is lit, because the peaks are so high and the shadows they cast so long. The transition from dark to light on the high peaks happens quickly once it starts: a line of illumination appears on the highest snow fields first, then moves rapidly down the flanks, and by 7 AM the full range is lit and the show is over.

Ridgway Reservoir sits two miles north of town on the Uncompahgre River, a state park with camping, boating access, and shoreline walking. The reservoir was built in 1987 and holds the runoff from the Uncompahgre watershed that comes off the high San Juans to the south. In late May, the reservoir is filling rapidly from snowmelt, and the water level is rising daily. The shoreline geology at Ridgway State Park exposes the Cutler Formation sediments that this part of the Uncompahgre valley sits on, the same formation described in the Day 2 episode: the red conglomerate and sandstone deposited in alluvial fans off the ancient Uncompahgre highland.

Walk to the reservoir shore at dawn and you have the full composition: the red rock shoreline, the water surface reflecting the pink-orange light of early morning, and the San Juans in the background going through their light show. The Uncompahgre Plateau to the west is lit first. The San Juans to the south catch the light last. In between, in the valley, the cottonwood and willow along the river are already fully leafed in late May, and the yellow-green of new cottonwood leaves in early morning light is a specific color that does not exist anywhere else at any other time of year.

The pronghorn that use the open sagebrush country around Ridgway were covered in the Day 2 episode. On the second morning, what you can do that you could not do on the first morning is look for them without looking for them. You know they use the sagebrush flats east of the reservoir. You know the movement pattern. You have the binoculars out of the bag without having to think about it.

There is a philosophical argument, made by people who think seriously about travel, that the second day at any place is the real day. The first day you arrive. The second day you are there. What you see in Ridgway on this second morning, the specific quality of light on that specific range at that specific angle, is available only to people who stayed. The people who drove through yesterday saw the same mountains. They did not see this.

You are soaking in the same water the Tabeguache people soaked in, though the pool structure is modern. You are looking at the same peaks they looked at. The San Juans were not different then: same height, same rock, same angle of morning light. What changes is the person receiving it. On a second morning, you receive it slightly differently. You are slightly less a visitor.

The drive south begins soon enough. Let it wait a few minutes while the light finishes its work on the Sneffels Range.
