# Chapter 08: Animals at the Edge — High Elevation Wildlife
*Estimated duration: 10.5 minutes | Word count: 1,445*

Above treeline, the rules change. The animals that live there year-round are not simply the same species as the valley animals, pushed uphill. They are specialists, each one carrying adaptations so specific to the alpine environment that removing them from it would be removing what makes them what they are.

Four animals define the high San Juans in late May. Two of them you might walk right past without noticing. One makes a sound that startles every person who hears it the first time. And one is watching everything from above with vision sharp enough to spot a rabbit from half a mile.

The pika.

If you have not met a pika, you are about to become obsessed with one. Ochotona princeps is a small, round-bodied mammal that lives in boulder fields called talus slopes at and above treeline. It is not a rodent, despite looking like a round grey mouse with very short ears. It is a lagomorph, more closely related to rabbits and hares than to mice, and it has been living in alpine talus since the Pleistocene. The pika that occupies the same boulder field as the glaciers that carved the cirque above it: both are relicts of a colder world.

The pika weighs roughly a quarter of a pound. It is seven to eight inches long, with thick greyish-brown fur, tiny round ears, and no visible tail. It lives in the spaces between boulders, which provide insulation from wind and cold and refuge from predators. You may hear it before you see it: the call is a loud, high-pitched nasal bleat, almost a honk, that sounds nothing like any rodent or bird. It is startling and distinctive and will carry across a talus field clearly even in wind. Pikas call to defend territory and to alert neighboring pikas to predators. They are vocal animals in a landscape that otherwise seems silent.

Here is what makes a pika remarkable: it does not hibernate. Every other small mammal in the alpine survives the winter through some form of dormancy, torpor, or hibernation. The pika stays awake all winter, burrowed in its talus, surviving on food it has stored. All summer, the pika cuts and dries vegetation, then carries the cured plant material into sheltered parts of its talus home in piles called haypiles. These are not small operations. A single pika's haypile can weigh a pound or more, representing months of sustained foraging and hauling by an animal that itself weighs only four ounces. Pikas have been documented carrying loads equivalent to a third of their own body weight.

The haypiling behavior is driven by an absolute physiological constraint: the pika cannot thermoregulate in heat. Its core body temperature regulation fails at air temperatures above approximately 77 degrees Fahrenheit. If ambient temperatures stay above that threshold for too long, the pika cannot survive. This is why it lives in talus at high elevation, where the ambient temperature and the microclimate deep in the boulders stay cool even on warm days. The talus acts as a natural cooling system.

Now understand the problem: as temperatures warm, the lower-elevation talus fields where pikas once lived become too hot in summer. Pika populations have been documented retreating to higher elevations or disappearing entirely from lower-elevation sites across the Great Basin and parts of Colorado. Unlike many animals that can shift their range northward as climate warms, the pika cannot simply move north; it needs altitude, not just latitude, and altitude is finite. There is no higher boulder field above the highest boulder field. The pika is a sentinel species for climate change precisely because its thermal tolerance is so narrow and its habitat so specific. Where pikas are disappearing, researchers know the summer temperatures in the talus have crossed a threshold.

Watch for pikas on any talus slope you stop at above treeline. They are active during daylight, especially in morning and late afternoon. The call will find you before the animal does.

The yellow-bellied marmot is the large mammal version of the alpine talus zone. Marmota flaviventris weighs five to eleven pounds, is eighteen to twenty-eight inches long, and looks like a woodchuck that has been living well on high-alpine meadow vegetation for many years. The belly is, as advertised, yellowish-orange to rust-colored. The marmot's alarm call is a loud, piercing whistle that carries far across open alpine terrain, which is why it is also called the whistle pig in some parts of its range.

Marmots live in extensive burrow systems in talus and at the talus-meadow interface. A colony consists of a dominant male, several females, and their young, sharing a network of burrows with multiple entrances. The social structure includes a sentry system: at least one animal watches for predators at any given time, and the alarm whistle from the sentry sends the colony underground immediately. The sentinel and the colony behavior are so consistent that predators adapted to hunt marmots, the golden eagle and the coyote primarily, have learned to approach from upwind and low angles, reducing the time between detection and contact.

Marmots hibernate. This is genuine hibernation, not the light torpor of some animals: core body temperature drops to within a few degrees of air temperature, heart rate drops from roughly 100 beats per minute to 3 to 4 beats per minute, and metabolic rate falls to roughly 2% of active levels. A marmot enters hibernation in September or October and emerges in April or May, surviving entirely on fat reserves accumulated during the summer. In high-elevation populations, the active season may be only four to five months long. Marmots must accumulate enough fat in four months to survive seven or eight months of hibernation. This is why marmot foraging behavior in summer is relentless: they eat almost continuously.

In late May, marmots are just emerging from hibernation and are intensely focused on eating. You will see them at meadow edges, in boulder fields with adjacent meadows, often sitting upright on a prominent rock surveying the surroundings before putting their head down to graze. They are not shy.

The white-tailed ptarmigan is Colorado's only true alpine bird, and it may be the most perfectly camouflaged vertebrate in North America. Lagopus leucura is a grouse-family bird, sixteen to nineteen inches long, living year-round in the alpine tundra above treeline. It does not migrate. In winter, the ptarmigan is pure white, including its feathers and the feathering on its legs and feet. In summer, it is mottled brown, grey, and white. The transition between these plumages happens in late spring, and by late May ptarmigan are often in intermediate plumage: patches of white remaining from winter, patches of brown emerging for summer. The camouflage is extraordinary in both forms. Against snow, a white ptarmigan is invisible. Against tundra rocks and plants, the summer plumage matches the grey-brown-white mottling of the substrate almost exactly.

Ptarmigan do not flush easily. They rely on their camouflage rather than flight as their primary predator defense. You may almost step on one before it moves. When it does move, the flight is low and brief, settling again into another patch of tundra where it immediately becomes invisible again. Look for them in alpine tundra meadows and on the rocky slopes above them. They travel in small flocks through the winter and break into pairs for breeding in May and June.

The golden eagle, Aquila chrysaetos, is the apex predator over this entire landscape. With a wingspan of six to eight feet and the ability to reach 150 miles per hour in a diving stoop, the golden eagle is adapted to hunting large open terrain, and the San Juan alpine provides exactly that. Golden eagles hunt marmots, pikas, rabbits, ground squirrels, and occasionally larger prey including young deer and domestic lambs. They also scavenge.

Golden eagle territories are measured in tens of square miles. A nesting pair holds a territory year-round, and the territory size reflects the productivity of the landscape: in the alpine, where prey density is lower than in lowland habitat, territories are larger. The same pair of eagles may hunt across the entire visible sky above Red Mountain Pass.

Look up. If something large is soaring without flapping, with flat or slightly bowed wings and a proportionally small head, and the flight is a sustained effortless glide using the updrafts on the south-facing slopes, that is an eagle. The golden eagle's golden nape, the warm brown-gold feathers on the back of the head, are visible at close range but require binoculars from road distance.
