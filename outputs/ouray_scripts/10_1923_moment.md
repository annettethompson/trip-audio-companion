# Chapter 10: The 1923 Moment — First Cars on Red Mountain Pass
*Estimated duration: 8.5 minutes | Word count: 1,165*

The year is 1921. A man named Harvey Thornton, a hardware merchant from Pueblo, Colorado, has driven his 1919 Ford Model T from Ouray to the base of the switchbacks on the south side of town, and now he is sitting in the car with the engine running and his wife in the passenger seat and his two teenage children in the back and he is looking at the road ahead of him and he is not moving.

His wife asks why they are stopped.

He says he is thinking.

She asks what he is thinking about.

He says he is thinking about the road.

The road ahead of him is Otto Mears's graded toll road, built for freight wagons in the 1880s, partially improved in the early 20th century, but still fundamentally a mountain track designed for horse-drawn vehicles. The switchbacks in the canyon south of Ouray are tight, cut into a cliff face, with the inner wall rising immediately to the traveler's left and the outer edge dropping without barrier to the canyon floor, several hundred feet below. The road is wide enough for a wagon and a team of horses, which means it is barely wide enough for a Model T and leaves almost nothing for error.

Harvey is thinking about the outer edge.

His children, who have never been on this road, are not thinking about the outer edge. They have read in the Denver papers about the spectacular scenery of the San Juan Mountains. They have heard that Silverton is a real mining town with real mines still operating. They have seen, in a magazine, photographs of the Red Mountains. They want to go.

Harvey's wife, who has driven with Harvey before, is also thinking about the outer edge.

The tension in this moment was not unique to the Thornton family. It was repeated hundreds, then thousands of times across the 1910s and 1920s as automobile tourism in the Colorado mountains exploded. The Lincoln Highway had opened the country to cross-country motoring. The automobile industry was producing cars that middle-class families could afford and maintain. The National Park Service was established in 1916 and actively promoting scenic tourism by automobile. Americans who had never traveled further than a train depot from their home towns were suddenly driving into terrain that the horse-and-wagon era had treated as industrial transport routes.

The problem was that those routes were designed for horses, and horses were categorically different from automobiles in one critical respect: instinct.

A horse on a mountain switchback knows where it is. The horse can feel the slope beneath its hooves. It can sense the drop to its right or left. Its weight distribution adjusts automatically to shifting terrain. When a horse approaches an outer edge on a steep switchback, it moves away from the edge without instruction, because the instinct for self-preservation operates through the animal's feet and legs before the animal's conscious mind engages. Experienced mountain horses did not fall off switchbacks. They were on switchbacks every working day and they navigated them correctly because they could not afford not to.

An automobile in 1921 has no such instinct. The automobile is a machine, and its mechanical relationship to the terrain is mediated through the driver's hands on the steering wheel and feet on the pedals. The driver cannot feel the slope. The driver cannot sense the drop. The driver is receiving visual information about a road surface that may be 20 feet in front of the front wheels, and that information arrives too slowly, through the brain, to trigger the kind of immediate physical compensation a horse makes automatically.

Early automobile drivers on mountain roads made specific, documented errors. They cut the inside of switchbacks too tight and got the rear wheels too close to the outer edge while correcting. They misjudged the width of the road and met an oncoming freight wagon with insufficient clearance. They stalled on steep grades and then had to restart on the grade, rolling backward while working the hand brake and the throttle simultaneously, with the outer edge directly behind them. Some drivers froze. Road workers and later paid guides documented cases of drivers who simply stopped mid-switchback, unable to identify what combination of steering and throttle and brake would move them forward without moving them over the edge, and who had to be physically talked through each move or physically escorted, the guide walking beside the car with a hand on the door, calling out instructions.

There was also the water problem. The Model T's cooling system was marginal on level roads. On steep sustained grades at altitude, the engine overheated. Experienced mountain drivers carried extra water and stopped regularly to let the radiator cool. The water sources on Red Mountain Pass in the early 1920s were mine drainage channels and the river in the canyon bottom, reachable in places by scrambling down the slope from the road. Some drivers ran their engine dry on the grade, required a tow, and had to be pulled to the top or back to the bottom by horses, which cost money and dignity.

The early automobile experience of Red Mountain Pass was marketed in precisely the way that any generation markets its genuinely dangerous entertainments: as adventure. The Denver newspapers ran accounts of motorists who had made the passage. The Colorado Automobile Association published routes and conditions. By 1921 and 1922, a small but growing number of automobiles were crossing the pass seasonally, and the passage was acquiring a reputation as one of the great drives in the American West, meaning one of the most terrifying.

The 1920s also produced the first systematic documentation of automobile accidents on mountain roads in Colorado. The Colorado State Highway Commission, established in 1909, began collecting accident data in the early 1920s as automobile traffic increased sharply. Red Mountain Pass and other high San Juan passes appear in these records with accounts of vehicles off the road, vehicles stuck on grades, and occasional fatalities. The accidents were taken seriously by the highway engineers as data, not as random misfortune. The conclusion drawn from the accident data was that the existing road design, adequate for horse and wagon, was inadequate for automobiles, specifically because the road did not have the width, the curve radius, or the grading that would allow a mechanical vehicle to navigate predictably.

The 1924 paving of the Million Dollar Highway, covered in Chapter 02, was one response. Better road design, wider cuts, improved switchback geometry, was the medium-term solution. Guardrails came later still, and in some sections of the road they remain minimal or absent even today.

Harvey Thornton, in his 1919 Ford in 1921, eventually put the car in gear and drove the switchbacks. He wrote about it in a letter to his brother that survives in the Colorado State Historical Society collection. He described the experience as the most frightened he had been since the war. His wife's hands, he wrote, were white on the door handle the entire way up. His son, who had been enthusiastic about the adventure, became very quiet when he could see over the outer edge.

At the top, Harvey stopped the car and looked back at the road he had just climbed. He wrote that from above, looking down at the switchbacks, the road looked like something drawn on paper by a child: thin, winding, arbitrary. He could not reconcile the line he was looking at with the experience of being on it. He wrote: I do not know that I would do it again. And then, in the next sentence: I am already planning to come back in August and try the road to Silverton.

That is the spirit of the Million Dollar Highway. It has always asked more than it seemed reasonable to give, and people have always said yes.
