# Chapter 12: Come Back For This — Ouray, Silverton, and the Full San Juan Loop
*Estimated duration: 7.0 minutes | Word count: 960*

You have seen a fraction of what this corner of Colorado offers. Here is the practical guide to what you missed and why each item justifies a return trip on its own terms.

**The San Juan Skyway: completing the full loop**

What you drove today, the Million Dollar Highway from Ridgway to Silverton, is one segment of a 236-mile National Scenic Byway called the San Juan Skyway. The full loop adds Colorado 145 from Silverton west and north through Dolores Canyon to Telluride and Rico, then north through Norwood and Placerville to Ridgway, completing the circle. This route takes a minimum of two days driven without stops; a proper exploration takes four to five days.

The Telluride section, accessible via CO-145 or by the free Telluride gondola from Mountain Village, is a different character from Silverton: a ski resort town with genuine Victorian bones underneath the resort infrastructure, set in a box canyon with a spectacular hanging valley waterfall visible at the canyon head. Bridal Veil Falls, which pours off the canyon wall above Telluride, is the tallest free-falling waterfall in Colorado at approximately 365 feet. The historic powerhouse at the base of the falls, built in 1907 to power the Smuggler-Union Mine, is a National Historic Landmark and is still connected to the grid.

Rico, between Telluride and Silverton on CO-145, is the ghost town that did not quite die. A handful of permanent residents, a general store, and the hulking remains of the Victoria Mill on the hillside above town. The drive through the Dolores canyon section on CO-145 is geologically spectacular: deep canyon through Permian and Triassic sedimentary rocks in a palate of red, orange, and buff. This is the part of the San Juan Skyway that most visitors skip. Do not skip it.

**Silverton in winter**

Silverton Ski Area, reached by hiking or snowcat from town, operates on a permit system and is one of the most extreme lift-served ski areas in North America: no marked runs, no groomed terrain, no ski patrol on the mountain, one lift serving expert and extreme terrain only. Silverton in winter is also genuinely isolated. When the passes close, the town's only road connection is south to Durango on US-550. The population of roughly 700 year-round residents manages its own infrastructure, its own power, its own everything. The two primary restaurants in winter operate on a schedule that suits the locals and adjusts for weather. Reservations are strongly recommended for anyone arriving mid-week in January. The Shady Lady Saloon in the Grand Imperial Hotel does not take reservations and does not need to.

Best season to return for winter Silverton: January through March for maximum snow and genuine winter isolation. Note that Red Mountain Pass closes periodically for avalanche control; check CDOT road conditions before driving from Ouray.

**Ouray ice climbing: the season and logistics**

The Ouray Ice Park, mentioned briefly in the Day 2 episode, operates from late November through mid-March, depending on freezing conditions. The park uses an artificial ice-making system, irrigating the canyon walls of the Uncompahgre Gorge below town to grow ice climbs ranging from beginner-appropriate WI2 to extremely demanding WI5+. Admission to watch is free. Renting equipment and hiring an instruction guide for a half-day beginner session is available through the Ouray Mountain Sports or Basecamp Ouray, and rates in 2025 were in the $200 to $350 range for a private half-day guide. Book guides in advance during peak ice season; February is the busiest month. The ice festival held each year in January draws top international ice climbers and includes clinics open to beginners.

Best access month: February for maximum ice development and the ice festival. Third week of January specifically for the festival if clinics are the goal.

**Specific dining in Ouray and Silverton**

In Ouray: Maggie's Kitchen on Main Street is the breakfast standard, known for locally sourced eggs and baked goods, opens at 7:30 AM and runs out of cinnamon rolls by 9 AM on weekends. Bon Ton Restaurant, in the historic St. Elmo Hotel, is the dinner choice, operating since 1898 with a wine list and menu calibrated for the end of a long day in the mountains. The St. Elmo bar is the best bar in Ouray.

In Silverton: Brown Bear Cafe on Greene Street is the breakfast and lunch operation with the best biscuits and gravy in the San Juans, with minimal controversy about this claim. For dinner, the Handlebars Food and Saloon is the working restaurant of the town, not the tourist one, with pool tables, local beer, and a menu that feeds people who actually live there. The Silverton Brewery, a few blocks off Greene Street, brews small-batch beers calibrated to 9,300 feet of elevation, which is not a gimmick: alcohol hits harder at altitude and they account for it in their session ales.

**The Bear Creek National Recreation Trail**

This trail leaves Ouray from the south end of town and climbs into the canyon carved by Bear Creek, gaining approximately 2,200 feet over 5 miles to Engineer Pass at 12,800 feet. The first two miles along the Bear Creek shelf trail are suitable for fit day hikers; the upper section requires solid route-finding and should not be attempted in afternoon thunderstorm season without an early start. The trail passes multiple historic mine sites and offers views back down into the Ouray canyon that are different from anything visible from the Million Dollar Highway. Early July through mid-September is the best season; late May conditions may include snow on the upper trail.

Return trip planning recommendation: dedicate at least four nights. Two in Ouray, two in Silverton. Drive the full San Juan Skyway loop on a dedicated day, not as a sidebar. The scale rewards time.
