# Chapter 05: The Town the Mountain Kept — Silverton
*Estimated duration: 9.5 minutes | Word count: 1,305*

Silverton should not exist as a living town. By any economic logic of the late 20th century, a small isolated community at 9,318 feet, accessible by one road that closes in bad winters and surrounded by collapsed mines, should have emptied out decades ago. The people left the mining towns all over the San Juans. Eureka is a ghost. Animas Forks is a ghost. Rico has a few hundred residents and a faint pulse. But Silverton has around 700 year-round residents and a summer that looks, if you squint, like 1893.

Understanding why Silverton survived requires understanding why it existed, which requires understanding the geology of why the ore was there in the first place.

Silverton sits in an elongated valley at the northern rim of the Silverton caldera, which is a separate structure from the La Garita caldera described in the Day 2 episode. The Silverton caldera collapsed roughly 27 to 28 million years ago, a significant but smaller eruption than La Garita, and it left a collapsed structure roughly 12 miles across. After the main collapse, volcanic activity continued with intrusions of magma into and around the caldera margin, producing high-temperature hydrothermal systems that deposited extraordinary concentrations of silver, gold, copper, zinc, and lead along the fracture systems surrounding the caldera.

This is the ore that the mining camps of the San Juans were chasing: caldera-margin hydrothermal deposits, concentrated by geology into ore grades that justified hauling men and machinery to elevations above 10,000 feet. The Animas River canyon, which connects Silverton to the outside world, runs directly through the caldera margin. The mines were not placed arbitrarily in the mountains; they were placed where the geology told anyone with enough training to look that ore would be found.

Silverton was officially platted in 1874, though miners had been working the district since at least 1871. The town that grew up in the valley was initially connected to the outside world only by pack trail. Ouray, to the north, was reachable by Otto Mears's toll road after the early 1880s. Durango, to the south, was reachable only by a grueling 50-mile trail through the Animas canyon that required fording the river multiple times. The isolation was complete enough that for the first decade of serious mining, everything that came into Silverton arrived on the back of an animal, and everything that went out arrived on the back of an animal.

The Denver and Rio Grande Railway changed this in 1882. The railroad reached Silverton from the south, following the Animas River canyon through terrain that the engineers initially considered impassable. The canyon walls in some sections are so close to vertical and so high that the survey crews reportedly worked by rope from the canyon rim. The railroad solved the isolation problem in a single construction season, and the decade from 1882 to 1893 was Silverton's boom: population several thousand, multiple smelters, hotels, saloons, newspapers, opera house.

The Silver Panic of 1893, when the federal government repealed the Sherman Silver Purchase Act and the price of silver collapsed, hit Silverton as it hit every silver mining town in Colorado. The population dropped sharply. Many mines closed. The smaller camps around Silverton became ghosts. But Silverton did not fully empty, because gold was also present in the ore and gold did not collapse, and because the railroad kept running, and because the town had enough physical and institutional infrastructure to survive the bad years.

The building stock that survived the collapse of the silver economy is what you see today on Greene Street, Silverton's main thoroughfare. The brick commercial buildings, the board-fronted structures, the ornate Victorian hotel facades: most of them date to the 1880s and 1890s, built during the boom and kept standing because the population that remained did not have money to tear them down and build something new. Poverty, in this case, was preservation. The town that could not afford to modernize in 1900 became the living museum that draws visitors in 2026.

Silverton's survival into the modern era depended on tourism, and tourism depended substantially on the narrow gauge railroad that connected Silverton to Durango. The Durango and Silverton Narrow Gauge Railroad, the D&SNG, is the successor to the Denver and Rio Grande's original Silverton branch line, and it still runs its historic steam and diesel trains through the Animas canyon between Durango and Silverton from May through October. We will talk about the D&SNG in much more detail in the Durango episode, because the train's history, its engineering, the canyon it traverses, and the experience of riding it deserve their own extended treatment. For now, know this: if you see a narrow gauge steam train pulling into Silverton while you are there, it has come 45 miles from Durango through a canyon that has no road, carrying passengers on a trip that the mountains spent a century trying to prevent from being possible. The locomotive is burning coal, the steam is real, and the engineering of the Animas canyon line is one of the great achievements of 19th-century railroad construction in the American West. What you should notice in Silverton today: the scale is honest. The streets are wide, built for ore wagons, not for tourist flow. The buildings are proportioned for a working mining town, not for boutique retail. Many of the storefronts on Greene Street contain businesses that a mining-era merchant would recognize: hardware, lumber, food supply, outfitting. The restaurants that operate are not performance dining; they are functional establishments that feed people who have come a long way and need to get back before dark.

The County Courthouse on Reese Street is 1906. The Grand Imperial Hotel on Greene Street is 1882 and still operating. The Bent Elbow restaurant in the hotel basement is one of the oldest continuously operating saloons in Colorado. The mining headframes visible on the hillsides above town are not props; they are the original equipment, left in place when the mines shut and kept standing because removal costs money that nobody spent.

There is a quality to Silverton that most preserved mining towns do not quite have. It is not a museum that has been dressed to look like the past. It is a place where the past simply did not leave, because the present never arrived with enough force to push it out. You are welcome to decide whether that is a triumph or a tragedy. The mountain that made it possible has no opinion.
