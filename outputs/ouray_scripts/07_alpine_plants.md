# Chapter 07: Flowers at the Edge of the World — Alpine Plants and Treeline
*Estimated duration: 9.0 minutes | Word count: 1,240*

The question is deceptively simple: why do trees stop? You are climbing through dense spruce and fir forest, and then abruptly, approximately at 11,500 feet in the San Juans, the trees end. Not because of soil. Not because of water. Not because of space. The trees stop because of wind and cold and the length of the growing season, and the boundary where they stop is called treeline, and it is one of the most ecologically distinct zones in North America.

Understanding treeline requires understanding what a tree actually is, structurally. A tree is a tall, rigid, upright plant. That form works beautifully in protected environments where the main priorities are reaching sunlight and competing with neighbors. But at the upper limits of forest, wind is the dominant environmental force, and wind kills trees by desiccation: it strips water from the needles and branches faster than the roots can replace it, especially in winter when the soil is frozen and water uptake is impossible regardless of the root system's effort. The needles on the windward side of a tree above treeline die first, giving alpine trees their characteristic one-sided, flag-tree form, all growth on the downwind side, the upwind side stripped bare.

At the very edge of the tree line, in a zone ecologists call Krummholz, the German word for crooked or twisted wood, spruce and fir trees grow in ways that look nothing like trees. They hug the ground, spreading horizontally instead of vertically. They grow behind boulders and into the spaces between rocks where wind is deflected. They form low, dense mats, in some places less than a foot tall, their trunks gnarled and prostrate. These are old trees. A Krummholz spruce mat that looks like a low shrub may be 200 years old or more. The survival strategy has worked; it just does not look like survival in any form you would immediately recognize.

Above the Krummholz, the alpine tundra begins, and this is where the flowers are.

In late May at elevations between 10,000 and 12,000 feet, the alpine flowering season is just beginning, timed precisely to the retreat of the snowpack. The alpine plants you will see today and tomorrow are running a compressed calendar: they may have as few as 60 to 80 frost-free days to germinate, grow, flower, set seed, and prepare for winter. They manage this not by evolving simpler life cycles but by front-loading their investment. Many alpine plants spend the majority of their summer storing carbohydrates underground, in root systems and rhizomes that can persist for decades or longer. The flower is funded by reserves built over years. What you see blooming in late May at 11,000 feet is the expenditure of an investment made over many seasons.

The glacier lily, Erythronium grandiflorum, is the alpine flower you are most likely to see today and the one most strongly associated with the retreat of the snowpack. It does not wait for the snow to be gone; it blooms at the edge of the melting snowbank, sometimes emerging through the last inch of snow. The flower is bright yellow, nodding, with reflexed petals, somewhat resembling a shooting star. The name refers to the habit of the corm, the underground storage organ, which is white and smooth like the body of a glacier lily in a very generous imagination. The plant is one to two feet tall, the flowers striking enough to be visible from a moving vehicle if you slow down near late snowbanks. Deer, elk, and black bears eat the corms, which are nutritionally dense after a winter of starch accumulation.

Sky pilot, Polemonium viscosum, is one of the signature flowers of the true alpine zone above treeline. It grows in rocky soil, often on exposed ridgelines and in high cirque basins, at elevations from 11,000 feet to above 13,000 feet. The flowers are deep blue to violet, tubular, clustered in tight heads, and intensely fragrant: the smell is often described as skunky or musky, which in alpine plant terms is not a deterrent but an advertisement. The pollinator is the queen bumblebee, Bombus balteatus, one of the few bumblebee species capable of thermoregulating in alpine conditions. The scent attracts her. The match is a co-evolutionary partnership maintained at the edge of where either species can survive.

Indian paintbrush, Castilleja species, is a parasite. Not in the social sense, but in the botanical one. Paintbrush is hemi-parasitic: it photosynthesizes and produces some of its own energy, but it also sends haustoria, specialized root structures, into the roots of neighboring plants and extracts water and nutrients. It tends to parasitize grasses, sedges, and sagebrush. The advantage of partial parasitism at altitude is significant: the paintbrush does not have to invest the full energetic cost of nutrient acquisition in thin, rocky, nutrient-poor alpine soils. Someone else paid part of the bill.

The colors of paintbrush range from brilliant orange-red to pink to yellow to nearly white, depending on species. The showy part is not the flower itself but the bracts, modified leaves that surround the flower, which hold their color long after the small tubular flowers within them have finished. This gives paintbrush a longer visible season than its actual flowering window.

Shooting star, Dodecatheon pulchellum, blooms in the same late-May window as the glacier lily, preferring moist meadows near snowmelt streams. The reflexed petals and downward-facing flower give it the form of a miniature comet or dart, which is exactly what the common name describes. The color is deep magenta to pink with a yellow-and-dark base at the center. It is found in clusters, often many plants growing together in a wet alpine meadow, and when you find a patch of them it looks like something that belongs on another planet, which in a sense it does: the high alpine is one of the most extreme environments where flowering plants persist.

Alpine avens, Geum rossii, is the plant that makes the high alpine tundra look yellow-green and lawn-like from a distance. It is a low-growing mat-former with small yellow flowers and deeply lobed, pinnate leaves, and it is one of the dominant plants in alpine turf communities above treeline. It can look from a distance like grass, but it is a rose-family plant, related to the strawberry, and its mats are extraordinarily dense, built up over decades by vegetative spread. Alpine avens turf is essentially a continuous living carpet, each plant interconnected, and it stabilizes the thin rocky soils against wind erosion. Walk on it with care if you walk on it at all.

The Krummholz zone and the true alpine tundra above it are among the slowest-recovering ecosystems in the world. A boot print in an alpine tundra plant community can persist for five to ten years. The growing season is too short and the plants too slow to fill gaps. What looks like a shortcut across a tundra meadow is years of damage, concentrated in a moment.

The alpine blooming season in the San Juans runs roughly from late May at lower elevations to late July at the highest. The glaciers that carved the cirques that shelter the snowbanks that water the meadows that feed the flowers: the chain is intact. The flowers are the visible end of a geological story that began 20,000 years ago.
