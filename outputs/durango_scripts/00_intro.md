# Chapter 00: The Day the Mountains Give Way
*Estimated duration: 2.8 minutes | Word count: 385*

Something is changing outside your windows right now, and it happened so gradually you may have missed the exact moment. The volcanic San Juans, which have been your ceiling and your compass for the past two days, are falling back. The peaks are still there in your rearview mirror, still dusted with snow in late May, still enormous. But ahead of you the land is opening. The sky is getting wider. The rock is getting softer.

You are dropping out of one of the most geologically violent landscapes in North America into one of its most ancient and quietly layered ones. Behind you: fire, eruption, the remains of calderas so large they could swallow entire mountain ranges. Ahead of you: an ocean floor. Not a recent one. A Cretaceous ocean, 75 million years gone, compressed into coal seams and shale beds and a sandstone mesa that kept an entire civilization alive for a thousand years.

Durango sits where these two worlds meet. It is a town that was built in a single season, placed on the map by a railroad president who looked at the mouth of this valley and calculated that this was exactly the right place to smelt ore, build yards, and create an industrial hub from scratch. The mountains fed it with silver and gold. The valley floor fed it with coal. The river fed it with water and, eventually, with travelers who came just to ride the train.

And the train. The train is still here. The same locomotives built in 1923 that Durango ordered from a factory in Schenectady, New York are still running today, still burning coal, still throwing smoke into the canyon as they climb the 45 miles north toward Silverton. If you visit the Durango station and put your hand on the side of a K-28 engine before it departs, you are touching something that has been making this run since the year your great-grandparents were young.

Today you will soak at a resort on the Animas River that draws from 32 distinct minerals. You will learn why the trees here smell like vanilla. You will hear the full story of a people who negotiated something remarkable: they stayed. You will understand why an EPA crew with good intentions turned a river bright orange on a Tuesday morning in August 2015, and what the river looks like today.

The geology chapter is about to go geologically backwards. Welcome to the San Juan Basin.
