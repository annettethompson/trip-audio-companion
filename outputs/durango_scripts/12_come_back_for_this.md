You have seen Durango from the road. Here is what requires a return trip, a dedicated visit, more time than a transit day allows.

Mesa Verde National Park. Thirty-five miles west of Durango on US-160. This is not a stop you can do justice to in an afternoon, and it deserves its own full day or, ideally, two. The park covers over 52,000 acres and contains approximately 600 cliff dwellings, including the largest in North America. Cliff Palace alone has 150 rooms and 23 kivas. Access to the major dwellings requires ranger-led tours, which fill quickly and require advance reservation on recreation.gov. The park is open year-round, but the best access to the cliff dwelling tours runs from late May through October. If you are visiting in peak season, book the ranger tours before you book your lodging. They sell out weeks in advance. Budget a minimum of 5 hours for a meaningful visit; a full day is better.

The Weminuche Wilderness. At 488,000 acres, the Weminuche is the largest wilderness area in Colorado. It begins immediately east of Silverton and stretches to Wolf Creek Pass. The D&SNG drops backpackers at remote trailheads along the Animas gorge that have no road access, making this one of the few wilderness destinations in the American West where you access the backcountry by steam locomotive. The Needleton and Elk Park stops are the standard entry points for the Colorado Trail and the Continental Divide Trail segments through the Needle Mountains. June through September is the hiking season; early July typically allows passage across most high routes after the snowpack releases. Permits are required for some backcountry zones.

Animas River whitewater. The Animas through Durango is a Class II river suitable for recreational floaters in the summer. The stretch from Baker's Bridge north of Durango into town includes Class III and IV rapids that attract intermediate to advanced kayakers. Mild to Wild Rafting, Durango Rivertrippers, and several other outfitters run guided half-day and full-day trips from the Durango put-in. Late May through July is the peak season, when snowmelt keeps the river high and fast. By August the flow drops and the rapids mellow. For experienced whitewater kayakers, the Class IV Upper Animas from Silverton to Rockwood is a multi-day technical run requiring shuttle logistics that most outfitters can help arrange.

The D&SNG if you have not ridden it. There is no practical way to fully experience this railroad from a car. The canyon section between Durango and Silverton is only accessible by train or by foot on the Weminuche Wilderness trail network. The full round trip from Durango to Silverton and back takes approximately 9 hours including the Silverton layover. The railroad offers a Cascade Canyon Winter Train, a shorter out-and-back running November through May, if you visit outside the summer season. Reservations through durangotrain.com, several weeks in advance for summer departures.

Durango Mountain Resort, called Purgatory by locals, sits 25 miles north of Durango on US-550. In winter it offers 1,600 acres of skiing across 11 lifts, with a vertical drop of 1,604 feet. The ski season typically runs December through early April. In summer, Purgatory converts to a mountain bike park with lift-served downhill trails and a beginner bike skills area. The proximity to Durango makes it accessible as a day trip from town, and the resort has on-mountain lodging for winter stays.

The Strater Hotel, at 699 Main Avenue in downtown Durango, was built in 1887 and is still operating as a full-service historic hotel. The ragtime piano bar in the Diamond Belle Saloon runs most evenings and is the kind of thing that is either exactly what you want or not, but you should know it exists. The hotel's Victorian architecture and the fact that it has been continuously operating for nearly 140 years make it worth seeing even if you do not stay there.

For a meal, Ore House at 147 East College Drive has been a Durango institution since 1972, specializing in Colorado beef cooked over mesquite with a wine list that takes itself seriously. For something more casual, Steamworks Brewing at 801 East Second Avenue is a brewpub in a converted industrial building with solid food and a rotating beer selection that leans toward the hoppier end of the spectrum. Both are within walking distance of downtown.

The Old Fort campus of Fort Lewis College, west of Durango on US-160 above Hesperus, is worth a slow drive-through. The stone buildings from the 1880s are on the National Register of Historic Places and are maintained in excellent condition. There is no fee for driving through the historic grounds, and the view of the La Plata Mountains to the south from the mesa is exceptional on a clear morning.

Durango rewards time. It is a genuine mountain town with a layered history, a working railroad, a university, a strong outdoor recreation culture, and a food and lodging scene that has matured well beyond its mining camp origins. A dedicated return, with the D&SNG round trip, a full day at Mesa Verde, and an evening at the Strater, would be a very good few days.
