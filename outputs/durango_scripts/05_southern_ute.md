On Day 2, you heard the story of the Tabeguache Ute, the band known to Americans as the Uncompahgre, and their forced removal in 1881 from the Uncompahgre Valley. That story ended at the Utah border: a people who had lived in the high valleys of the San Juans since before the Spanish arrived, compelled in a matter of days to walk away from their homeland and onto a reservation in northeastern Utah.

That story is not the only one. There is a second Ute story in this landscape, and it unfolds differently. Because some of the Ute bands, the ones living farthest south, negotiated. And some of them stayed.

The Southern Utes are not a single band but a confederation of three: the Mouache, the Capote, and the Weeminuche. Their territory was distinct from the Tabeguache homeland to the north. While the Uncompahgre and White River Utes occupied the high valleys and plateau country of what is now central and western Colorado, the Southern Utes lived farther south, in the drainages feeding the San Juan River: the Animas, the La Plata, the Piedra, the Pine. This was their country. The river you have been traveling alongside since Silverton is a Southern Ute river.

The pressure that removed the northern bands also targeted the Southern Utes. In 1880, all the Confederated Bands of Ute Indians in Colorado occupied a reservation of approximately 12 million acres in the western part of the state. The government wanted that land. The silver and gold discoveries in the San Juans created enormous political pressure to open Ute territory to mining and settlement. After the events of 1879 at the White River Agency, which gave politicians the pretext they needed, the campaign to remove all Colorado Utes moved quickly.

The Uncompahgre and White River Utes were removed by force in 1881, as you heard in the Ridgway chapter. But when the same pressure came for the Southern Utes, something different happened. The chairman of the commission overseeing the removals made a practical judgment that moving the Southern Utes would be counterproductive. They were geographically separate from the northern bands. They had different relationships with surrounding communities. Moving them would be more difficult and less politically necessary than moving the more prominent northern bands.

The result was a negotiated outcome that preserved a narrow strip of Southern Ute territory in southwestern Colorado. It was dramatically reduced from what it had been, compressed into a 110-mile-long band of land running east to west through what are now La Plata, Archuleta, and Montezuma counties, immediately south and east of Durango. But it was land. In Colorado. Which is where they had always been.

The distinction between this outcome and the Uncompahgre outcome is not a minor one. For the Tabeguache, removal meant relocation to Utah, separation from the landscape that defined their culture, and a forced beginning in unfamiliar terrain with insufficient resources. For the Southern Utes, the outcome was confinement and loss, significant loss, but in a place they knew, along rivers they had names for, within a landscape that held their history.

The Southern Ute Indian Tribe today is a federally recognized nation with a reservation of approximately 1,059 square miles. Its land base includes portions of the Animas River valley. The tribal headquarters is in Ignacio, Colorado, about 25 miles southeast of Durango. The tribe has used the natural resources of its land, particularly natural gas and oil deposits in the San Juan Basin, to build an economic base that has generated significant tribal revenues over the past several decades. The Southern Ute Growth Fund, established in the 1990s, has invested those revenues in diversified assets that give the tribe a financial foundation largely independent of federal appropriations.

This is not a story of passive survival. The Southern Utes have been active participants in the legal, political, and economic life of southwestern Colorado throughout the 20th and 21st centuries. They have litigated water rights on the Animas and its tributaries. They have negotiated with federal and state governments over mineral rights. They have invested in businesses, built infrastructure, and maintained cultural programs that keep Southern Ute language and traditions alive for younger generations.

When you drive south of Durango, you are driving through their reservation. The land on either side of the highway, which looks like open rangeland and pinon-juniper hills, is sovereign Southern Ute territory. The Red Willow Energy company, the Sky Ute Casino, the Pino Nuche Puramputs (a Ute phrase meaning "my people's land") community center: all of this is the present expression of a negotiated survival that nearly did not happen.

The Animas River runs through this territory as it has for thousands of years. The Southern Ute called it Semca, "the river of lost souls," a name that may reflect the dangerous quality of the water in flood season or may carry older meanings that the historical record does not fully preserve. The river they called by that name is still running. So are they.
