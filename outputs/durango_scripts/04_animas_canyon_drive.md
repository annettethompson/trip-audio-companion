The drive south from Silverton to Durango on US-550 is one of the more compressed geological tours in the American West. In roughly 45 miles, you pass through rock representing several hundred million years of Earth history, moving from the volcanic San Juans you covered on Day 3 into terrain that tells a completely different story. This chapter is your guide to what you are looking at and when to look.

For the first several miles south of Silverton, the canyon walls are the volcanic rocks you have already been introduced to. Ignimbrites, volcanic breccias, the rust-orange and gray of welded ash layers. The Animas River here is a high mountain stream, fast and cold and narrow, running over a bed of angular volcanic cobbles. The canyon is steep. The train track and the highway compete for the same ledge cut above the river in places where the walls come all the way down to the water.

Watch the canyon walls. Around mile marker 60, roughly 12 miles south of Silverton, the character of the rock begins to change. This is a transition that geologists mark at a place called Bakers Bridge, where a bridge crosses the Animas River and the bedrock of the canyon drops below the river level. What is exposed in the canyon walls above and beyond this point is no longer volcanic rock from the San Juan eruptive episodes covered in the Ridgway episode. You are now looking at Precambrian basement rock, the same ancient foundation discussed in the Glenwood Canyon chapter on Day 1. These are very old rocks, dark and dense and fractured, that predate the volcanic San Juans by well over a billion years.

This transition is not subtle if you know what to look for. The volcanic rock tends to be layered in a way that reflects the flow and deposition of erupted material. The Precambrian rock beneath it is different in texture, more crystalline, more massive, with fracture patterns that reflect deep crustal cooling rather than surface deposition. The color shift can help you: the volcanic sequences in this canyon tend toward grays, tans, and rust-orange, while the Precambrian basement rock is often darker and more uniform.

Below Bakers Bridge the basin sequence begins to build, and you will start seeing a succession of sedimentary formations stacked on top of the basement as the canyon continues south. The San Juan Basin geology chapter explains the full context of what these sedimentary layers represent. Your job on this drive is simply to notice the shift.

The river changes with the rock. In the upper gorge, above Bakers Bridge, the Animas is moving fast through volcanic and metamorphic material, and the water runs clear and cold with the character of a true mountain stream. Below the bridge, as the gradient eases and the valley begins to widen, the river settles. It meanders more. The floodplain appears. The water is still cold but the energy has changed. This is the river beginning its transition from alpine stream to valley river.

Watch the plants. They will tell you where you are almost as clearly as the rock does. At the upper end of the drive, the vegetation is subalpine: Engelmann spruce, subalpine fir, willows along the stream. As you lose elevation and the canyon walls ease back, the spruce disappear and you enter a montane zone: ponderosa pine begins to appear on south-facing slopes, Gambel oak fills the dry hillsides, and the sagebrush you became familiar with around Ridgway returns on the open benches. By the time you reach the outskirts of Durango, the lower slopes carry pinon pine and Utah juniper, the characteristic plant community of the Colorado Plateau at around 6,500 feet.

The moment when the canyon opens fully and Durango's valley becomes visible is one of the more satisfying pieces of geography on this trip. You have been in narrow terrain for days, and suddenly the valley is wide, the Animas has room to move, and a real city appears on the valley floor. The mountains are still visible to the north but they are now the background rather than the foreground. The San Juan Basin geology chapter tells you what is underneath this valley. This drive chapter just asks you to feel the opening, to notice that the sky has gotten bigger and the rock has gone somewhere else.

Keep an eye on any roadcuts as you enter Durango from the north. The darker seams you may see in lighter-colored sedimentary rock are coal, part of the Fruitland Formation described in the geology chapter. The same material that once fueled the Durango smelter and kept the narrow gauge locomotives running is visible in the walls beside the highway. History and geology on the same surface.
