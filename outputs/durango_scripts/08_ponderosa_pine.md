Stop the car for a moment when you find a ponderosa pine. Get out, walk to the tree, press your nose against the deeply furrowed plates of its bark on a warm afternoon, and breathe in. What you will smell is not what you expect from a tree. You will smell vanilla. Or butterscotch. Or something in between, depending on your particular nose, because people tend to perceive one or the other but almost never a blend.

This is real. It is not a trick of expectation or the kind of thing you have to convince yourself you are noticing. The ponderosa pine, Pinus ponderosa, produces volatile terpene compounds in its bark that in warm conditions smell unmistakably of baked goods. The terpenes are not an accident. They serve as a chemical defense against bark beetles and other wood-boring insects, but the effect on human olfaction is a complete surprise the first time it happens.

The smell is most pronounced in mature trees. Ponderosas go through a visible transformation as they age: young trees have dark, almost black bark that is tight and corky. Older trees shed that outer layer to reveal bark the color of orange peel or pale cinnamon, broken into thick interlocking puzzle-piece plates. This transition, which typically happens around 110 years of age, is when the tree enters what foresters call the "yellowbelly" phase. The new bark underneath is where the terpene concentration is highest, and where the vanilla smell is strongest on a warm afternoon with the sun hitting the plates directly.

The ponderosa pine is one of the most widely distributed tree species in western North America. Its range runs from the coast of Oregon and Washington through the inland Northwest, south through the Rockies, and into Mexico. In Colorado it occupies a band of elevation between roughly 5,500 and 9,000 feet, which makes it precisely the tree you are entering as you drop from the subalpine spruce-fir forests of the high San Juans into the montane zone around Durango. At around 6,500 to 7,000 feet, where Durango sits, ponderosa is the dominant tree on dry south-facing slopes and ridgelines.

What defines a ponderosa forest, beyond the smell, is its structure. Old-growth ponderosa forests have a character that is almost parklike, a word that appears consistently in the ecological literature and that captures something real. The trees are large and widely spaced, with high canopies that let light reach the ground. The understory is open: bunch grasses, occasional shrubs, patches of bare soil. You can walk through a healthy ponderosa forest and see for several hundred yards in any direction. It does not feel enclosed or dark.

This structure is the product of fire.

Ponderosa pine forests evolved with frequent, low-intensity fire. Before European settlement and the subsequent suppression of fire, the ponderosa forests of the southern Rockies burned on roughly a 5 to 10-year cycle. These were not catastrophic stand-replacing fires. They were ground fires that moved quickly through the dry grass and needle litter beneath the trees, killing small seedlings, cleaning out accumulated fuel, and leaving the mature trees standing. The mature trees survived because of their bark: old-growth ponderosa bark is up to six inches thick in places, an effective insulator that protects the living cambium layer from ground fire temperatures.

The fire ecology of the ponderosa is a closed loop of adaptation. The tree produces thick bark that survives fire. Fire kills competing species that do not have thick bark. The killed competitors decompose and enrich the soil for the surviving ponderosas. The open parklike structure that results from this cycle allows wind to move through the forest, which dries the surface litter, which becomes fuel for the next fire. The forest maintains its own conditions.

Fire suppression, practiced throughout the 20th century, has altered this system significantly. In areas where fire has been excluded for decades, ponderosa forests have filled in with younger, smaller trees that would not have survived in the historical fire cycle. The accumulated fuel load in these denser forests can support fires that burn hot enough to kill even the mature ponderosas, converting a fire-adapted forest to a landscape of standing dead trees. Forest managers across the Southwest are now using prescribed fire and mechanical thinning to try to restore the historical structure.

At Durango's elevation, the ponderosa zone transitions gradually downward into the pinon-juniper woodland, the low-elevation pairing of pinon pine and Utah juniper that defines the plateau country around Durango below about 6,500 feet. The pinon, Pinus edulis, is a much smaller tree than the ponderosa, rarely exceeding 35 feet, with a round crown and needles in bundles of two. Its ecological role and the animals that depend on it are covered in the wildlife chapter. What is worth noting here is the vegetational gradient you are moving through: subalpine spruce-fir gives way to montane ponderosa-Gambel oak gives way to semi-arid pinon-juniper. Each zone is visible from the road as you descend, each one cooler and drier than the one above.

In the hot dry summer of the Southern Rockies, the ponderosa's scent intensifies. The sun heats the bark plates, the terpenes volatilize, and the entire hillside can carry the smell of something warm and sweet. It is one of the more unexpected sensory experiences in a landscape that tends to announce itself through dramatic geology and elevation rather than aromatics.

Find a tree. Press your nose to the bark. The mountain smells like a bakery.
