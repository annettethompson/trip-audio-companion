Elk.

If you have been paying attention to the open meadows and forest edges since dropping out of the high country, there is a reasonable chance you have already seen them. The San Juan elk herd is one of the largest in Colorado, which itself carries the largest elk population of any state in the country at over 290,000 animals. The San Juan herd ranges from the Animas River east to Wolf Creek Pass and contains roughly 17,000 to 19,000 animals. They are here, and in late May they are doing something very specific.

The cows are calving. In the montane meadows and forest edges between about 7,000 and 9,000 feet, in the exact elevation band you are moving through, cows have separated from the main herd to give birth to single calves, usually in a dense stand of vegetation near a water source. The calves are born spotted, camouflaged in a way that makes them nearly invisible when lying still in dappled light. In the first days of life, a calf's primary defense is precisely that: lying still and not smelling like anything significant while its mother grazes nearby.

The bulls are at lower elevations right now and will remain there until the heat of summer pushes them up. In late May, the bulls are carrying velvet, the dense, blood-vessel-rich covering of skin that grows the antlers from scratch every single year. Antlers are the fastest-growing tissue of any mammal. In summer, a bull elk's antlers can grow as much as an inch per day. The velvet nourishes that growth by delivering a constant supply of oxygen and minerals. If you see a bull with what looks like fuzzy, fat antler beams in a warm reddish tone, that is the velvet stage, and what you are witnessing is bone forming in real time.

The Animas River corridor, with its mix of open riparian meadow, dense willow thickets, and transition into ponderosa pine forest, is good late-spring elk habitat. Scan the edges of forest openings in the early morning and late afternoon. Elk move to open terrain at dawn and dusk and retreat to forest cover in the middle of the day. If you are staying near the Animas north of Durango, the meadows on either side of the highway in the evening hours are worth a slow scan.

Now: the bears.

Spring is the riskiest season for a black bear, which is counterintuitive until you understand what has just happened to them. A Colorado black bear will have spent between five and seven months in a den, not fully unconscious but in a metabolic state of reduced heart rate and body temperature that allows survival without food or water. When they emerge in March or April, depending on elevation and the individual, they are substantially lighter than they were in fall and their digestive systems have essentially been offline.

The first weeks out of the den are what biologists call walking hibernation. The bear is moving, active, visible, but still metabolically depressed. It eats primarily new vegetation: emerging grass shoots, the first forbs of spring. This is not the high-calorie food a bear needs for recovery. It is the gentle restart to a digestive system that needs re-acclimation before it can handle meat or dense plant material.

By May, the walking hibernation phase is ending. The bears are hungry in a more urgent way. Natural food is not yet abundant at the elevations where they are searching. This is the period of highest human-bear conflict in Colorado, as bears that need calories begin to investigate human-associated food sources: garbage cans, bird feeders, improperly stored camping food, the easily accessible calories that a hungry bear will absolutely pursue if given the opportunity.

The Animas River corridor is good black bear habitat year-round. The riparian forest provides cover. The adjacent slopes carry the berries and acorns that bears will eat in summer and fall to build fat reserves for the next denning season. In late May, a bear using the Animas corridor is probably working the riparian margin, turning over rocks and logs for invertebrates, digging for roots, and staying alert to anything that smells like food.

If you encounter a black bear on this trip, which is not unlikely, the appropriate response is to remain calm, stand your ground or back away slowly, make yourself large, and make noise. Black bears are not predatory toward humans under normal circumstances. They are food-focused and easily dissuaded. Running is counterproductive, as it triggers a prey-pursuit instinct. Most human-bear encounters in Colorado resolve themselves quickly when the human holds position and the bear decides the interaction is not worth the energy.

Now look up at the cliff faces along the Animas River canyon. Look for smooth, gray, gourd-shaped structures plastered against the rock in dense clusters. Those are cliff swallow nests, and if you are traveling in May or June, the builders are there too, a constant aerial circus of small brown-and-rust-colored birds darting at high speed in and out of a colony that may contain several hundred nests.

The cliff swallow, Petrochelidon pyrrhonota, is the most colonial swallow in the world. Colonies regularly contain 200 to 1,000 nests. Individual nests are constructed entirely from mud pellets, 900 to 1,200 of them per nest, carried one at a time in the bird's bill from a mud source, sometimes up to a few miles away. The completed nest is gourd-shaped: a round chamber attached to the cliff face by a narrowed entrance tunnel. The walls are 0.2 to 0.7 inches thick, just enough insulation to maintain a stable temperature for the eggs inside.

The engineering involved is genuinely remarkable. Each pellet is pressed into position with a rapid shaking motion of the bill. The mud must be the right consistency, enough clay to hold shape, enough sand to resist cracking as it dries. The cliff swallow selects its mud source carefully, preferring deposits near water where the particle size is appropriate. A pair building a new nest will make several thousand trips to the mud source before the structure is complete.

In the pinon-juniper woodland around Durango, two more birds deserve attention: the pinon jay and the Townsend's solitaire. The pinon jay, Gymnorhinus cyanocephalus, has a relationship with the pinon pine that goes beyond habitat preference. The jay is the primary seed disperser for pinon pine. It harvests seeds before they fully ripen, carries them in a specialized esophageal pouch, and caches them in the ground in groups of 4 to 6. The seeds that are not recovered germinate to become new pinon pines. The jay's memory is extraordinary but not perfect, and the unclaimed caches are how the forest regenerates. The relationship is obligate in a real sense: without the pinon jay, the pinon pine forest would regenerate far more slowly. Without the pinon pine, the jay has no food base. Each requires the other.

The Townsend's solitaire is a winter specialist in juniper woodlands. In warm months, solitaires are birds of high-elevation conifer forests, often above treeline. In winter, they move down to the juniper zone and eat almost nothing but juniper berries for months. A single solitaire will establish and defend a winter territory around a particularly productive juniper, aggressively excluding other birds from what it considers its personal food supply. Come spring, the berries are gone and the solitaire returns to the high country.
