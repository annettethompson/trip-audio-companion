Eight miles north of downtown Durango, on the west bank of the Animas River, is a hot springs facility that has been on this site in one form or another since the late 19th century. For most of its history it operated as Trimble Hot Springs, named for a local landowner. In 2019, new ownership purchased the property, undertook extensive renovations, and renamed it Durango Hot Springs Resort and Spa.

The name change and the renovation together represent a deliberate repositioning. The old Trimble Hot Springs had the informal, community-swimming-pool character that many Colorado hot springs facilities share: concrete pools, modest amenities, the social democratic atmosphere of a place where ranchers and tourists soak alongside each other. The new Durango Hot Springs has moved in a different direction: more than 40 distinct water features, private cedar soaking tubs inspired by Japanese Ofuro bathing culture, a full spa offering massages and body treatments, and a 25-meter resort-style swimming pool alongside the mineral pools.

It is worth being honest about what this means for different visitors. If you found the primitive, unstructured quality of Orvis Hot Springs two nights ago to be its appeal, the aesthetic of Durango Hot Springs will feel quite different. This is a curated experience rather than a pastoral one. There is a design sensibility at work in how the pools are arranged and how the facility is landscaped. Whether you find that enhancing or intrusive depends on what you came for.

What the springs themselves offer is substantial. The water draws from a geothermal source and contains 32 naturally occurring minerals, including lithium, magnesium, potassium, sodium, calcium, chloride, fluoride, sulfate, silicon, iron, and manganese, among others. The combination is distinct from the lithium-dominant chemistry of Orvis, which was the focus of the Ridgway chapter, and from the sodium bicarbonate profile of the Day 1 springs. At Durango, the 32-mineral blend reflects the different geology of the basin setting and the particular aquifer feeding the source.

One thing immediately noticeable: there is no sulfur smell. Many Colorado hot springs have that characteristic rotten-egg quality that comes from hydrogen sulfide dissolved in the water, a byproduct of certain bacterial processes and mineral interactions in the subsurface. Durango Hot Springs does not. The water is odorless in the way that a mineral spring can be when the dominant chemistry runs toward calcium and magnesium salts rather than sulfides. For people who find the sulfur smell of other springs off-putting, this can be a significant practical difference.

Pool temperatures run from 99 to 112 degrees Fahrenheit across the 32 different mineral soaking areas, organized into family-accessible and adult-only sections. The warmest pools, at 112 degrees, are hot enough to require acclimatization and are not suitable for extended immersion, particularly at altitude. The contrast cycling that the Orvis chapter discussed as a physiological tool is available here as well, with two dedicated cold plunges alongside the warm pools. The shift between hot and cold stimulates the same circulatory response regardless of the facility's aesthetic character.

The facility also operates what it calls an AquaGen water treatment system, which it describes as unique in the world of hot springs. The system infuses all pools with nano-meter and micro-meter oxygen bubbles, which the company says provide antimicrobial properties without introducing chemical additives. Independent scientific literature on oxygen nanobubble technology exists in the context of water treatment and aquaculture, though its application in hot springs contexts is newer. The claimed result is water that stays clean without the chlorine or bromine that most conventional pools use, while maintaining a mineral composition closer to the natural source.

The setting deserves mention separately from the facility. The Animas River runs along the eastern edge of the property. In late May, the river is typically running high with snowmelt, the water green-gray and fast, audible from the soaking areas. The mountain backdrop to the north includes the same volcanic peaks you have been driving through for three days, now visible in the distance across the valley. Cottonwood trees in new leaf line the riverbank. The contrast between the hot mineralized water and the cold mountain river fifty feet away creates a sensory context that no amount of facility design can manufacture.

This is also, depending on where you are in the trip, likely your last soak. Three hot springs in four days is not a typical travel itinerary. What you may find, if you pay attention, is that the experience of the third soak is different from the first: more informed, more attuned to what the water is doing and why, less novel and more deliberate. The body knows, after several exposures, what to expect from thermal immersion. The relaxation response, covered in the Day 1 physiology chapter, comes faster and goes deeper when the nervous system has already learned the pattern.

The facility opens daily at 9 AM and closes at 10 PM. Reservations are strongly recommended, particularly in peak season. They frequently reach capacity on weekends.
