Most towns in the American West grew the way weeds grow: opportunistically, where conditions happened to favor them. A miner found gold near a creek. A store opened to serve the miners. A saloon opened to serve the store owner and the miners. A post office arrived. A church arrived. Over a generation, a town took shape from a combination of accident, geography, and human hunger.

Durango is not that kind of town. Durango was designed.

In 1880, General William Jackson Palmer, president of the Denver and Rio Grande Railroad, had a problem. The silver and gold coming out of the San Juan Mountains was being processed at smelters in distant locations, which meant that ore was being transported over rough terrain to facilities that were not ideally positioned for the future growth of the mining district. Palmer wanted a smelter town in the Animas Valley, south of the mountains, close enough to the ore sources to receive material efficiently by rail but low enough in elevation to operate year-round.

He also had a local obstacle. Animas City, a small settlement just north of where Durango now sits, was the existing community in the valley. The Denver and Rio Grande approached Animas City about establishing a depot. The negotiations failed. The city wanted terms the railroad was unwilling to meet.

Palmer's response was to simply create a new town. In September 1880, he formed an entity called the Durango Trust, together with associates William Bell and John Porter, and purchased land on the Animas River approximately two miles south of Animas City. The site was surveyed and platted. Lots went on sale at prices ranging from $50 to $100. In the first two days of sales, buyers purchased more than $15,000 worth of parcels. People understood that the railroad was coming and that the railroad meant money.

The name Durango almost certainly came from the Mexican state of Durango, which in turn derived its name from a Basque word for "water town." Palmer had connections to Mexico through business interests, and the Animas River location gave the name an apt resonance. The post office opened on January 3, 1881. By April of that year, the smelter was operational.

John Porter began building the smelter for the New York and San Juan Mining and Smelting Company, another enterprise in which Palmer and Bell held interests. The smelter was the reason for everything else. Coal from the nearby valley would fuel it. Ore from Silverton, once the railroad reached that far north, would feed it. The metals extracted would be shipped east. The railroad yards, the workers' housing, the commercial district, the saloons and hotels and stores, all of it existed in service of the industrial logic at the valley's center.

The Denver and Rio Grande's main line from Alamosa arrived in Durango on August 5, 1881, accompanied by a ceremony large enough to bring the governor of Colorado. The crowd that gathered understood that the ceremonial moment was real: this was the beginning of a different economy. And then, as covered in the railroad chapter, the line north to Silverton was completed the following year.

Durango grew with a speed that still seems startling when you read the numbers. Within one year of its founding, the town had a population of 2,400. Within six years, the Durango smelter was processing one million pounds of silver, lead, gold, and copper annually, employing 300 workers. Animas City, which had refused the railroad's terms, was eventually annexed by its more successful neighbor.

What the rapid growth created was a town with a particular character that you can still see in the architecture of the downtown historic district. Durango was not built incrementally by individual pioneers with whatever materials were at hand. It was built all at once, with railroad money, by a company that intended it to be permanent and profitable. The Victorian commercial buildings along Main Avenue, the Strater Hotel opened in 1887, the smelter district along the river, all of this reflects a planned industrial ambition rather than the organic accumulation of a frontier settlement.

The smelter itself operated until 1930. For fifty years it processed the ore that came down from the San Juan Mountains by rail, and for fifty years the smoke from its stacks was part of the daily atmosphere of the Animas Valley. When the smelter closed, Durango had to reinvent itself economically, a process that has continued through several cycles. Tourism, the outdoor recreation economy, Fort Lewis College, and the sustained popularity of the narrow gauge railroad all became part of what replaced the industrial core.

What remains is a city with a more complex history than it often gets credit for. Durango presents well as a charming mountain town with Victorian architecture and a famous train. What that presentation rests on is a foundation of industrial planning, corporate real estate strategy, and a smelting operation that for five decades consumed the output of hard-rock mines that employed thousands of workers in conditions we would consider unacceptable today. The pretty downtown and the historic railroad are the legible surface of a story that goes considerably deeper.
