The Animas River that you are traveling alongside today is not the same river from bottom to top. It begins at about 12,000 feet in the peaks above Silverton, a cold, fast stream fed by snowmelt and the seepage from old mine workings in the Silverton caldera. By the time it reaches Durango at 6,500 feet, it has dropped more than a mile in elevation, more than doubled in volume from tributary contributions, warmed from near-freezing to something you could put your hand in, and passed through a transition from alpine to montane to semi-arid riparian. It is effectively several rivers in sequence, each adapted to different conditions.

The riparian corridor along the Animas is disproportionately valuable to wildlife relative to its width. In Colorado's semi-arid landscape, the narrow strips of cottonwood and willow gallery forest along rivers and streams support an outsized fraction of the total biodiversity in a given landscape. Most amphibian species in the region, the majority of songbird species, many mammal species, and virtually all aquatic life depend on the riparian zone at some point in their life cycle. The cottonwood-willow system along the Animas, from the willows in the upper canyon to the mature Fremont cottonwood groves in the lower valley, is the living edge of the river.

Beavers are present in the Animas valley and have been recorded actively cutting cottonwoods in the wetland areas north of Durango. The beaver's influence on a river system is disproportionate to its size. A single beaver dam raises the local water table, slows the water's movement, promotes sediment deposition, and creates the still-water conditions that cattails, sedges, and other wetland plants require. Research on beaver-influenced stream systems has found that beaver dams create hydraulic gradients in the streamside subsurface that are 10 to 13 times greater than seasonal flow variation. In the context of a river like the Animas, which runs through a semi-arid landscape that naturally concentrates wildlife around water, beaver activity has landscape-scale effects far beyond what you would expect from a 50-pound rodent.

The aquatic community of the Animas tells a more complicated story. To understand the river's health, ecologists look at its macroinvertebrates: the aquatic insects, crustaceans, and worms that live in the streambed and are visible to the naked eye. Mayflies, stoneflies, and caddisflies are the three orders most sensitive to water quality. They require well-oxygenated, clean water with specific temperature ranges. When you find robust populations of all three in a river, you are looking at a healthy system. When they are absent, the water quality has been compromised.

The Animas has a complicated macroinvertebrate story because the Animas has a complicated water quality story. The river runs through the heart of the Silverton mining district, where hard-rock mining has been depositing heavy metals into streams since the 1870s. Acid mine drainage, the acidic, metal-laden water that seeps from abandoned mine workings, has been entering the Animas and its tributaries for over 150 years. Studies of aquatic invertebrate tissue in the Animas have found elevated levels of zinc, lead, and cadmium accumulated in the insects. They are present but carrying a chemical burden that reflects the mining history of the watershed.

And then there was August 2015.

On August 5, 2015, an EPA crew working at the Gold King Mine north of Silverton accidentally breached a plug of rock and soil that had been holding back a reservoir of contaminated water inside the mine. The blowout released an estimated 3 million gallons of mine waste, carrying heavy metals including cadmium, lead, arsenic, beryllium, zinc, iron, and copper, directly into Cement Creek, a tributary of the Animas River. The contaminated water turned the Animas bright orange as it traveled downstream, reaching the Durango area within 24 hours. The river was closed to all recreational use and Durango halted its water intake from the Animas.

The orange color was striking enough that it circulated in photographs and news broadcasts across the country, making the Gold King spill one of the most visually documented environmental incidents in recent Colorado history. Lead was found at concentrations 100 times the allowable limit. Iron was found at 326 times the limit. The contaminated plume continued downstream into the San Juan River in New Mexico and ultimately reached Lake Powell.

The most significant long-term impacts fell not in Durango but on the Navajo Nation, whose members downstream on the San Juan River in New Mexico, Arizona, and Utah depend on the river for agricultural irrigation. The agricultural losses and water access disruptions caused by the spill affected Navajo farming families whose land borders the river, and those impacts lasted through multiple growing seasons.

The Animas itself recovered faster than many expected, and the explanation is sobering. Studies found that the spill had little measurable long-term effect on the river ecosystem beyond what was already present, largely because the Animas already contained elevated heavy metal concentrations from the 150-year legacy of upstream mining. The baseline was not clean. The Gold King spill was a dramatic, visible intensification of a chronic contamination problem that had been accumulating quietly since before the Durango smelter existed.

One year after the spill, the EPA placed the Silverton mining district on the Superfund list, qualifying the region for federal cleanup funding. The process of remediating abandoned mine drainage in the San Juan Mountains is expected to take decades and billions of dollars. The colorful orange event of 2015 was a single day's version of what has been entering the Animas in smaller quantities every day since the 1870s.

The river recovers season by season. Mayfly hatches occur in winter and spring. Caddisfly and additional mayfly hatches continue through fall. The riparian margin regenerates. The beavers build. The cottonwoods put out new growth. The Animas is not a dead river. It is a river carrying the chemical memory of an industrial era, still working to process what was put into it, still productive enough to support the great blue herons that stand motionless in the shallows and the osprey that still cross the valley, the same birds whose ecology was covered in the Day 1 chapter. The river is doing what rivers do: continuing.
