To reach Silverton in 1923, you took the train.

There was no other reliable way. The road through the Animas gorge that would eventually become part of US-550 did not exist in its modern form. There were wagon tracks in places, rough enough for a determined driver in good weather, but the canyon was the train's domain. If you needed to get to Silverton or come back from it, you went to the Durango depot, bought a ticket, found a seat in one of the narrow green coaches, and waited for the locomotive to build steam.

The year 1923 is significant for the narrow gauge because it is the year the Denver and Rio Grande Western Railroad took delivery of ten new K-28 locomotives from the American Locomotive Works in Schenectady, New York. These were not experimental machines. They were proven narrow gauge workhorses built to the specific demands of the Silverton line: the 3-foot track gauge, the 2.5 percent grade climbing out of Durango, the 45-degree curves in the canyon, the thin air above 9,000 feet where a locomotive loses pulling power and the engineer has to know the line's rhythms well enough to compensate.

The K-28s were called the Sport Models by the crews who worked them, a name that reflected their relatively clean proportions compared to the older, bulkier engines they were replacing. They were modern in 1923. They are still in service today.

Stand on the Durango platform in your mind, in May of 1923. The platform is wood. The station building behind you smells of coal smoke and machine oil. The locomotive that has been sitting on the track for the past hour is exhaling small puffs of steam from valves along its side, like a large animal that has not yet decided to move. The narrow coaches alongside the locomotive are painted Pullman green, the standard color of the Denver and Rio Grande. The windows are the kind that open manually, with a leather strap to hold them at the height you want.

The passengers assembling around you are a specific sample of the world the Silverton branch connected. A mining engineer in a canvas jacket with a leather satchel is checking papers against a list, organizing his week before the whistle blows. A merchant carrying dry goods samples is talking with the station agent about delayed freight from the previous week. A family, the mother in a traveling dress, the father with a fedora and a camera, the children in Sunday clothes despite it being Tuesday, is looking at the locomotive with the children's specific mixture of excitement and anxiety. The family is here for the ride. Even in 1923, people came to the Silverton train specifically to see the canyon.

And there was the hardware of mining still present in the freight car attached at the rear: drill bits, blasting supplies, replacement machinery parts. The economics of Silverton in 1923 were complicated. The great silver boom of the 1880s had peaked and crashed with the Silver Panic of 1893, thirty years earlier. Some mines were still operating, pulling gold rather than silver in many cases, working lower-grade ore than the bonanza years. The railroad was still making the run because the mine operators still needed the supply chain and the workers still needed passage. But the freight manifest in 1923 was thinner than it had been in 1893.

The whistle. The bell. The conductor's call. The locomotive begins to move with a sound that is part mechanical clang and part deep rhythmic exhalation, the exhaust strokes of a reciprocating steam engine at low speed, each one audible, each one feeling slightly improbable, as if the machine were deciding in real time whether to continue the effort. The coaches creak and sway on the narrow track. Coal smoke drifts back through the open windows, and the smell is specific and permanent: hot iron, soft coal combustion, a faint sweetness from the lubricating oil on the valve gear.

The canyon begins within minutes of leaving Durango. The river appears below the track, closer in places than is comfortable to contemplate, running fast and green in the gorge. The walls rise. The ledge cuts, where blasting crews in the 1880s carved the roadbed out of solid rock directly above the river, are visible as you cross them, a few feet of ballasted track between the canyon wall on one side and a drop to the Animas on the other. The passengers from 1923 who had not made this run before gripped their armrests.

The journey from Durango to Silverton took three to four hours in 1923, depending on stops and conditions. There was a stop at Cascade Canyon for water. There were occasions when snow or rock debris required a crew to clear the track before the train could proceed. In spring, as now, the Animas ran high with snowmelt and the sound of the river was constant through the open windows, competing with the locomotive's exhaust.

The passengers from 1923 who were making this run for the first time were entering the same gorge you can enter today, under the same kind of locomotive power, on the same track gauge, looking at the same canyon walls. The 1923 Silverton was a town in economic transition, its mining heyday thirty years in the past, its population a fraction of the boom years, its future uncertain. The train was its lifeline, its connection to the supply chains and the outside world that kept it viable.

This is the world the Duttons would step into: a working railroad in the mountains, operated with late-industrial technology that was already beginning to feel like a survival from an earlier era, serving a community that depended on it precisely because there was no alternative yet. The automobile would come to Red Mountain Pass within that same decade, as you heard in the Day 3 chapter. But in May of 1923, the K-28 on the Silverton branch was still the way things worked. Coal smoke, canyon walls, three hours through terrain that tolerated the train because there was nowhere else to go, and the arrival in Silverton to the smell of high-altitude air and the sound of a small mountain town doing the work of surviving another season.

Locomotive 473, one of those 1923 K-28s, is still in service on the D&SNG today. If you board the train in Durango, there is a meaningful chance that the engine pulling your car is more than a century old, built in the same year you keep returning to in this series, and running the same route it has always run.
