There is no other railroad in the United States quite like this one. The Durango and Silverton Narrow Gauge Railroad has been running continuously since 1882. Not restored, not revived. Running. The same route. The same gauge of track. Coal-fired steam locomotives burning through the same canyon, climbing the same grade, crossing the same river bends that they have crossed for 144 years. When you stand on the Durango station platform and watch a locomotive roll out, you are not watching a performance of the past. You are watching the past still going.

Understanding why this railroad exists requires going back to 1880, which is when Durango itself was invented. The Denver and Rio Grande Western Railroad was pushing south and west through Colorado, chasing the silver and gold being pulled out of the San Juan Mountains. Silverton was producing ore at a rate that made investors dizzy. The problem was logistics. Silverton sat at 9,318 feet in a high valley completely ringed by mountains. Getting ore out required either pack animals over a pass or a railroad. The railroad was the only solution that could scale to match the volume of silver coming out of the ground.

General William Palmer, president of the Denver and Rio Grande, looked at the topographic problem and solved it by working backward from the ore. If the mountains north of Durango contained silver, and if a railroad could be carved through the Animas River gorge to reach that silver, then what was needed first was a processing facility at the southern end of the line. Durango was founded in September 1880 for exactly that purpose: to be the place where the raw ore arriving by rail would be reduced into metal. The smelter was the entire point. The town was built around it.

Construction on the line to Silverton began in the fall of 1881. The Denver and Rio Grande put approximately 500 workers on the project, primarily Chinese and Irish laborers, and pushed them through the winter. The terrain was extreme. The Animas gorge is not a gentle canyon. It narrows to a slot in places, forces the track onto ledges above the river, and climbs at grades that required the narrow gauge specifically because standard gauge equipment would have been too wide to navigate the curves and too heavy for the bridges. The 3-foot track width that looks quaint today was an engineering decision.

The grading crews reached Silverton in the late spring of 1882. The first construction train rolled into town on July 8. Passenger service started three days later, on July 11, 1882. The total construction cost was $917,303 for 45.2 miles of line, achieved in roughly nine months. By any measure of 19th-century civil engineering, it was a remarkable performance.

For the first several decades, the railroad was a working line. It hauled ore, supplies, mail, and passengers in a mix that reflected the real economic life of the San Juan Mountains. Silverton's mines were producing. The railroad was the artery that kept the high country connected to the world below. There was no road through the Animas gorge equivalent to the railroad. Travelers going to Silverton went by train. Merchants receiving goods received them by train. Miners returning home from a season in the high country came home by train.

By 1923, the year this episode focuses on, the train was still a working enterprise, but the texture had changed. The great silver boom of the 1880s had peaked and receded. Mining was still happening in Silverton, but at a reduced scale. The railroad was carrying a more complicated passenger mix: miners and merchants sharing coaches with tourists who had begun arriving specifically for the ride. The Animas gorge was already understood to be spectacular. Even before road tourism existed, travelers were booking passage just to see it.

That same year, 1923, the Denver and Rio Grande Western Railroad ordered a new batch of locomotives from the American Locomotive Works in Schenectady, New York. Ten engines of a design called the K-28, known among railroad workers as the "Sport Models" for their clean proportions and relatively modern design for a narrow gauge machine. The locomotives were built for the specific demands of the Silverton line: the curves, the grades, the high-altitude operation. They delivered exactly what was needed.

One of those 1923 engines, Locomotive 473, has been in continuous service on this line ever since. Not occasionally. Continuously. It has made the climb through the Animas gorge thousands of times in over a century of operation. When you ride the D&SNG today, there is a measurable chance you are sitting behind an engine that was manufactured the same year your great-grandparent may have been a child.

The railroad came close to ending several times. The mining economy of the San Juans went through cycles of collapse, recovery, and collapse again. By the late 1940s, the freight business had declined to a fraction of what it had once been, and the Denver and Rio Grande was running the Silverton branch at a loss. What saved it was an entirely unexpected source: Hollywood. Filmmakers had discovered the gorge and the train in the late 1940s, and a series of Western films shot on location brought national attention to a line that most people outside southwest Colorado had never heard of. Tourism on the D&SNG began in earnest after World War II.

In 1981, the Denver and Rio Grande Western sold the line. The Durango and Silverton Narrow Gauge Railroad Company was formed to operate it as a heritage railroad, and the operation has continued under private ownership. The American Society of Civil Engineers designated the line a National Historic Civil Engineering Landmark in 1968. Congress later designated it a National Historic Landmark.

What this means in practical terms is that the railroad you can board in Durango today is operating on the original track, with original steam power, on the original timetable in the sense that it still departs in the morning and arrives in Silverton in time for lunch. The cars roll at roughly the same pace they always did. Coal smoke still drifts back through the open gondola cars. The river is visible below the edge of the ledge cuts just as it was in 1882.

There is something worth sitting with about an institution that simply kept going. The Silver Panic of 1893 did not stop it. Two world wars did not stop it. The collapse of the mining economy did not stop it. The Great Depression did not stop it. Floods, snowslides, and the conversion of the rest of the American rail network to diesel and standard gauge did not stop it. The Durango and Silverton is still here in part because the terrain that made it difficult to build also made it impossible to replace. There was no road through that gorge for most of the railroad's history, and by the time the road came, the train had already become something people traveled a long distance specifically to experience.

If you are spending any time in Durango, boarding the D&SNG is not a tourist novelty. It is a direct encounter with the economic logic that created this entire part of southwestern Colorado. The train built Durango, kept Silverton alive, and now runs because people want to sit in a coal-smoke-smelling open car above a river gorge and feel, for three to four hours, what it was like when a narrow gauge steam railroad was the lifeline of the high country.
