About 15 miles west of Durango, on a mesa above the town of Hesperus, stands what used to be a fort. The buildings are still there, absorbed into the campus of Fort Lewis College. Most students walking across the quad today have a general awareness that the college has a complicated history, but the full arc of what happened on that site, from military post to instrument of forced assimilation to institution of higher learning, takes some telling.

Fort Lewis began in 1878, not on the mesa above Hesperus but at Pagosa Springs, 60 miles to the east. The Army established a post there because the Ute reservation boundary ran through the region and the military needed a presence to manage what the government called "the Indian situation." Within two years, the Army decided Pagosa Springs was too far from the main Ute and Navajo populations to be operationally useful. In 1880, the fort was moved to a site on the La Plata River near Hesperus, where the new post, completed in 1881, occupied a strategic position above the valley.

By that point, of course, the Uncompahgre and White River Utes had already been removed, as covered in the Southern Ute chapter and in the Ridgway episode. The military pressure that had originally justified the fort had largely resolved itself, from the government's perspective, through the removals. Fort Lewis served its frontier mission for only a decade before the Army decided it was no longer needed as a combat installation.

In 1891, Fort Lewis was decommissioned. And this is where the history becomes harder to tell cleanly.

The federal government did not abandon the site. Instead, it converted the former fort into an Indian boarding school, part of a nationwide system of institutions whose stated purpose was the assimilation of Native American children into Anglo-American culture. The philosophy guiding these schools is most accurately conveyed by its own proponents: the goal, in the words of the period, was to "kill the Indian and save the man." Children were removed from their families, sometimes forcibly, brought to institutions far from their home communities, prohibited from speaking their languages, required to wear Anglo clothing, and given Anglo names. Their traditional practices were forbidden.

Students from the Navajo, Ute, Sioux, and Apache nations attended Fort Lewis. The conditions at federal boarding schools of this era ranged from merely inadequate to genuinely abusive. Disease was common in the crowded dormitories. Homesickness was treated as a disciplinary problem. Children who spoke their native languages were punished.

Fort Lewis College has publicly reckoned with this history in recent years, commissioning reports and issuing statements acknowledging the harm done on the grounds where the college now stands. History Colorado produced a formal report on the Fort Lewis Indian Boarding School. The college has described this process as an ongoing responsibility rather than a concluded chapter.

The transition from boarding school to college happened in stages. In 1911, the federal government transferred the property and buildings to the state of Colorado to be used as an agricultural and mechanical arts high school. The transfer came with a condition, one that remains legally binding today: the institution must admit Native American students free of tuition, on an equal basis with all other students. That 1911 mandate established a commitment that has been in place for over a century.

Fort Lewis College today is a four-year public liberal arts institution with approximately 3,000 students. It has one of the highest percentages of Native American enrollment of any public college in the United States, regularly enrolling students from more than 200 federally recognized tribal nations. The tuition waiver, which now covers tuition costs but not fees, housing, or other expenses, makes Fort Lewis accessible in a material way to students from nations that have historically been economically marginalized by the very government policies that created institutions like Fort Lewis in the first place.

There is a layer of historical irony in all of this that the college does not avoid. The same site that was used to enforce the destruction of Native American culture is now, by the terms of its own transfer agreement, committed to educating Native American students. The buildings that housed children taken from their families are classrooms where descendants of those same nations study on terms that, while imperfect, represent a legal acknowledgment that something was owed.

If you drive west on US-160 toward Hesperus, you can see the old stone buildings of Fort Lewis on the mesa to the north, a separate campus from the main Fort Lewis College in Durango. They are well-maintained, designated on the National Register of Historic Places, and genuinely beautiful in the way that 19th-century institutional architecture can be. They are also the physical remains of a chapter in American history that shaped the lives of generations of Native American families in this corner of Colorado and beyond.
