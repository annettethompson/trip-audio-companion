# Script Quality Rules — Trip Audio Companion

These rules exist to prevent a single problem: content that was already covered showing up again in a different chapter, either diluted or contradicted. Audio listeners can't flip back to check. If something gets explained twice, it sounds like the writer forgot what they wrote. These rules are the fix.

---

## How to use these rules

Before writing any episode:

1. **List all topics covered in depth in previous episodes.** Those topics are off-limits for full treatment in the new episode. They may be referenced briefly but not re-taught.
2. **Assign each new topic to exactly one home chapter.** Write the assignment list before drafting anything. Every major topic (geological formation, wildlife species, historical event, cultural group, physiological mechanism) gets a home.
3. **Write a topic inventory for each chapter before drafting.** List the 5-8 topics that chapter will cover. Cross-check each one against the home chapter assignments. If a topic belongs to a different chapter, strip it down to one reference sentence.
4. **Do a final pass.** Before submitting any draft, scan every chapter for mentions of topics whose home is elsewhere. Each of those mentions must be one sentence or fewer, with no mechanisms, comparisons, or biological explanation.

---

## Rule 1: Every topic has one "home chapter" — all others get one sentence max

Before writing any episode, create a topic assignment list. Every major topic covered in the episode (geological formation, wildlife species, historical event, cultural group, physiological mechanism, mineral chemistry) gets assigned to exactly one chapter. That is its home chapter.

**In the home chapter:** treat the topic fully. Explain mechanisms, give context, tell the story, use data and comparisons.

**In every other chapter:** one sentence. No explanation. No mechanism. No comparison. No data. Just an orienting reference that acknowledges the topic exists without teaching it.

**Wrong (topic appears in two chapters with explanation):**
- Geology chapter: "Hydrothermal water rises through fault zones because pressure and permeability create pathways for heated groundwater..."
- History chapter: "The springs here form when hydrothermal water rises through fault zones — the same geological process that creates Yellowstone's geysers..."

**Right (topic has a home; other chapter references it):**
- Geology chapter: full hydrothermal mechanism explanation.
- History chapter: "These are the same hydrothermal springs the Ute used for centuries — their geology is covered in the previous section."

---

## Rule 2: The intro teases without explaining

The intro hook is a trailer, not a lecture. It can name facts, features, and stories that will appear in the episode. It cannot explain them.

**What the intro is allowed to do:**
- Name a geological formation ("Precambrian granite that's 1.7 billion years old")
- Name a historical event ("the Ute called these waters 'pagosa' — healing waters")
- Name a phenomenon ("water rising from 700 feet below the earth's surface")
- Create anticipation ("we'll tell you exactly why this one smells like that")

**What the intro is NOT allowed to do:**
- Explain a mechanism ("the reason the water is hot is because...")
- Give comparisons ("hotter than Yellowstone, more mineralized than...")
- Provide data that belongs in a later chapter
- Answer questions it just raised

If the intro explains something, listeners have less reason to keep listening. The explanation belongs in the home chapter. The intro creates the question; the home chapter delivers the answer.

---

## Rule 3: Drive/transition chapters observe — they don't teach

The drive chapter is the listener's eyes on the road. Its job is to point at things and name them, not explain them.

**What the drive chapter is allowed to do:**
- Direct attention: "Watch for the red-orange roadcuts coming up on your right — that's the Cutler Formation."
- Name a feature: "You're now entering the San Luis Valley, the largest alpine valley in North America."
- Create a bridge: "The canyon walls here tell a story we'll get into in the geology chapter."
- Set scene with sensory details: "The air changes here — cooler, more sulfurous — you're close."

**What the drive chapter is NOT allowed to do:**
- Explain why the Cutler Formation is red-orange (that's the geology chapter)
- Explain what makes a valley "alpine" or what a rift graben is (geology chapter)
- Explain the sulfur smell (hot spring chemistry chapter)

The drive chapter earns its keep by making listeners look up from their phones. The geology chapter earns its keep by satisfying the curiosity the drive chapter created. They need each other — but they can't overlap.

---

## Rule 4: Wildlife gets one spotlight, one observation elsewhere

Each animal species gets one dedicated section where it is treated in full: biology, behavior, ecology, habitat, how to spot it, why it matters to this landscape. That is its home.

**In non-home chapters:** wildlife appears only as a sensory detail or a single-sentence passing mention. No biological explanation. No behavior description beyond the immediate observation.

**Wrong (biology appears outside the wildlife section):**
- Drive chapter: "Bighorn sheep are incredibly well-adapted to these canyon walls — their split hooves act like climbing shoes, and they can descend a 60-degree slope without slipping..."

**Right (drive chapter passes, wildlife section teaches):**
- Drive chapter: "Don't be surprised if you see bighorn sheep on those canyon walls — they treat this cliff face like a highway."
- Wildlife section: full explanation of hoof anatomy, cliff navigation, herd behavior, rutting season, why they're here.

This rule prevents the wildlife section from feeling like a recap of things the listener already heard on the drive.

---

## Rule 5: No mechanism gets explained twice

A mechanism is any process with cause, effect, and sequence: hydrothermal ore formation, volcanic geothermal heat transfer, glacial carving, fault-zone spring emergence, altitude acclimatization, mineral absorption through skin.

Each mechanism gets one full explanation in the earlier chapter where it first becomes relevant. Every later chapter that references the same mechanism uses a pointer, not a repeat explanation.

**Pointer formats that work:**
- "...the same hydrothermal system we described in the geology chapter."
- "...the fault-zone emergence pattern you heard about at Glenwood Springs."
- "...altitude acclimatization, covered in the Steamboat Springs physiology section."

**What never appears in a later chapter:** a re-explanation of how the mechanism works, even a condensed version. Even one sentence of mechanism is one sentence too many if the home chapter already handled it.

If two chapters genuinely both need the mechanism to make sense, rethink the chapter structure rather than explaining it twice.

## Rule 6: Write a topic inventory before drafting

Before writing any chapter, write a list of the 5-8 topics that chapter will cover. This is not optional and it is not done after the draft — it is done before the first sentence.

**Topic inventory format:**

```
Chapter: [Chapter name]
Topics this chapter will cover:
1. [Topic] — HOME (this chapter teaches it fully)
2. [Topic] — REFERENCE (home is [other chapter]; one sentence only here)
3. [Topic] — HOME
4. [Topic] — REFERENCE (home is [episode 2, geology section]; do not re-explain)
...
```

The inventory forces the writer to make conscious decisions about ownership before the draft begins. Topics that belong elsewhere get marked as REFERENCE before a single explanatory sentence about them gets written.

After the draft: scan the chapter against the inventory. Every REFERENCE topic must be one sentence. If it's longer, cut it.

---

## Rule 7: Cross-episode continuity — never repeat what an earlier episode already taught

Each episode exists within a series. Listeners who have heard earlier episodes should not feel like they are hearing the same content again with different place names attached.

At the top of every episode planning document, list all topics covered in depth in previous episodes. Common examples:

- Episode 1 covered: basic geothermal system mechanics, sodium bicarbonate mineral profile, altitude acclimatization basics
- Episode 2 covered: lithium-rich spring chemistry, Ute relationship with hot springs, elk ecology and behavior

Those topics are now off-limits for full treatment in new episodes. They may appear as brief references ("the same geothermal mechanics we covered at Steamboat") but may not be re-explained, even for listeners who missed earlier episodes.

**The "missed episode" problem:** it is tempting to re-explain foundational concepts for listeners who may be starting mid-series. Resist this. The solution is a short "previously in the series" note at the episode opening, not repeated full explanations inside each chapter. New listeners can listen to earlier episodes. Repeat listeners should not be penalized with content they already heard.

## Rule 9: Geology is capped at 20 minutes per episode — spread across all chapters

Geology content across the entire episode must total no more than 20 minutes. At 137.75 WPM effective rate, 20 minutes = approximately 2,755 words. Count all geology-focused content regardless of which chapter it appears in.

**What counts toward the geology cap:**
- Any chapter or section whose primary subject is geological formation, rock type, tectonic event, mineral chemistry, volcanic history, fault zone mechanics, or geothermal plumbing
- Geology-heavy paragraphs inside otherwise mixed chapters (hot springs mineralogy, canyon formation, travertine chemistry, etc.)

**What does NOT count:**
- One-sentence geology references in drive or wildlife chapters (Rule 3/4 pointers)
- Come Back chapter mentions of trail geology (which must stay brief per Rule 8)
- The intro hook teasing a geological feature (Rule 2)

**How to apply the cap:**

Before drafting, assign an estimated word budget to each geology section. Total them. If they exceed 2,755 words, either:
- Consolidate two geology sections into one richer chapter
- Trim the less essential formation details (save the deep-cut material for the audio; the companion page cards carry the visual reference)
- Move supplementary geology context to a "geology reference" note that links to the companion page instead of reading it aloud

**The priority within the 20-minute budget:**

1. The geological origin of the hot spring itself (always first — this is why they're there)
2. The most dramatic or unusual geological feature visible from the road or trail
3. Any geology directly tied to the 1923 connection or mining history

If the budget runs short, cut in reverse priority order.

**The goal:** Bob gets genuinely meaty geology — specific formation names, tectonic events, mineral chemistry — but Annette isn't sitting through a geology lecture for half the drive. The 20-minute cap keeps geology as the richest thread in the episode, not the dominant one.

## Rule 8: "Come Back For This" stays practical — no ecology or history

The Come Back chapter is a practical planning guide. It answers four questions:

1. **WHERE:** Specific trails, spots, facilities — named and described with enough detail to find them.
2. **WHEN:** Best season, time of day, weather conditions, permit windows.
3. **HOW DIFFICULT:** Honest difficulty rating. Distance, elevation gain, technical requirements.
4. **WHY WORTH RETURNING:** What makes this worth a dedicated trip, not just a passing stop.

The Come Back chapter does not introduce:
- New wildlife biology or ecology facts
- New geological explanations or formation descriptions
- New historical context or cultural background
- Any mechanism that wasn't already covered in an earlier chapter

If a trail has spectacular geology, the Come Back chapter can say "the trail passes through the same Cutler Formation visible from the highway" but it does not explain what the Cutler Formation is. The geology chapter did that. Come Back just confirms the geology is worth seeing up close.

If an activity is best for wildlife spotting, the Come Back chapter can say "prime bighorn habitat" but it does not describe bighorn behavior or ecology. The wildlife section did that.

Come Back is a closing chapter that sends listeners forward with a practical plan, not a teaching chapter that introduces new content after the main episode is done.

## Rule 10: Research named properties, films, and famous figures with direct local connections

Every episode must include at least one story about a real famous person, named property, or specific film/TV production with a **verifiable, place-specific connection** to the exact location covered. Not "Colorado is popular with celebrities" — but "this ranch, this road, this hotel, this specific scene."

**What qualifies:**
- Named private ranches owned by recognizable figures (Ralph Lauren's Double RL Ranch near Ridgway; Ted Turner's Vermejo Park; etc.)
- Films or TV productions shot on location at specific identifiable spots (True Grit 1969 filmed in Ridgway/Ouray; D&SNG appearances in dozens of Westerns; Butch Cassidy scenes near Telluride)
- Historical figures who stayed, worked, or died at the specific location (Doc Holliday in Glenwood Springs; Theodore Roosevelt at Hotel Colorado; etc.)
- Celebrity or cultural figures with documented ownership or repeated presence

**The standard:** the connection must be to the specific town, road, ranch, or building — not the general region.

**Research process:** Before drafting, run a targeted search for "[location name] celebrity ranch," "[location name] film location," "[location name] famous owner," "[location name] historical figure." Verify the connection is real before writing it. If it checks out, write it as a narrative moment — give the person texture, not just a name-drop.

**Tone:** Tell the story the way you'd tell a good piece of gossip to a friend who loves history. Ralph Lauren doesn't just own a ranch near here — he bought 14,000 acres of the old Ute grazing land in 1991, built it into a working cattle operation, uses authentic period tack, and has hosted everyone from Prince Charles to Anna Wintour. *That's* the story.

**If no famous person connection exists for this stop:** substitute with a specific, named historical incident (a famous murder, a notable disaster, a legendary business deal, a record-breaking achievement) tied to the exact location.

## Quick reference checklist

Use this before submitting any episode draft:

- [ ] Topic assignment list exists and every major topic has exactly one home chapter
- [ ] List of topics covered in previous episodes exists at the top of the planning document
- [ ] Topic inventory written for each chapter BEFORE drafting
- [ ] Intro hook names facts but explains none of them
- [ ] Drive chapter points at features, does not explain them
- [ ] Each wildlife species has one full-treatment section; all other appearances are one sentence
- [ ] Every mechanism appears in full exactly once; later references are pointers only
- [ ] Come Back chapter contains no new wildlife biology, geology, or history
- [ ] Geology word count totaled across all chapters: must be ≤ 2,755 words (20 min at 137.75 WPM)
- [ ] At least one named person / film / ranch story with a verifiable place-specific connection
- [ ] Final pass: every REFERENCE topic in non-home chapters is one sentence or fewer
