# Come Back For This — Colorado Hot Springs Road Trip 2026

*Research compiled May 20, 2026. Each section covers what's worth a dedicated return trip at that stop.*

---

## Glenwood Springs

### Hiking

**1. Hanging Lake Trail**
- Difficulty: Moderate | Distance: 3 miles round trip | Elevation gain: ~1,000 ft
- Why it's worth it: One of the most photographed spots in Colorado. A turquoise, mineral-rich lake sits suspended mid-canyon, fed by waterfalls that pour from a ledge above. The geology is the hook for Bob: this is a classic travertine formation where calcium carbonate deposits built up over centuries to form a natural dam holding the lake aloft. It genuinely looks impossible.
- Permit required: Yes, $12/person (May-Dec), timed entry. Book at visitglenwood.com/HangingLake. Sell out fast on weekends.

**2. Grizzly Creek Trail**
- Difficulty: Moderate | Distance: ~8 miles round trip
- Why it's worth it: Follows an ice-cold tributary of the Colorado River into a side canyon off Glenwood Canyon. Excellent trout fishing, cool even in summer, and you're walking through layers of Precambrian metamorphic and Paleozoic sedimentary rock the whole way. The contrast between the canyon walls is striking for anyone who loves geology.

**3. Glenwood Canyon Recreation Trail**
- Difficulty: Easy | Distance: 15.5 miles one way (or any portion)
- Why it's worth it: A paved trail following the Colorado River through the canyon. For context: this is one of the most geologically dramatic paved trails in the U.S. The canyon walls rise 1,000-2,000 feet on either side, exposing rock layers that span 600 million years of Earth history. Great for a scenic morning bike ride or casual walk.

**4. South Canyon Hot Springs Trail**
- Difficulty: Easy-Moderate | Distance: ~2 miles round trip
- Why it's worth it: A lesser-known hike that leads to a primitive hot spring along the Colorado River. Combines a canyon geology walk with a soak in a natural pool. Very different vibe from the resort pools.

### Extreme Sports & Adventure

**1. Colorado River Whitewater Rafting**
- What to know: The Shoshone section near Glenwood is a classic Class III run. Several excellent outfitters: Defiance Rafting Co. (raftdefiance.com), Blue Sky Adventures (raftbluesky.com), and Adventure Outdoors (coloradoraft.com).
- Bob's angle: 2026 is being called "Colorado's longest rafting season" by Visit Glenwood Springs because of the strong snowpack. Flows should be excellent. The Shoshone Hydroelectric Plant creates a consistent, guaranteed rapid that doesn't slow down in late season.
- Combo packages: Glenwood Canyon Zipline offers raft + 4-zip combo packages, flying high above the canyon on 4 full zip lines plus bridge crossings, then hitting the river. Smart way to do both in one day.

**2. Glenwood Caverns Adventure Park**
- Why Bob would love it: This is America's only mountaintop theme park at 7,132 feet, reached by gondola. Key rides: the Giant Canyon Swing launches you 1,300 feet out over Glenwood Canyon, the Alpine Coaster runs 3,400 feet down the mountainside, and Defiance is the highest looping roller coaster in the U.S.
- The cave tours (Historic Fairy Cave and King's Row) are genuinely world-class and scientifically fascinating: 300+ million year old formations including stalactites, flowstone, and rimstone pools.
- Geology angle for Bob: the caves were formed through a combination of calcite-dissolving acidic groundwater and later hydrothermal activity from Glenwood Hot Springs below.

**3. Glenwood Canyon Zipline**
- Standalone option at zippingglenwoodsprings.com
- Four zip lines plus a 40-foot climbing wall and high adventure ropes course. Views directly into the canyon are dramatic.

### Restaurants (for Annette)

**1. The Pullman** -- Downtown on 7th Street restaurant row. New American cuisine in a warm setting. Consistently top-ranked on TripAdvisor. Known for creative seasonal menus and great cocktails.

**2. Riviera Supper Club & Piano Bar** -- A Glenwood institution since 1947. New American with impeccable river views and live piano music. The kind of place that feels like a genuine occasion. More upscale, excellent for a special dinner.

**3. Casey Brewing and Blending** -- Grand Avenue. Award-winning wild ales and sours made with 99% Colorado ingredients. One of the most respected craft breweries in the state, known nationally for their mixed-fermentation approach. Great food alongside serious beer.

**4. Daily Bread Cafe** -- Casual, exposed brick, cozy chairs. The local's breakfast and lunch spot. Morning pastries and coffee, excellent sandwiches. Good for a relaxed start to the day.

**5. Smoke Modern Barbecue** -- For Bob especially. Locally owned, wood-smoked everything, not a chain.

## Ridgway / Ouray

### Hiking

**1. Box Canyon Falls (Ouray)**
- Difficulty: Easy | Distance: 0.3-0.5 miles | Elevation gain: minimal
- Why it's worth it: Canyon Creek has carved a narrow slot through solid quartzite rock and drops 285 feet in a dramatic plunge. The canyon is so narrow and deep it creates its own microclimate. The Black Swift, one of North America's rarest birds, nests behind the waterfall every summer (returns in June). Visitor center has excellent geology and wildlife exhibits.
- Geology for Bob: The quartzite here is some of the hardest rock in the San Juan Mountains, which is why the waterfall still exists -- harder to erode than the surrounding material.

**2. Upper/Lower Amphitheater Loop (Ouray)**
- Difficulty: Moderate-Hard | Distance: ~5 miles
- Why it's worth it: Classic Ouray hike through conifer forest with panoramic views of the valley and the San Juan peaks. You're walking through layers of Proterozoic and Tertiary volcanic rock. Connects to longer backcountry routes.

**3. Blue Lakes Trail (near Ridgway)**
- Difficulty: Hard | Distance: 7 miles round trip | Elevation gain: 2,800 ft
- Why it's worth it: Arguably the most spectacular hike in the Ridgway/Ouray area. Three turquoise alpine lakes beneath Mount Sneffels (14,158 ft). The wildflower display in late June/early July is legendary. Bob would love the volcanic geology -- Sneffels is a stratovolcano remnant.

**4. Million Dollar Highway Geology**
- Not a hike but worth noting: The drive south on US-550 from Ridgway through Ouray exposes 70 million years of geological history. The red walls are iron-oxide-stained sandstone. The white cliffs are calcareous shale. Bob could narrate this drive on its own merits.

### Extreme Sports & Adventure

**1. Ouray Via Ferrata -- MUST DO**
- Why it's one of the best in the U.S.: The via ferrata uses the walls of the Uncompahgre Gorge, a slot canyon carved through Precambrian granite. There are three routes:
  - **Downstream Route (4B rating)**: More accessible, great for first-timers. Features a cable bridge, rung sections across blank rock, and a suspended bridge over the gorge.
  - **Upstream Route (4.5B rating)**: Significantly harder with sustained overhanging terrain. Bob's route.
  - **Gold Mountain Via Ferrata**: The longest and highest via ferrata in the country. Guide required. Most serious climbers call it the crown jewel.
- Season: May through October, 8am-4pm. No permit required for Downstream/Upstream routes, but a guide is available through Base Camp Ouray (basecampouray.com) and San Juan Mountain Guides (mtnguide.net).
- All required gear (harness, helmet, via ferrata set) can be rented locally.

**2. Ouray Ice Park (summer off-season, but worth knowing)**
- The world's first public ice climbing park is built in the Uncompahgre Gorge each winter. In summer the gorge becomes the via ferrata corridor. Worth noting for a winter return trip.

**3. Jeep/4WD Tours on Engineer Pass / Corkscrew Pass**
- Multiple outfitters in Ouray offer guided 4WD tours into the backcountry on roads like Engineer Pass (13,218 ft) and Corkscrew Pass. These are old mining roads through genuine high-altitude terrain. Bob would find the mining history angle (silver and gold mines visible along the routes) deeply interesting.
- Swiss Chalet Tours and Ouray Off-Road Adventures are local operators.

### Restaurants (for Annette)

**1. Colorado Boy Southwest Pub (Ouray)** -- Southwestern flavors with great pizza and green chile. Craft beer. Charming mountain ambiance. Consistently top-rated in Ouray. Casual and fun.

**2. Ouray Brewery (Ouray)** -- The local craft brewery. Good pub food, great beers brewed in-house. River views from the patio. The place locals go.

**3. The Million Roadhouse (Ridgway)** -- millionroadhouse.com. Heritage and contemporary dining, local brews, craft cocktails, and innovative dishes. The best option in Ridgway proper and beloved by locals.

**4. True Grit Cafe (Ridgway)** -- Named after the John Wayne film (portions of which were filmed in the area). Classic American diner surrounded by memorabilia. Fun atmosphere, good burgers, affordable.

**5. Eatery 66 (Ridgway)** -- Innovative New American cuisine with local ingredients. More upscale option in Ridgway. Worth it for a special dinner.

## Durango

### Hiking

**1. Colorado Trail -- Junction Creek Section**
- Difficulty: Hard | Distance: Variable (can do 5-15 miles one way)
- Why it's worth it: This is the famous Colorado Trail, which runs 535 miles from Denver to Durango. The Junction Creek section is accessible right from town and climbs through ponderosa pine and spruce-fir forest into real backcountry. Challenging climbs, epic descents. Bob can literally say he hiked part of the CT.

**2. Animas Mountain Trail**
- Difficulty: Hard | Distance: ~5 miles | Elevation gain: 1,400 ft
- Why it's worth it: Steep and technical, but it's IN Durango. No drive required. Summit views of the Animas River valley and the San Juan peaks. Access to the Overend Mountain Park system (300 acres of trails within city limits -- Durango is serious about its trail system).

**3. Hermosa Creek Trail**
- Difficulty: Moderate | Distance: 20 miles point-to-point
- Why it's worth it: One of the great long-distance trail options near Durango. Follows a beautiful creek through a lush forested valley. It's also a world-class mountain bike trail. The geological setting -- Cretaceous marine sediments forming the valley -- is interesting. Best done as a shuttle trip.

### Extreme Sports & Adventure

**1. Durango & Silverton Narrow Gauge Railroad -- Wilderness Trailhead Drop**
- What makes it special for Bob: The railroad isn't just a scenic ride (though it's spectacular). From May 16 through October, they run a Wilderness Access Train that drops hikers and backpackers at remote trailheads like Needleton and Elk Park in the Weminuche Wilderness, inaccessible by road. You ride into the backcountry through Glenwood Canyon on a steam locomotive, get dropped in the wilderness, and hike out. This is genuinely one of the most unusual backcountry access options in the American West.
- Geology bonus: The train runs through 45 miles of the Animas River Gorge, which exposes rock from Precambrian basement to Cretaceous shales -- a 1.7-billion-year geology transect.

**2. Durango Mountain Biking -- World Class**
- Why it's exceptional: Durango has produced more professional mountain bikers than almost any other city in the U.S. (Ned Overend, John Tomac, Missy Giove all trained here). 300+ miles of trails.
- Bob's top picks:
  - **Horse Gulch/Telegraph Trail System**: Right in town. Great network with everything from beginner to advanced with jumps, berms, and tabletops.
  - **Twin Buttes**: West of town. Technical switchbacks plus flowy descents with Animas Valley overlooks.
  - **Hermosa Creek Trail**: The classic Durango epic, a 20-mile flowing descent through canyon and forest.
- Rental: MTB rentals widely available from local shops like Hassle Free Sports and Durango Wheel Club.

**3. Animas River Kayaking and Rafting**
- The Animas runs right through downtown Durango. Class II-III sections suitable for a guided half-day trip. Multiple outfitters including Mild to Wild Rafting (mild2wildrafting.com) offer trips. The train-to-raft combination packages are worth noting: ride the train to Silverton, raft back down.

### Restaurants (for Annette)

**1. The Mahogany Grill (inside the Historic Strater Hotel)** -- Victorian era ambiance, steakhouse and seafood, locally sourced from family farms. One of Durango's most beloved dining experiences. The Strater Hotel is an 1887 landmark. Dinner here is a genuine event.

**2. Seasons of Durango** -- Oak-fired meats, fresh seafood, seasonal menu. Known for the bone-in filet mignon and Chilean sea bass. One of Durango's finest restaurants.

**3. Carver Brewing Company** -- The oldest brewpub in Durango. Great craft beers and solid food. The Four Corners Steak Sandwich is legendary locally. Casual, fun, historic building.

**4. Oscar's Cafe** -- Classic 1950s diner experience. Breakfast all day, real burgers, crispy fried chicken, homemade pies. Unpretentious and beloved. The kind of place that's been the same for 40 years and earns it.

**5. Durango Diner** -- 55 years in downtown Durango. Famous locally for the green chili. Breakfast and lunch only. A true local institution.

## Pagosa Springs

### Hiking

**1. Treasure Falls Trail**
- Difficulty: Easy-Moderate | Distance: 1 mile round trip
- Why it's worth it: A 105-foot waterfall just off Highway 160 on the way to Wolf Creek Pass. Dramatic, accessible, photogenic. Worth a quick stop on the drive.

**2. Crater Lake Trail (from Wolf Creek Pass)**
- Difficulty: Moderate | Distance: ~5 miles round trip | Elevation gain: ~800 ft
- Why it's worth it: Descends from the Continental Divide down to a crystal-clear alpine lake surrounded by spruce-fir forest. One of the most scenic day hikes accessible from Wolf Creek Pass. The divide views at the trailhead are stunning.

**3. Turkey Creek Trail**
- Difficulty: Moderate | Distance: Variable
- Why it's worth it: Wildlife watching trail -- good for deer, elk, and spring wildflowers. Animal lovers' pick for the Pagosa area.

**4. West Fork Trail (Wolf Creek area)**
- Difficulty: Easy-Moderate | Distance: ~6 miles round trip
- Why it's worth it: Follows the West Fork of the San Juan River through a beautiful valley corridor. Aspen groves, mountain meadows, 14er views. Voted one of the best hikes in southwestern Colorado consistently.

### Extreme Sports & Adventure

**1. San Juan River Rafting and Kayaking**
- Pagosa Outside (pagosaoutside.com) offers San Juan River rafting, tubing, SUP, and kayak rentals. They've been the main local outfitter for 25 years.
- Wilderness Journeys Pagosa (wildernessjourneyspagosa.com) offers 4x4 backcountry tours into the San Juan National Forest on top of river activities.

**2. SUP and Kayaking -- Echo Lake and San Juan River**
- Pagosa Mountain Sports in downtown offers SUP board rentals.
- Echo Lake is the beginner/family option. The San Juan River offers more challenging paddling.

**3. 4x4 Backcountry Tours**
- Wilderness Journeys Pagosa runs guided off-road tours into the national forest. For Bob's combination of geology interest and adventure, this is a great option -- you access terrain otherwise unavailable and the San Juan Mountains have extraordinary volcanic and hydrothermal geology.

### Restaurants (for Annette)

**1. Riff Raff Brewing Company** -- The brewery is housed in a Victorian building and runs on locally harnessed geothermal energy from Pagosa's hot springs. Colorado's only hot-springs-powered brewery. Patio on the San Juan River. Great burgers, solid beer, excellent atmosphere. This is the go-to.

**2. Alley House Grille** -- New American cuisine in a converted historic building. Creative menu, local focus, more upscale. Durango locals drive to Pagosa specifically to eat here.

**3. Thai Pagosa** -- Award-winning Thai restaurant. An unlikely gem in a small Colorado mountain town that consistently earns the best-in-town honors. Drunken noodles, curries, summer rolls.

**4. Sage Eatery** -- Started as a food truck and became a beloved permanent restaurant. Known for high-quality local meats and great burgers. Locals' favorite.

## Moffat / San Luis Valley (Great Sand Dunes)

### Hiking

**1. High Dune (First Ridge) -- Great Sand Dunes National Park**
- Difficulty: Moderate-Hard | Distance: 2.5 miles round trip | Elevation: starts at 8,170 ft
- Why it's worth it: The most accessible summit in the dune field with panoramic views of the entire field, the San Luis Valley floor, and the Sangre de Cristo peaks behind. Walking on loose sand is roughly 3x as exhausting as solid ground, so this "short" hike is genuinely challenging.
- Geology for Bob: The dunes are the result of 440,000 years of wind and water work. Sand from ancient lakebeds was carried northeast by wind, hit the Sangre de Cristos, and dropped. Five distinct dune types are present: barchan, parabolic, reversing, star, and transverse.

**2. Star Dune Summit**
- Difficulty: Very Hard | Distance: 8 miles round trip
- Why it's worth it: At 750 feet, Star Dune is the tallest dune in North America. The hike takes most of the day. Zero trail -- you navigate by landmark. The views from the top are otherworldly. For Bob, this is the geological showpiece of the entire trip.

**3. Mosca Pass Trail**
- Difficulty: Moderate | Distance: 7 miles round trip | Elevation: ends at 9,737 ft
- Why it's worth it: Escapes the dunes entirely, climbing through aspen and spruce forest to a high mountain pass. Complete change of environment from the dune experience. Good wildlife habitat.

**4. Zapata Falls**
- Difficulty: Easy | Distance: 1 mile round trip (with a final wade/rock-hop into the gorge)
- Why it's worth it: A 30-foot waterfall hidden inside a deep rock gorge on the flanks of the Sangre de Cristos, about 10 miles south of the park. The creek has carved a narrow cave-like grotto through sedimentary rock. You wade into the gorge to reach it. Bring towels. This is the surprise gem of the area.

### Extreme Sports & Adventure

**1. Sandboarding and Sand Sledding**
- The park allows it on the dunes. Board rentals available at Kristi Mountain Sports in Alamosa (a few miles south). Unique, physically demanding, and genuinely fun.

**2. Medano Creek Water Play**
- Seasonal (usually April-July): A creek that flows across the sand flat at the base of the dunes, creating a shallow, flowing river. Late May/early June is prime season. Families wade and splash. It's one of the stranger natural phenomena in the country -- a flowing beach.

**3. Horseback Riding in the Preserve**
- Horses are allowed in the Great Sand Dunes Preserve (the national forest land adjacent to the park). Riding through the dunes and onto the dunefield with a mountain backdrop is a genuinely spectacular experience. No commercial outfitter directly at the park, but rides can be arranged through ranches in the area.

### Restaurants (for Annette) -- Alamosa is the main town, 30 min from Great Sand Dunes

**1. Friar's Fork (Alamosa)** -- James Beard semifinalist for Best New Restaurant (2023). Italian cuisine inside a restored historic church building. Completely unexpected for a small San Luis Valley town. Worth the trip on its own.

**2. San Luis Valley Brewing Company (Alamosa)** -- The local craft brewery since 2006. Hearty American fare, good beer. Casual and welcoming. The gathering spot for locals.

**3. Calvillo's Mexican Restaurant (Alamosa)** -- True local institution. Authentic Mexican with homemade tortillas, savory green chile, and carne asada. The kind of place that's been doing the same thing right for decades.

**4. Milagros Coffee House (Alamosa)** -- A social enterprise under local charity La Puente (fighting hunger and homelessness in the valley). Great coffee, pastries, sandwiches. You feel good about spending money here.

## Villa Grove

*Note: Villa Grove is genuinely small. There are essentially no restaurants in Villa Grove itself. The nearest dining options are in Saguache (15 miles north) or Salida/Poncha Springs (37 miles north). Plan accordingly -- stock food or drive to Salida for dinner.*

### Hiking

**1. Orient Mine Bat Flight Hike**
- Difficulty: Moderate | Distance: ~4 miles round trip (2 miles up, all uphill)
- Why it's worth it for animal lovers: At the height of bat season (mid-June through mid-September), an estimated 250,000 Brazilian Free-Tailed Bats erupt from the mine at dusk. It's one of the most spectacular wildlife events in Colorado, almost unknown outside the state.
- Logistics: Located 4.5 miles south of Villa Grove on Saguache County Road GG (gravel). Admission is free but reservation required. Check in at the visitor center. Gate closes at 10pm. Bring a flashlight -- you'll hike down after dark.
- History bonus: You pass through the ghost town of Orient on the way up, where up to 400 people once lived during the iron ore mining era.

**2. Hot Springs Canyon Trail**
- Difficulty: Challenging | Distance: 5 miles out-and-back | Time: ~3 hours
- Why it's worth it: A challenging canyon hike through the Sangre de Cristo foothills with exposed geology. Less traveled than the more famous trails further north.

**3. Valley View Hot Springs Area Trails**
- Short, gentle trails connect the pools at Orient Land Trust (the nonprofit that manages Valley View Hot Springs). The upper pools require a 0.25-mile uphill hike. A two-mile path leads to the Orient Mine from the hot springs complex.
- Note: Valley View is clothing-optional. The setting -- natural pools at the foot of the Sangre de Cristos -- is extraordinary.

### Extreme Sports & Adventure

This area is not an adventure sports hub. The main draw is the solitude, the bats, and the springs. The closest adventure activities are in Salida/Buena Vista (37 miles north).

### Restaurants Near Villa Grove

**Honest assessment: There are no dining options in Villa Grove.** Plan ahead.

- **Saguache (15 miles north)**: A few basic options including local Mexican food.
- **Best option: Drive to Salida (37 miles north)** -- a surprisingly vibrant food scene for a small Colorado mountain town. Amicas (pizza and craft beer), Soulcraft Brewing, and Currents are all worth the drive.
- **Stock food at the villa or camp grocery supplies** -- this is the practical solution for this stop.

## Nathrop / Mount Princeton Area

### Hiking

**1. Chalk Cliffs and Chalk Creek Canyon**
- Difficulty: Easy walk/drive-by
- Why it's worth it for Bob: The Chalk Cliffs are sheer walls of brilliant white-gray rock on the southeast slopes of Mount Princeton -- but they're not chalk. They're kaolinite, formed by hydrothermal activity as hot mineral water seeps through granite faults, chemically altering the rock into soft, powdery clay minerals. It's one of the most visually dramatic examples of hydrothermal alteration geology in Colorado.
- Best view: Overlook off Highway 285 between Nathrop and Poncha Springs.

**2. Narrow Gauge Trail (#1432)**
- Difficulty: Moderate | AllTrails rating: 4.4 stars from 1,479 reviews
- Why it's worth it: On the pass between Mount Antero and Mount Princeton, two 14ers. Epic views without the extreme elevation gain of a full summit. Accessible to non-peak-baggers. Named for the historic narrow-gauge railroad that served the mining camps here.

**3. Mount Princeton Summit Trail**
- Difficulty: Very Hard | Distance: ~7 miles round trip | Elevation gain: 4,300 ft | Summit: 14,197 ft
- Why it's worth it for Bob: This is a Collegiate Peak -- the 14ers in this range are named for Ivy League universities. Princeton has great access and the geological section of the Sawatch Range exposed on the way up is a serious geology lesson in igneous and metamorphic rock.

**4. Mount Elbert Summit Trail (nearby, from Twin Lakes)**
- Difficulty: Hard | Distance: 9.5 miles round trip | Elevation gain: 4,700 ft | Summit: 14,433 ft
- Why it's worth it: The highest mountain in Colorado. A "Class 1" route (walk-up, no technical climbing). The North Elbert Trail (4.3 miles) is shorter but steeper. Best done early -- afternoon thunderstorms are serious at 14,000 feet.

### Extreme Sports & Adventure

**1. Browns Canyon National Monument -- Arkansas River Whitewater Rafting**
- Why it's world-class: Browns Canyon is consistently ranked among the top 5 whitewater rafting destinations in the U.S. The Arkansas River through this 21,000-acre monument runs Class III-IV depending on flows. Late May/June flows are typically the biggest of the year.
- Top outfitters:
  - **Wilderness Aware Rafting** (inaraft.com) -- Most popular, daily departures from Nathrop
  - **Monumental Expeditions** (monumentalexpeditions.com) -- Based at Chalk Creek Campground right in Nathrop, small groups, owner-operated
  - **Browns Canyon Rafting** (raftbrownscanyon.com) -- Half-day and full-day options, ~10 miles of Arkansas River
  - **Performance Tours** (performancetours.com) -- 30+ years running this section
- The geology angle for Bob: Browns Canyon exposes 1.4-billion-year-old Proterozoic granite and gneiss. Rafting through it is like floating through the basement of North America.

**2. Rock Climbing -- Browns Canyon Granite**
- The canyon walls offer significant rock climbing on the same ancient granite. A number of established routes on the canyon walls. Contact local outfitters for guided climbing options.

**3. Mount Princeton Hot Springs** (already on the trip itinerary)
- Worth noting the creek-side natural pools that cool down below the main resort. Chalk Creek runs ice-cold right past the hot springs -- the contrast soak (hot pool to cold creek) is spectacular.

### Restaurants (for Annette)

**Buena Vista** (the nearest real town, about 8 miles north of Nathrop):

**1. House Rock Kitchen** -- Innovative, creative menu with a great outdoor patio. Local-centric. One of the favorite dinner spots for the BV food scene.

**2. Eddyline Restaurant** (Eddyline Brewery on South Main) -- Excellent grass-fed burgers and Colorado craft beer. The outdoor patio is a great spot for post-rafting lunch.

**3. Wesley and Rose (inside the Surf Hotel)** -- The most upscale dining option in Buena Vista. Worth it for a special dinner. The Surf Hotel is a beautifully designed boutique hotel and the restaurant matches.

**4. Sorelle Delicatessen** -- Locally loved sandwich and deli shop. Great for a quick, quality lunch before a hike or raft trip.

**In Salida** (20 miles south, another option):

**5. Amicas** -- Wood-fired pizza and great craft beer. Salida's most beloved restaurant. The patio on the Arkanas River is a standout.

## Leadville

### Hiking

**1. Mineral Belt Trail**
- Difficulty: Moderate | Distance: 11.6-mile loop | Paved surface
- Why it's worth it for Bob: This paved loop around the city passes historical mining artifacts, relics, and remnants of the Leadville silver rush. You walk past headframes, mine waste piles (called "dumps"), ruins of stamp mills, and Victorian-era mining infrastructure -- all with views of the Sawatch and Mosquito mountain ranges on all sides. It's history and geology combined.
- Altitude note: The trail starts at 10,152 feet. Even fit hikers feel it. Go slow on day one.

**2. North Mount Elbert Trail**
- Difficulty: Very Hard | Distance: 4.3 miles one way | Elevation gain: 4,400 ft | Summit: 14,433 ft
- Why it's worth it: Colorado's highest peak, accessible from the Leadville side. The North Trail (from Halfmoon Campground) is shorter than the South Trail. An early alpine start (before dawn) is strongly recommended to beat afternoon thunderstorms.
- Best season: July-September for most hikers. Late May (when you're visiting) means there may be snow above 12,000 feet. Check conditions at 14ers.org before committing.

**3. Turquoise Lake Trail**
- Difficulty: Easy-Moderate | Distance: 9.5-mile loop | Elevation: ~9,900 ft
- Why it's worth it: A loop around Turquoise Lake just west of Leadville. Stunning Sawatch Range views, aspen groves, good wildlife habitat. One of the most scenic easy-access loops near any Colorado mountain town.

### Extreme Sports & Adventure

**1. Leadville Colorado & Southern Railroad**
- Not quite extreme, but genuinely special: A 2.5-hour round-trip ride climbing from 10,152 feet to nearly 11,000 feet on restored vintage railroad cars through high alpine scenery. Operates Memorial Day weekend through October. Bob's railroad and history interests collide here. This is the highest standard-gauge railroad in North America.

**2. Matchless Mine Tour**
- A surface tour of the most famous silver mine in Colorado's history. Horace Tabor's Matchless Mine produced millions in silver in the 1880s. The story of "Silver Dollar" Baby Doe Tabor (who froze to death here in 1935, refusing to leave the mine she'd been ordered to guard) is one of Colorado's most dramatic true stories.
- Open daily Memorial Day through September, 11am-4:45pm. Tours at noon, 1, 2, and 3pm.

**3. ATV / UTV Tours**
- Leadville ATV Tours offers guided off-road access to high-altitude terrain above Leadville. You can reach alpine elevations of 12,000+ feet with open-air access to terrain that's otherwise only accessible on foot. Bob's combination of horses and extreme sports transfers well to UTVs in this landscape.

**4. Twin Lakes Paddling**
- Twin Lakes (just south of Leadville) offers kayaking, SUP, and paddleboarding on crystal-clear high-altitude water with direct views of Mount Elbert. Rentals available seasonally.

### Restaurants (for Annette)

**1. The Silver Dollar Saloon** -- Family-owned since 1943. An original oak bar. Victorian-era building on Harrison Avenue. Chef Eric focuses on fresh, local ingredients with classic American dishes. This is the most authentic piece of old Leadville you can eat in.

**2. Tennessee Pass Cafe** -- Farm-to-table approach, locally sourced ingredients, creative seasonal menu. A genuine gem for a small mountain town at 10,000+ feet.

**3. Treeline Kitchen** -- Locally owned, family-run since 2017. Cozy courtyard with a firepit and blankets on the chairs. The mountain-town ambiance is real.

**4. High Mountain Pies** -- Pizza institution in Leadville for 20+ years. In a building that looks like an old house, which it essentially is. Great wings, salads, customizable pies, outdoor seating. The reliable local favorite.

**5. Casa Blanca Restaurant** -- Family-owned for over 40 years, serving authentic New Mexican dishes. A hidden gem slightly off the main street. The green chile is the reason to go.
