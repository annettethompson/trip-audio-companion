## 1. Iron Mountain Hot Springs — The Site, Its Geology, Its History

### What Iron Mountain Hot Springs Is

Iron Mountain Hot Springs sits directly on the south bank of the Colorado River in west Glenwood Springs, Colorado. It is a National Natural Landmark, recognized alongside Glenwood Caverns Adventure Park for the rare geothermal and cave systems that have been forming beneath Glenwood Springs for millions of years. The facility currently features 16 geothermal pools fed by four distinct natural springs originating deep underground, plus a freshwater pool. Pool temperatures range from 98°F to 108°F, giving bathers a gradient of thermal intensity. A 2023 expansion added a 21+ adults-only area with internationally-inspired mineral pools, a cold plunge, and the Sandbar Café.

### What Makes It Geologically Special

The designation "National Natural Landmark" is not marketing language. The site sits at the confluence of a very specific set of geological circumstances that are rare enough to merit federal recognition. As the Colorado River carved progressively deeper into Glenwood Canyon over the past three-plus million years, the geothermal springs that emerge here shifted accordingly — they track the river's erosional path. This means the location of Iron Mountain Hot Springs is itself a record of river incision history.

The water that feeds the pools is true geothermal water, not simply heated surface water. Surface water percolates through fractures and faults in the bedrock, descending to great depth where geothermal gradients heat it — approximately 25-30°C per kilometer of depth under normal continental crust. At depth, the water picks up dissolved minerals from the surrounding rock. Buoyed by its heat (hot fluids are less dense), it rises back through fracture networks and fault zones to emerge at the surface as springs.

### The 14 Minerals in the Water

As this geothermal water circulates through ancient rock layers, it dissolves and absorbs 14 restorative minerals. The five most abundant are iron, sulfate, chloride, sodium, and calcium. Iron gives the property its name — the water has a distinctly reddish-brown tinge from dissolved ferrous iron, which oxidizes to ferric iron (rust) on contact with air, the same chemistry that stains sandstone formations orange throughout the Colorado Plateau. The sulfate comes from gypsum and anhydrite beds in the Paleozoic stratigraphy. The high sodium and chloride content reflects the deep circulation through the Paradox Formation, a thick Pennsylvanian-age evaporite sequence laid down when an inland sea repeatedly evaporated, concentrating salt.

Balneological research (the science of mineral-water bathing therapy) indicates that sulfate and magnesium are absorbed transdermally during soaking. A 2023 systematic review in PMC found that hot spring minerals absorbed through the skin may have antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, and analgesic effects. The evidence base is still developing but the mechanism is plausible: magnesium deficiency is widespread, and transdermal absorption during extended warm-water soaking provides a physiologically relevant dose.

### Site History

For centuries, nomadic Ute Indian bands traveled to this exact stretch of the Colorado River to soak in the iron-rich mineral waters. The Ute name for the broader spring complex was Yampah — "Big Medicine" — reflecting the central role of these waters in their healing traditions.

In 1897, Sheriff Bob Ware established the first formal bathhouse on this stretch of the river, calling it the West Glenwood Health Spa. Over the following decades the facility changed hands repeatedly and was known variously as the Wash Allen Bathhouse, the Gamba Mineral Springs, the Glenwood Health Spa, and the Fort Defiance Bathhouse. Visitors traveled from considerable distances to soak in the iron-rich waters. The facility closed in 1966.

Iron Mountain Hot Springs reopened on the historic site in 2015 after a multi-year development effort, restoring and modernizing what had been dormant for nearly 50 years. The current facility has won recognition for blending contemporary amenity with the site's 130-year bathing tradition.

## 2. Colorado Hot Springs Geology — How Geothermal Systems Form Here

### The Basic Physics of a Hot Spring

A hot spring is a natural discharge of groundwater that has been heated by the Earth's internal energy and returns to the surface. The physics are elegantly simple: water is dense when cold and buoyant when hot, so it establishes a convective circulation. Surface water — rain, snowmelt — enters the ground through permeable rock, fractures, and faults. It descends. As it descends, it heats up at the geothermal gradient (roughly 25-30°C per kilometer for continental crust, more under tectonically active areas). Once hot enough to be less dense than the cooler water above it, it begins to rise, carrying its dissolved mineral load back up along whatever permeable pathway exists — often the same fault system it descended through.

This is called deep circulation, and it is the dominant model for Colorado's many hot springs. The heat source is not a magma chamber in most cases; it is simply the accumulated geothermal heat of the Earth's interior, which is hotter the deeper you go. Some Colorado springs may have a component of magmatic heating — cooling igneous intrusions at depth warming the circulating groundwater — but the deep-circulation model is sufficient to explain most of Colorado's 93+ known thermal areas.

### Why Colorado Has So Many Hot Springs

Colorado has an unusually high concentration of hot springs — 93 known thermal areas including natural springs, augmented natural springs, and wells — for several interconnected reasons:

1. **Rocky Mountain uplift and extensional faulting**: The Colorado Rockies are a tectonically active zone. The region experienced massive Laramide compression starting ~75 million years ago that built the mountains, followed by Basin-and-Range style extension in the Cenozoic that created the Rio Grande Rift. This extensional fault system runs through central and western Colorado. Faults are the plumbing system for geothermal water — they are the fracture networks that let water descend deep and return to the surface. More faults = more hot springs.

2. **The Leadville Limestone aquifer**: The Leadville Limestone (Mississippian age, approximately 340 million years old) is a critical component of Colorado's geothermal system. This carbonate rock is both permeable and soluble. It dissolves in the carbonic acid (H₂CO₃) produced when rainwater absorbs CO₂ from soil and decaying organic matter. This dissolution creates secondary porosity — caves, caverns, sinkholes, enlarged fractures — through which both descending meteoric water and ascending thermal water can flow. The Leadville Limestone essentially acts as a horizontal aquifer, routing thermal water laterally from deep circulation zones to surface discharge points. At Glenwood Springs, the Leadville Limestone is recognized as the primary geothermal aquifer, with surface spring temperatures reaching 44-51°C (111-124°F).

3. **The Paradox Formation as a mineralization source**: As thermal water circulates through the deep Paradox Formation — a thick sequence of Pennsylvanian-age evaporites (salt, gypsum, anhydrite) deposited 300+ million years ago when repeated marine incursions evaporated in a restricted basin — it picks up enormous quantities of dissolved solids. The Yampah springs at Glenwood Springs discharge approximately 120 cubic yards of dissolved salt into the Colorado River daily. This is the same chemical process that makes the Colorado River saltier as it moves downstream — a serious issue for agriculture in the lower basin.

### The Specific Geology of the Glenwood Springs Geothermal System

The Glenwood Springs area lies at the intersection of several structural features that make it an exceptional geothermal location:

- The **Sawatch fault** system runs through the area, providing the deep fracture networks that serve as circulation pathways
- The **White River Uplift** to the north creates the topographic gradient that drives surface water into the subsurface
- The late Paleozoic **Eagle Basin** structural low influenced the deposition of thick evaporite sequences now being dissolved by circulating groundwater
- Neogene tectonism, volcanism, and geomorphic evolution (including the nearby Dotsero volcanic center) contribute to elevated geothermal heat flow in the region

The Glenwood Springs has one of the highest geothermal water discharge rates in Colorado. The Big Spring alone reaches 143 liters per second — that's 2,263 gallons per minute of hot mineral water continuously emerging from the earth. The springs have extraordinarily high dissolved solids: Graves Spring reaches 21,500 mg/L total dissolved solids. For comparison, seawater is approximately 35,000 mg/L — so this spring water is more than half as salty as the ocean.

This extremely high mineralization reflects very deep and slow circulation through the thick evaporite sequences. The water has been underground for approximately 20,000 years before emerging at the surface — a reminder that what you're soaking in at Iron Mountain Hot Springs fell as snow during the tail end of the last Ice Age.

## 3. Glenwood Canyon — Geological Formation and the Rock Record

### The Canyon in Deep Time

Glenwood Canyon is 12.5 miles long and up to 1,700 feet deep, carved by the Colorado River through the Glenwood Springs anticline — a structural uplift of ancient rock that the river was forced to cut through rather than detour around. What makes the canyon scientifically extraordinary is the nearly complete section of Earth history it exposes: 1,700 million years of rock formations are visible from the roadway and bike path.

To put that in perspective: the canyon walls you drive through represent more than a third of Earth's entire 4.5-billion-year history, compressed into a 12-mile drive.

### The Precambrian Foundation — 1.7 Billion Years Old

At the deepest levels of the canyon, in its narrowest and most dramatic section near Hanging Lake, the Colorado River has cut down to Precambrian basement rock approximately 1.7 billion years old. These are granites, gneisses, and schists — igneous and metamorphic rocks that formed during ancient mountain-building events that predate all complex multicellular life on Earth. At the time these rocks crystallized, the most complex organisms on Earth were single-celled.

The Precambrian granite here is visually distinct: it is gray-pink, coarse-grained, often showing the banded texture of metamorphism or the pegmatitic veins of late-stage magmatic crystallization. The canyon walls in the deepest section have a dramatically different texture than the upper walls — rougher, more irregular, cut by dark dikes.

### The Great Unconformity

One of the most scientifically significant visible features in Glenwood Canyon is the Great Unconformity — the contact where Cambrian-age Sawatch Sandstone rests directly on the Precambrian basement. This contact surface represents 1,200 million years of missing rock — 1.2 billion years of Earth history that was deposited, then stripped away by erosion before the Cambrian sediments were laid down on top.

When you see that contact line in the canyon wall — the smooth surface where pale tan sandstone lies on darker, contorted ancient rock — you are looking at a 1.2-billion-year gap. The erosion surface was beveled to near-flatness before the Cambrian sea advanced across it and began depositing sand. The unconformity is the same feature visible at the Grand Canyon, but here you can see it from a highway at roughly 5,800 feet elevation.

### The Sawatch Sandstone — Cambrian

The Sawatch Sandstone (also called Sawatch Quartzite) directly overlying the Great Unconformity is Cambrian in age, approximately 500 million years old. It consists of clean, well-sorted quartz sand — a beach or nearshore marine deposit recording the first advance of a warm Cambrian sea across the leveled Precambrian surface. The Sawatch is resistant to erosion (the quartz grains are cemented into quartzite in many exposures) and forms prominent cliff bands. It is tinted by green and red minerals.

### Climbing Through the Paleozoic

Above the Sawatch, the canyon walls contain a nearly continuous stack of Paleozoic formations spanning from the Cambrian (500 Ma) through the Pennsylvanian (300 Ma):

- **Dotsero Formation** (Upper Cambrian) — thin-bedded dolomite with flat-pebble conglomerate and green-gray shale
- **Manitou Dolomite** (Ordovician, ~450 Ma) — older marine carbonate
- **Chaffee Group** (Devonian, ~375 Ma) — records the return of the sea after a long absence
- **Leadville Limestone** (Mississippian, ~340 Ma) — this is the critical unit. A massive, thick carbonate deposited in a warm, shallow tropical sea when Colorado was near the equator. The Leadville is the geothermal aquifer, the cave-forming formation, and forms the prominent white-gray cliffs of the canyon's upper rims. When you see the light-colored vertical cliff bands near the canyon rim, you are looking at the same formation that the Vapor Caves and Glenwood Caverns are carved into.
- **Belden Formation** (Pennsylvanian) — shale and limestone, recording a somewhat deeper or murkier sea
- **Pennsylvanian clastic units** — sandstone from the Ancestral Rockies, a separate mountain-building event before the modern Rockies existed

### How the River Cut the Canyon

The ancestral Colorado River flowed approximately 2,400 feet above its current location more than three million years ago, across a broad, gently sloping surface that had been built up over the Cenozoic. Then something changed the dynamic: regional uplift of the Colorado Plateau and Rocky Mountain system accelerated, steepening the river's gradient and triggering downcutting. Over the past 3+ million years, the river has cut downward at approximately 10 inches per thousand years on average — slow enough that it seems trivially slow, but fast enough to carve a 1,700-foot canyon.

The canyon is narrow because the Colorado cut downward faster than it widened. The rock was strong enough and the cutting fast enough that the walls remained nearly vertical. The result is one of the most dramatic canyons in North America that most people have never heard of because Grand Canyon gets all the attention.

### The Gray Layer and the Dotsero Connection

Beneath the highway, buried in the canyon floor sediments, lies what engineers call the "Gray Layer" — a muddy, unstable subsurface deposit that has caused multiple highway closures and one fatal rockslide. This layer formed geologically recently: a massive rockslide once dammed the Colorado River at this location, creating a lake behind the dam. Into this lake settled fine sediment and, critically, volcanic ash from the Dotsero volcanic eruption that occurred approximately 4,200 years ago, just upstream. The ash and sediment consolidated into an unstable clay-rich deposit that becomes a slip surface when wet. The Gray Layer is essentially a stratigraphic record of the Dotsero eruption, preserved beneath the highway.

### The Dotsero Volcano — Just Upstream

Speaking of Dotsero: just east of Glenwood Canyon along I-70, visible from the road, is Dotsero Crater — Colorado's only known volcano and one of the youngest volcanic features in the contiguous United States. The USGS Dotsero Volcanic Center documentation puts the most recent eruption at 4,150 ± 300 years BP, which is approximately 2200 BCE — during the Bronze Age. Mammoths were already extinct by then, but humans were present in the area.

Dotsero is a phreatomagmatic crater (maar) — formed when rising basaltic magma encountered groundwater and the resulting steam explosion blasted a 750-meter-wide, 76-meter-deep crater through the overlying rock. The explosion produced lahars (volcanic mudflows), scoria cones on the canyon walls, and an a'ā lava flow that buried 168 acres of the Eagle River floodplain. The lava is visually distinctive — rough, dark, clinkery basalt — and can be seen near the road.

The USGS classifies Dotsero as a "moderate threat" hazard, meaning it could erupt again. It is a monogenetic volcano (expected to erupt only once and then be finished), but the geology of the region doesn't preclude new volcanic vents forming nearby.

## 4. Glenwood Springs Town History

### The Ute People: Millennia Before the Resort

The Ute people — specifically the Parianuche band, known as the "elk people" — were the original inhabitants of western Colorado and had been using the Glenwood Springs hot springs for centuries before any European set foot in the Colorado Rockies. The springs they called Yampah, meaning "Big Medicine" in the Ute language, were central to their healing traditions. Warriors would come to soak after battles. The sick were brought to the vapor caves. The springs were not recreational amenity; they were medicine.

The Utes were nomadic hunters and gatherers who followed the seasonal movements of elk, deer, and bison. They wintered at lower elevations and summered in the high mountains, and Glenwood Springs — at the confluence of the Colorado and Roaring Fork rivers, with abundant hot water and sheltered canyon terrain — was an important winter destination.

Chief Ouray, the Tabeguache Ute leader who was respected by both tribal members and U.S. government officials, negotiated treaties through the 1860s and 1870s. An 1868 treaty limited Ute territory near Aspen while preserving hunting grounds around Glenwood Springs. But the gold and silver strikes in the mountains created relentless settler pressure. By 1874, Ouray accepted a treaty surrendering four million acres for compensation that was never actually delivered.

The Meeker Massacre of 1879 — in which Ute warriors killed Indian Agent Nathan Meeker and his male employees at the White River Agency in response to Meeker's attempt to plow under their horse pasture and force them into agriculture — provided the political justification that settlers had been waiting for. In 1880, Congress voted to remove the Utes from Colorado entirely. In August 1881, the Army force-marched the Uncompahgre Ute band 225 miles to the Uintah Reservation in Utah. The White River Utes were sent to the same location. Colorado was opened for settlement immediately after.

The town of Glenwood Springs was incorporated in 1885, four years after the Ute removal. The hot springs the Ute had used for millennia became the foundation of a resort economy within a generation.

### James Landis and Isaac Cooper — The First Settlers

The first permanent homestead near the future Glenwood Springs site was established by James Landis, who settled at the confluence of the Roaring Fork and Grand (Colorado) Rivers in the early 1880s. In 1882, Civil War veteran Captain Isaac Cooper purchased Landis's property and established the Defiance Town and Land Company, acquiring 400 acres. Cooper named the settlement after his hometown of Glenwood, Iowa.

Glenwood Springs had electricity by 1886 — making it one of the first hydroelectrically lighted cities in the world, before New York City had widespread electric lighting. The railroad arrived in 1887, transforming what had been an isolated frontier outpost into an accessible resort destination.

### Walter Devereux and the World's Largest Hot Springs Pool

The person who turned Glenwood Springs from a frontier settlement into an internationally recognized resort was Walter Devereux, an engineer and silver baron from a wealthy eastern family. In 1886, Devereux and his brothers purchased the Yampah Spring and 10 acres from Isaac Cooper.

Devereux's vision was audacious: he would build the largest hot springs swimming pool in the world. Construction began in 1886 by diverting the natural flow of the Colorado River to the south side of a mid-river island. Workers excavated the original riverbed and paved it with fire brick. The walls of both the pool and the Richardson Romanesque-style bathhouse were built of Peachblow sandstone quarried along the Frying Pan River near Basalt. The bathhouse featured 42 sunken Roman vapor baths made of imported porcelain tubs — 30 for men, 12 for women — plus parlors, a gymnasium, a physician's office, smoking rooms, and reading rooms. The cost was $100,000 (roughly $3 million today).

The pool opened to the public on July 4, 1888. Measuring 615 feet by 75 feet, it set a world record for the largest hot springs pool that it still holds today. It holds 1 million gallons of water and is fed by the Yampah Spring flowing at over 2,000 gallons per minute. Glenwood Hot Springs became a world destination for wealthy Victorians within a decade.

### Hotel Colorado — The Medici Palace of the West

Flush with success from the pool, Devereux commissioned Hotel Colorado in 1891, completed and opened in 1893. Designed by New York architects Boring, Tilton & Mellon and modeled architecturally after the Villa de Medici in Rome, the hotel brought European grandeur to the Colorado frontier. Construction cost $850,000 — the equivalent of over $23 million today. The grand opening celebration featured an orchestra playing through the night, fireworks, and approximately 300 wealthy families.

The hotel's amenities were extraordinary for the era: a 25-foot waterfall in the central lounge, an outdoor electrically-lit fountain that shot waterspouts several hundred feet into the air. Glenwood Springs was a destination for the Gilded Age wealthy in the same way that Saratoga Springs or Newport served the East Coast.

President Theodore Roosevelt transformed the hotel into a temporary White House in 1905, hosting a three-week hunting trip and using the hotel's facilities as his official headquarters. He addressed crowds from the balcony. President William Howard Taft arrived in 1909 for his own visit. "Unsinkable" Molly Brown — the Denver socialite who survived the Titanic — was a regular guest. Buffalo Bill Cody stayed briefly. Actor Tom Mix filmed a movie there.

During Prohibition (enacted in Colorado in 1916), notorious outlaw Diamond Jack Alterie used the hotel as his headquarters and reportedly hosted Al Capone in the building's clandestine bar. The hotel operated secret speakeasies until Prohibition's repeal in 1933.

In 1943, the U.S. Navy leased the entire hotel as a naval convalescent hospital, treating 6,525 veterans through the end of World War II.

### Doc Holliday's Final 57 Days

John Henry "Doc" Holliday was 35 years old when he arrived in Glenwood Springs in the summer of 1887. He was not well. Born in Georgia, trained as a dentist at the Pennsylvania College of Dental Surgery (graduating 1872), he had contracted tuberculosis — then called consumption — while working as a dentist in Atlanta. The disease drove him west to drier climates, and it drove him to gambling, since he was too ill to practice dentistry reliably.

By the time he arrived in Glenwood Springs, Holliday had participated in the famous gunfight at the O.K. Corral in Tombstone, Arizona (1881) alongside Wyatt Earp, and had survived a federal manhunt, shootings, and years of frontier drifting through Dodge City, Denver, Leadville, and other boom towns. Wyatt Earp later described him as "a loyal friend and good company" and "the deadliest man with a six gun I ever knew."

Holliday came to Glenwood Springs because of its reputation for therapeutic mineral waters and the vapor caves — both were being promoted as cures for tuberculosis in that era. He checked into the Hotel Glenwood Springs, which would later burn down in 1945, its location now occupied by a western goods store. He worked briefly as a dentist and gambled during his stay.

He spent 57 days in Glenwood Springs. On the morning of November 8, 1887, he asked for a glass of whiskey, drank it, looked down at his bare feet — he had expected to die with his boots on, in a gunfight, not in bed — and reportedly said "This is funny." Then he died. He was 36.

The Reverend W.S. Rudolph of the Presbyterian Church presided at the graveside service that afternoon. No friends or family were present. He is buried in Linwood Cemetery, where visitors can hike the Doc Holliday Trail today, though the exact location of his grave within the cemetery remains uncertain — which, some say, is how he would have wanted it.

## 5. Plants of the Glenwood Springs Area — What to See in Late May

### The Ecological Setting: Multiple Plant Communities at One Location

Glenwood Springs sits at 5,756 feet at the confluence of two major river systems — the Colorado River and the Roaring Fork — where the canyon opens into a broader valley. This creates an unusual juxtaposition of plant communities within a short distance: riparian woodland right along the rivers, Gambel oak and mountain shrub on the canyon slopes, and sage-dominated dry uplands on the benches above. In late May, all of these communities are actively transitioning from winter dormancy into full spring growth, making this one of the best times of year for botanical observation.

### Riparian Woodland Along the Colorado River — What's Happening Right Now

The dominant tree of Colorado River riparian zones at this elevation is narrowleaf cottonwood (*Populus angustifolia*). Right now — in late May at 5,756 feet — narrowleaf cottonwoods are in full early-summer leaf. Their flowers (catkins) actually appear 1-2 weeks before leaf initiation, typically in April-May at this elevation. By late May, the leaves are fully out but still that intense, translucent yellow-green of new growth, not yet the darker green of summer. The trees are actively growing their new shoots.

Within 2-4 weeks, the female cottonwoods will release their seed cotton — the white fluffy material that gives the tree its name. This is pure seed dispersal via wind, each seed attached to a silky fiber that keeps it airborne. By June the cotton will be flying everywhere.

Narrowleaf cottonwood forms dense stands along the river's alluvial banks, colonizing freshly deposited sediment. Its roots are enormously effective at stabilizing streambanks. The trees grow clonally — large groves may be a single genetic individual spreading via root sprouts — which creates the uniform appearance of many cottonwood stands.

Associated species in the riparian zone at Glenwood Springs include:
- **Box elder** (*Acer negundo*) — a fast-growing maple, distinctive for its compound leaves (unusual for a maple), growing as a shrubby tree along the river margins
- **Red-osier dogwood** (*Cornus sericea*) — shrubby, with bright red winter stems now covered in white flowers in late May (look for flat-topped clusters of tiny white flowers)
- **Thinleaf alder** (*Alnus incana tenuifolia*) — a shrubby tree with catkins, nitrogen-fixing (a remarkably valuable ecological function — it fertilizes the soil around it)
- **Chokecherry** (*Prunus virginiana*) — covered in dense white flower clusters in late May, a signature sight along Colorado riparian corridors; the clusters of small white flowers on pendulous racemes are unmistakable
- **Rocky Mountain maple** (*Acer glabrum*) — another shrubby maple, typically in canyon shade
- **Wild plum** (*Prunus americana*) — may still be in late bloom in late May; the flowers appear before the leaves, white and fragrant
- **Western serviceberry** (*Amelanchier alnifolia*) — has typically finished blooming by late May at this elevation, but still in leaf; produces edible purple berries later in summer that are a critical food source for bears, deer, and birds

### Gambel Oak (*Quercus gambelii*) — The Canyon Slope Signature

On the rocky, sunny slopes above the river and throughout the canyon terrain, Gambel oak dominates. This is the signature shrub-to-small-tree of the Colorado foothills between roughly 5,000 and 8,000 feet elevation. In late May, Gambel oak is in active bloom — the tiny wind-pollinated flowers appear between May and July. The leaves have emerged from the characteristic rounded, lobed shape and are still that bright green of new growth.

Gambel oak is remarkable for its resilience. It reproduces both by seed (acorns) and by clonal rhizomes — underground root systems that allow entire hillsides to regenerate from a single root network after fire. After a wildfire, Gambel oak sprouts from its roots within weeks. It is the ecological foundation of the foothills zone: its dense stands provide shelter for mule deer, black bears eat its acorns, and its thickets create the structural complexity that makes foothills habitat so productive for wildlife.

At this season, look for:
- **Big sagebrush** (*Artemisia tridentata*) on dry benches and south-facing slopes — its silvery gray-green aromatic foliage is distinctive and fragrant
- **Bitterbrush** (*Purshia tridentata*) — a yellow-flowering shrub critical as deer winter browse, likely still in late bloom in late May at this elevation
- **Birchleaf mountain mahogany** (*Cercocarpus montanus*) — a gnarled, aromatic shrub with distinctive feathery seed plumes later in summer; no common flowers right now
- **Western serviceberry** — see above

### Wildflowers in Late May at 5,756 Feet

Late May at Glenwood Springs elevation is prime wildflower season for the lower foothills communities. The canyon-bottom and south-facing slopes are ahead of north-facing slopes by 2-3 weeks. What you should be seeing right now:

- **Cow parsnip** (*Heracleum maximum*) — the giant of the riparian zone, a robust perennial forb reaching 6-8 feet, with enormous compound umbel flower heads of tiny white flowers. Where there's moisture, there's cow parsnip. It's hard to miss.
- **Wild geranium** (*Geranium caespitosum*) — purple-pink five-petaled flowers on rocky slopes
- **Penstemon species** — several species of these tubular-flowered perennials are blooming in late May on rocky slopes, ranging from blue-lavender to scarlet
- **Yellow composites** — various species of sunflower relatives beginning to open on sunny slopes
- **Tufted hairgrass** and sedge communities in wet areas along the river

## 6. Wildlife in the Glenwood Springs Area — What to Actually Look For

### Bighorn Sheep — The Canyon Walls

The most dramatic wildlife experience arriving into Glenwood Springs is bighorn sheep (*Ovis canadensis*) on the rocky canyon walls along I-70. The cliffs east of town along Interstate 70 are prime bighorn habitat — the steep, inaccessible terrain gives them protection from predators, and the rocky substrate supports the grass, sedge, and shrub forage they prefer.

Rocky Mountain bighorn sheep are built for canyon terrain. Their hooves have a hard outer edge for traction on rock and a soft inner pad for grip, making them capable of moving at speed on terrain that would stop any predator. They are most active during daylight hours. Adult males (rams) and females (ewes) typically live separately outside of the November-December rut.

In late May, ewes are lambing — look for ewes accompanied by small, spindly lambs on ledges that seem impossibly precarious. The lambs are remarkably agile within days of birth.

Their diet at this season shifts from winter browse (willow, mountain mahogany, bitterbrush) to spring grasses and forbs. They descend to lower elevations in search of the fresh green growth that's just starting to come in on the canyon slopes.

### Mule Deer — Everywhere

Mule deer (*Odocoileus hemionus*) are ubiquitous throughout the Glenwood Springs area. They get their name from their large, mule-like ears. They are most active at dawn and dusk — the best viewing times are the 30-60 minutes around sunrise and sunset. During the day they rest in scrub oak thickets on the hillsides.

In late May, doe mule deer are in or near the end of their gestation period — fawns begin arriving in late May through June. The spotted fawns are hidden in tall grass or brush for the first several weeks of life, with the doe returning to nurse several times a day. If you see a fawn alone, it is not orphaned — the mother is nearby.

The scrub oak (Gambel oak) hillsides surrounding Glenwood Springs are prime mule deer habitat, and deer frequently move through town, including into backyard gardens.

### Black Bears — Active Now After Winter Dormancy

Spring is one of the best times to see black bears (*Ursus americanus*) in the Glenwood Springs area because the bears are hungry after winter dormancy and actively foraging. Colorado black bears typically emerge from their dens in late March through April, and by late May they are in full active foraging mode.

What are they eating right now? In late May, the bear diet includes:
- New spring grass and sedges (bears are surprisingly effective grazers in spring)
- Forbs — especially clover and other nitrogen-rich green vegetation
- Insects — rotten logs are torn apart for beetle larvae and ants
- Any remaining carrion from winter

The serviceberry and chokecherry that line the riparian zones haven't fruited yet (that's July-August), so spring bears are working harder for less caloric return than they will in the fall. Males typically weigh 275-450 pounds; females are smaller. Their sense of smell is extraordinarily acute — they can detect food at five miles distance.

Black bears in the Glenwood Springs area have become highly accustomed to human food sources, which creates regular bear-human conflicts. The town has bear-proof trash regulations. Never leave food in a vehicle.

### Golden Eagle and Osprey Along the Rivers

The two rivers at Glenwood Springs — the Colorado and the Roaring Fork — support thriving raptor populations because they offer what raptors need: fish in the water and open corridors for hunting.

**Osprey** (*Pandion haliaetus*) are active fishing specialists along the Colorado River right now. In late May, Glenwood Springs osprey are actively nesting — pairs typically have established their platform nests on power poles, bridge structures, or tall riverside trees by early May, and eggs have been laid or are actively incubating. Watch for osprey hovering 20-60 feet above the river surface, then plunging feet-first into the water to grab fish. Their feet have rough, spiny pads and reversible outer toes that help grip slippery fish. They catch trout regularly from the Colorado River.

**Golden eagles** (*Aquila chrysaetos*) use the canyon updrafts for soaring and hunt the open slopes for ground squirrels, rabbits, and occasionally mule deer fawns. They are visually distinct from bald eagles by their entirely dark brown plumage with a golden sheen on the back of the head.

**Bald eagles** (*Haliaeetus leucocephalus*) also frequent the Colorado River — the river corridor is a recognized wintering area for bald eagles in Colorado, but some remain year-round. Adults are unmistakable with their white head and tail.

**Steller's jays** (*Cyanocitta stelleri*) — noisy, brilliant blue-and-black birds in the canyon's pine and oak zones — are the Colorado equivalent of blue jays and equally assertive.

**Western tanager** (*Piranga ludoviciana*) — males are among the most spectacular birds in North America, with a red head, yellow body, and black wings. They are migrating through or establishing breeding territories right now in the canyon woodlands.

### Rocky Mountain Elk

Rocky Mountain elk (*Cervus canadensis*) herds congregate in the open meadows and irrigated areas between Glenwood Springs and Carbondale (the valley south of town on Highway 82). In late May, elk are transitioning from their wintering ranges at lower elevation to higher summer pastures in the White River National Forest. You may see small groups still at lower elevations, especially in early morning in the meadows along the Roaring Fork.

## 7. Altitude Physiology at 5,756 Feet — What Annette and Bob Will Notice

### The Physical Reality at 5,756 Feet

Glenwood Springs sits at 5,756 feet (1,754 meters) above sea level. At this elevation, atmospheric pressure is approximately 83% of sea-level pressure. This means that each breath contains approximately 17% less oxygen than the same breath at sea level — not because the fraction of oxygen in air changes (it's still 20.9% everywhere in the atmosphere), but because the total number of air molecules per breath decreases with altitude.

This is the moderate altitude range. Most healthy people can go up to 8,000 feet with minimal significant effect, but 5,756 feet is high enough that the body registers the difference, especially if arriving from sea level or low elevation.

### The Immediate Physiological Response

Within the first few hours at altitude, the body initiates several compensatory responses:

1. **Increased breathing depth**: The respiratory control center in the brainstem detects reduced blood oxygen saturation and increases both the depth and, to a lesser extent, the rate of breathing. You may notice that you're breathing more deeply than usual, particularly during light exertion.

2. **Increased heart rate**: To compensate for lower oxygen per breath, the cardiovascular system increases cardiac output, primarily by increasing heart rate. Your resting heart rate may be 5-15 beats per minute higher than at home for the first day or two.

3. **Increased pulmonary artery pressure**: Blood is redistributed into lung regions that are normally under-perfused at sea level, maximally recruiting the gas exchange surface area of the lung.

4. **Enzyme changes**: The body increases production of 2,3-bisphosphoglycerate (2,3-BPG), a molecule that reduces hemoglobin's affinity for oxygen — counterintuitively, this actually facilitates oxygen delivery to tissues at lower partial pressures.

The full acclimatization process takes 1-3 days at this elevation. Full red-blood-cell polycythemia (increased RBC mass) takes weeks to develop, but the acute responses are immediate.

### Sleep at Altitude

One of the most predictable effects at 5,756 feet is disturbed sleep, particularly the first night or two. Periodic breathing — rhythmic oscillation between rapid, deep breathing and shallow or momentarily absent breathing — is common at altitude. It's controlled by the respiratory center's overcorrection: the deep breaths lower CO₂ below the threshold that triggers breathing, causing a brief pause, then the rising CO₂ triggers a burst of deep breaths again. It typically doesn't wake you up but reduces sleep quality.

### Dehydration at Altitude

The increased breathing rate at altitude significantly increases respiratory water loss — you're exhaling more air per hour, and each exhalation is saturated with water vapor. Combined with the dry air of the high-altitude mountain climate, this creates meaningful dehydration risk even without physical exertion. Recommended fluid intake at altitude is 3-4 liters per day, more if physically active.

Alcohol is more dehydrating at altitude and its effects are modestly amplified. Worth knowing for the first day.

### The Hot Springs + Altitude Interaction — A Physiological Double-Whammy

Here is where it gets genuinely interesting for a science-literate traveler. You're at altitude — your cardiovascular system is already compensating, your heart rate is somewhat elevated, your blood vessels are already in a mildly vasodilated state to optimize oxygen delivery. Then you immerse in 98-108°F water.

Hot water immersion produces thermoregulatory vasodilation: the body responds to core temperature elevation by massively increasing blood flow to the skin surface for cooling. This is accomplished by vasodilatation of cutaneous blood vessels, redistributing blood from the central circulation to the periphery. The result:

- Cardiac output increases significantly (primarily via increased heart rate; stroke volume remains roughly unchanged)
- Core blood pressure drops as vascular resistance falls
- Skin blood flow can increase by 2-3 liters per minute

A 2023 research study on balneotherapy found that during 40-minute hot spring immersion, central blood pressure decreased approximately 10 mmHg, heart rate increased up to 20%, and blood oxygen saturation measurably declined during bathing. Summer bathing (in warm ambient temperatures, as in late May) showed 28% higher heart rate increase and 55% higher cardiac output increase compared to spring baseline — meaning the cardiovascular demand of hot spring soaking is higher in warm weather.

At altitude, you're adding this heat-induced cardiovascular stress to an already-compensating system. This is not a reason to avoid the springs — billions of people worldwide use hot springs safely — but it is a reason to:
- Stay well hydrated before getting in
- Limit continuous soaking to 20 minutes per session
- Allow cool-down periods between soaks
- Listen to your body: dizziness, nausea, or headache in the pool means get out and cool down

The cold plunge at Iron Mountain Hot Springs is not just a fashionable amenity — alternating between hot and cold is a cardiovascular workout, training vascular reactivity. The physiological term is "contrast hydrotherapy."

## 8. The Yampah Spa Vapor Caves — Geology, History, and Why They Exist

### What the Vapor Caves Are

The Yampah Spa & Vapor Caves, at 709 E. 6th Street in Glenwood Springs, contains the only naturally occurring vapor caves in North America — and among the very few on Earth. They are not spa grottos engineered to feel cave-like. They are actual underground chambers carved by geothermal processes, accessed by descending a staircase from the spa building. The three chambers are connected by a concrete-and-brick-reinforced tunnel, but the rock surfaces, loose dirt, and natural steam environment remain. Conditions inside: approximately 110-113°F (43-45°C) with 100% relative humidity.

Yampah is the Ute word for "Big Medicine." These springs have been used for healing for thousands of years.

### How the Caves Were Formed — The Geochemistry

The vapor caves are karst features — caves formed by chemical dissolution of soluble rock. The specific mechanism here is more complex than typical limestone karst, and it's why these caves are scientifically unusual.

Richard Rhinehart, digital editor of Rocky Mountain Caving, describes the formation process as a "mixing zone cave": "You have what's known as a mixing zone cave, where you have the water coming down through cracks from rain and snow melt up above, but you also have the warm water coming up and the two of them together can be really powerful at chemically eroding out these cave passages."

The chemical mechanism: both the descending meteoric water and the ascending geothermal water carry dissolved CO₂. CO₂ + H₂O = H₂CO₃ (carbonic acid). Carbonic acid is particularly aggressive at dissolving the Leadville Limestone (calcium carbonate, CaCO₃) in the chemical reaction: CaCO₃ + H₂CO₃ → Ca²⁺ + 2HCO₃⁻. The mixing of the two water sources — different temperatures, different CO₂ concentrations, different ionic compositions — creates conditions that are more corrosive to limestone than either water source alone, a phenomenon called "mixing corrosion."

Over millions of years, this process has carved the cave passages through the Leadville Limestone beneath Glenwood Springs. The Vapor Caves are one expression of this process; Glenwood Caverns Adventure Park, on the mountain above town, is another, larger expression of the same dissolution mechanism.

### The Thermal Water Source and Its Chemistry

Hot mineral water flows through the cave floors at 125°F (52°C) — above the temperature of Iron Mountain's pools — and creates natural geothermal steam that fills the chambers. The water originates from springs in the hillside behind the spa. Total flow across the Glenwood Springs spring complex is approximately 6,000 gallons per minute, with temperatures at individual springs reaching 44-51°C (111-124°F) at the surface.

The vapor cave water has been analyzed and found to contain 34 minerals and trace elements. The mineral profile includes:
- Sodium: 6,600 mg/L (significant — this is about 19% of seawater sodium concentration)
- Chloride: 9,000 mg/L 
- Sulfate: 1,800 mg/L
- Zinc: 0.044 mg/L
- Barium: 0.4 mg/L
- Boron, copper, iron, lithium, magnesium, manganese, potassium, silicon — all present

The high sodium-chloride content reflects the deep circulation through Permian-Pennsylvanian evaporite sequences (ancient salt deposits). The sulfate reflects dissolution of gypsum and anhydrite beds. The lithium is characteristic of geothermal water that has circulated through granitic crust at depth — lithium is abundant in feldspars and micas that decompose under hydrothermal conditions.

Sitting in 110°F steam in a naturally carved limestone cave breathing mineral-laden vapor is a genuinely unusual physiological experience. The skin and respiratory mucosa are simultaneously exposed to elevated temperature, elevated humidity, and this mineral cocktail.

### Three Caves, Three Different Fates

The broader Glenwood Springs geothermal complex originally had at least three distinct cave features:

**Cave No. 1**: The first to be commercially exploited. Initially, patients were literally lowered into the cave on a stretcher, then the opening was sealed to trap the steam. Railroad construction subsequently led to visitor fatalities at this location (the mechanism is not fully described in available sources), and Cave No. 1 was closed.

**Cave No. 2**: Operated as an artificial shaft to accommodate overflow guests when the main cave was busy. This supplemental approach served the demand during the Victorian-era health tourism boom.

**Cave No. 3**: The foundation of the current Yampah Spa & Vapor Caves operation, in continuous commercial use since 1893. Three chambers now exist, connected by reinforced tunnel, with electric lighting and marble benches providing somewhat more comfort than the original candlelit experience.

### The Ute Sacred History

Before the 1881 removal, Ute warriors came to the vapor caves to treat wounds after battle and to pray. The Southern Ute Tribal Historic Preservation Officer has described the caves as analogous to their sweat lodges, "going back into Mother Earth." The use was spiritual as well as therapeutic — the caves occupied a sacred role in Ute cosmology, not merely a medical one.

The broader Glenwood Springs spring complex was contested territory: the Utes, Arapaho, and Cheyenne are all documented to have fought over access to these springs. A water source of this quality — hot, highly mineralized, with documented therapeutic effects — was worth fighting for in a pre-pharmaceutical world.

## 9. What to Notice While Driving Into Glenwood — The Canyon From I-70

### The Approach: 12.5 Miles of Geological Theater

Glenwood Canyon is 12.5 miles long. If you enter from the east (from Vail/Denver), you enter from the narrow, tight eastern end and progressively experience the canyon opening toward Glenwood Springs. If entering from the west (coming from Grand Junction direction), you approach through the wider, more open valley and then plunge into the canyon for the final 12 miles into town.

Either direction, the experience of driving I-70 through Glenwood Canyon is one of the most dramatic road-travel experiences in North America.

### The Engineering of the Road Itself

The last 12-mile section of I-70 to be completed in the entire interstate highway system was Glenwood Canyon — finished in 1992 after 12 years of construction and $490 million. The delay was not negligence; it was the extraordinary difficulty of threading a modern interstate through an extremely narrow, environmentally sensitive canyon while the environmental movement of the 1970s was demanding a new level of care.

The solution required:
- 40 bridges and viaducts (one roadway is elevated above the other to minimize footprint)
- 3 tunnels bored through solid rock
- 15 miles of retaining walls
- 30 million pounds of structural steel; 400,000 cubic yards of concrete
- A French-built slip-form gantry machine to construct the elevated roadway
- Rock faces that were stained after blasting to match natural exposures

The sheer walls along the highway are above a stable angle in many places — there is barely room for the road and the bike path between the river and the cliff face. Rocks ranging from softball to Volkswagen-Beetle size shed from the walls regularly. The canyon has been closed multiple times for rockfall, mudslides, and once catastrophically in 2021 when debris flows from the burned Grizzly Creek fire watershed buried the road under feet of mud.

### What to Look For on the Walls: Reading the Geology

**In the lower canyon walls, near river level**: The darkest, most irregular, contorted rock — granite and gneiss with swirling patterns, visible foliation in the metamorphic zones, occasional quartz veins cutting through the older rock. This is the Precambrian basement, 1.7 billion years old. These rocks have no fossils; life had not yet evolved complex forms when these crystallized.

**The Great Unconformity contact line**: Look for where the smooth, regular horizontal sedimentary layers above seem to be pasted directly onto the crumpled, irregular Precambrian below. This contact surface, where it's visible, represents 1.2 billion years of missing time.

**The Sawatch Sandstone just above the unconformity**: Light tan to buff-colored, well-layered, relatively thin unit. This is Cambrian beach sand, ~500 million years old.

**Climbing up the walls through the Paleozoic sequence**: The walls get lighter and more uniform in color through the carbonate units. The massive, light gray cliff bands of the Leadville Limestone dominate the upper canyon walls — look for the smooth, nearly vertical cliff faces with few horizontal beds visible. This is the same rock that is dissolved into caves above town.

**The Dotsero volcanic features**: If entering from the east, watch for dark, rough, clinkery dark rock near the road as you approach the Dotsero exit. This is the basaltic lava flow from the ~4,200-year-old eruption. It looks distinctly different from the surrounding sedimentary rocks — darker, rougher-textured, blocky. The crater itself is visible from the road as a round depression in the hillside.

### The Sensory Experience of the Canyon

Even without knowing the geological details, Glenwood Canyon does something to the human nervous system. The walls close in. The sky narrows to a ribbon. The sound of the river becomes continuous white noise. The scale is overwhelming — these walls are hundreds of feet taller than the tallest building in most American cities, and they're natural, and they've been here for three million years.

The Colorado River in late May is running high and fast from snowmelt — the color will be glacial green-brown, carrying silt from upstream snowpack. White water is visible in the tighter canyon sections. Rafting groups will be on the river.

The bike path that runs the entire length of the canyon — cantilevered in places off the cliff face, crossing the river twice on pedestrian bridges — is one of the most extraordinary recreation corridors in Colorado and worth mentioning to listeners as a reason to return for a non-driving experience of the canyon.

## 10. Additional Science Notes and Context

### The Glenwood Hot Springs Pool — Scale and Context

For context about the scale of the Glenwood Springs geothermal system: the main Glenwood Hot Springs Pool, across the river from Iron Mountain, is the world's largest mineral hot springs pool by documented world record. Measuring 615 feet by 75 feet (longer than two football fields), it holds approximately 1 million gallons of water. This is continuously refreshed by the Yampah Spring at over 2,000 gallons per minute — meaning the entire volume of the pool turns over in roughly 8 hours. Captain Richard Sopris, who arrived in the 1850s on a geological survey expedition, named the spring Grand Springs. He reportedly fell ill during his expedition and was taken by Utes to soak in the mineral waters.

### The Hanging Lake Travertine System

Just upstream in Glenwood Canyon (accessible by trail from the canyon), Hanging Lake is a National Natural Landmark where travertine — calcium carbonate precipitated from mineral-rich spring water — has built up a dam that holds the lake above the valley floor. This is the same chemical process that creates cave formations (stalactites, stalagmites) but operating at the surface. When mineral-rich water, supersaturated in calcium bicarbonate, contacts air and loses CO₂, it precipitates calcium carbonate as travertine. The result is the stunning turquoise pool that has become one of Colorado's most recognizable natural images. The aqua-blue color is from light scattering off extremely fine suspended travertine particles — the same principle that makes glacial lakes blue.

### Mt. Sopris — The Igneous Intrusion to the South

Visible from the Roaring Fork Valley south of Glenwood Springs, Mt. Sopris (12,965 feet, about 15 miles away) is an igneous intrusion — molten rock that was injected into the surrounding crust and then solidified, after which the overlying rock eroded away to leave the more resistant igneous core exposed. This type of feature is called a laccolith (if the intrusion domed the overlying layers) or a stock (if it's a broader, more cylindrical body). Mt. Sopris's distinctive twin summits are symmetrical because the intrusion was roughly circular in plan view.

### The Colorado River in Late May

The Colorado River flowing through Glenwood Springs in late May is near its annual peak discharge, fed by snowmelt from the upper Colorado basin, the Roaring Fork basin, and all their tributaries. The river will be running at roughly 3,000-8,000 cubic feet per second depending on the snowpack year — fast, turbid, powerful. The color is characteristically glacial — green-brown, not the clear blue of late summer low water.

This is peak whitewater rafting season. Class III-IV rapids at Shoshone (just upstream in the canyon) are the draw for commercial rafting operations. The power station at Shoshone uses a senior water right — one of Colorado's oldest — that actually takes precedence over downstream municipal water rights in dry years, a source of ongoing water law controversy in Colorado.
