## 1. Hanging Lake Overview — What It Is and Why It Matters

Hanging Lake is a small, spectacularly colored lake perched on the cliff wall of Glenwood Canyon, approximately 1,200 feet above the canyon floor, accessible only by trail from I-70. It is one of the most visited natural features in Colorado and one of the most geologically unusual lakes on the continent.

The National Park Service designated Hanging Lake a **National Natural Landmark in June 2011**, citing it as "an outstanding example of a lake formed by travertine deposition around the shoreline." The designation covers approximately 72 acres of federally owned land within White River National Forest.

The lake is remarkable for two converging reasons: its geological rarity (an actively forming travertine dam holding a lake above a valley floor) and its associated hanging garden ecology (moisture-dependent plant communities on the surrounding cliff faces that are extremely unusual at this latitude and elevation).

## 2. Travertine Geology — How the Lake Formed and Why It's That Color

### How Hanging Lake Formed

Hanging Lake formed through the combination of two geological events:

1. **A geologic fault drop**: A geological fault in the canyon wall caused a section of the valley floor to sink downward, dropping away from the surrounding terrain. This drop created the "hanging" position — the lake sits above the main canyon floor on what was once a lower portion of valley that tilted and subsided.

2. **Active travertine dam construction**: Dead Horse Creek, which flows into the lake from above, is supersaturated with dissolved calcium carbonate (limestone). As this water flows over the lake's edges, it loses carbon dioxide on contact with air, which reduces its ability to hold calcium carbonate in solution. The calcium carbonate precipitates out as travertine — a dense, layered limestone. Over thousands of years, this precipitation has built up a travertine rim around the lake that now holds the water in place like a natural dam. The process continues today: Hanging Lake is an actively forming, still-changing geological system.

This is identical in chemistry to how stalactites and stalagmites form in caves (CaCO₃ precipitation from CO₂ loss), but operating at the surface in flowing water rather than in still cave air.

### Why This Is Scientifically Rare

Travertine lakes and travertine dams are uncommon globally. The famous examples include Plitvice Lakes in Croatia and Pamukkale in Turkey. In the United States, Yellowstone's terraces and this site are among the most documented examples. What makes Hanging Lake distinct is the combination of an active, biologically colonized travertine system with a genuinely "hanging" position above a major canyon — rather than a flat terrace deposit. It is one of Colorado's largest active travertine systems.

The travertine is extremely fragile. The oils from human skin destroy the mineral structure. Swimming is permanently banned. Boardwalks exist specifically to prevent foot contact with the formations. Even touching the logs that extend into the lake can accelerate deterioration of the formations built up on them.

### Why the Water Is That Turquoise Color

The vivid blue-green color of Hanging Lake is produced by two optical mechanisms:

1. **Light scattering by suspended travertine particles**: The water contains extremely fine suspended particles of calcium carbonate. These particles scatter incoming light preferentially at shorter (blue) wavelengths — the same physics that makes glacial lakes appear turquoise. The particles are so fine they remain suspended and don't make the water look cloudy; instead they produce the characteristic mineral-lake blue.

2. **Dissolved mineral background**: The high mineral content of the water (particularly calcium and bicarbonate from Dead Horse Creek's limestone-dissolving path) gives the water a slight optical density that enhances the blue tones. The water is described as "crystal clear" — you can see the lake bottom, typically 10-15 feet down — but the color is unmistakable.

The result is a color that looks photoshopped even when you're standing at the edge looking at it in person.

## 3. Spouting Rock — The Waterfall Through Solid Rock

Spouting Rock is a short side trail detour (about 10-15 minutes) above Hanging Lake that leads to one of the canyon's most visually dramatic features: a powerful waterfall emerging directly from a hole in a solid limestone cliff face. The water does not flow over the top of the cliff. It flows through the rock itself, shooting horizontally out of the cliff in a curtain of white water.

### How It Works

The Leadville Limestone that forms Glenwood Canyon's walls is both soluble and fractured. Dead Horse Creek, flowing above, percolates down through these fractures and joints in the limestone. When the descending water encounters an impermeable layer (typically shale or a dense zone within the limestone), it can no longer continue downward and is redirected horizontally along the impermeable contact. This horizontal flow follows fractures and dissolution channels through the limestone until it finds an exit: in this case, through the cliff face at Spouting Rock.

The result is a classic karst discharge feature — water emerging from a spring in a cliff face — but at an unusually dramatic scale. The water has essentially created its own subsurface plumbing system through the limestone over thousands of years of dissolving and widening fractures. What looks like a geological magic trick is actually the Leadville Limestone's well-documented tendency to route water through its own body rather than over its surface.

The same chemistry that forms the Glenwood Vapor Caves and Glenwood Caverns is operating here at smaller scale: water dissolving limestone creates passages, passages route water, water exits through cliff faces as springs.

## 4. The Trail — What You See on the Way Up

### Trail Specifications

- **Distance**: 2.4 miles round trip (some sources cite up to 2.76 miles depending on measurement method), approximately 1.2 miles one-way
- **Elevation gain**: 1,000 to 1,200 feet over that 1.2 miles — steep by any measure
- **Difficulty**: Moderate to strenuous; involves rocky steps and several footbridges
- **What makes it worth it**: The grade is consistently steep with no flat relief sections, but the destination is genuinely extraordinary

### Dead Horse Creek — Your Trail Companion

The trail follows Dead Horse Creek the entire way up. This is the same creek that feeds Hanging Lake above. The name comes from early settlers; the canyon was historically called Dead Horse Canyon. The creek is a fast, cold, clear mountain stream running high in May from snowmelt.

Dead Horse Creek is precisely the type of habitat American Dippers (see Wildlife section below) require. The water is fast-moving, oxygenated, clear enough to see the bottom, cold year-round, and produces abundant aquatic invertebrates. If you see a small, slate-gray, wren-like bird standing on a rock in the rushing water and repeatedly dipping its body up and down — that is a dipper.

### What You Encounter Along the Trail

- **Footbridges** crossing Dead Horse Creek several times as the trail switchbacks up the canyon
- **Riparian vegetation** along the creek: mosses, moisture-loving ferns, alders, willows in the creek bottom
- **Canyon walls** closing in as you gain elevation — you're in a side canyon of Glenwood Canyon, narrower and more sheltered
- **Evidence of the 2020 Grizzly Creek Fire** in some sections — burned snags mixed with vigorous regrowth
- **Large boulders** as natural steps in the steeper sections
- **The boardwalk at the lake** — constructed to protect travertine; you cannot leave the boardwalk at the lake

### Plants Specifically on This Trail

The trail's microclimate is distinctly different from the main canyon. The narrow side canyon creates:
- More shade than the main Glenwood Canyon walls
- Higher humidity from the creek spray and seeps
- Calcium-rich drips and seeps from the travertine-laden water

This creates conditions for the hanging garden plant community described in Section 5.

## 5. Plants — Hanging Gardens, Maidenhair Fern, and the Microecology

### What Hanging Gardens Are

"Hanging gardens" is a specific ecological term for plant communities that grow on cliff faces and canyon walls where water seeps out of the rock. They are not gardens in the human sense; they are naturally occurring, moisture-dependent plant communities that colonize the wet vertical surfaces of canyon walls, spring seeps, and wet cliff faces.

In the Colorado Plateau and canyon country, hanging gardens are highly localized ecosystems that function like biological islands — surrounded by dry, rocky, or upland habitat on all sides, but maintaining a perpetually moist microenvironment from seeping groundwater. They support species that cannot survive in the surrounding dry climate but thrive in the constant moisture of the seep zone.

At Hanging Lake, the travertine-mineral-laden seeps and the spray from the waterfalls create these conditions on the surrounding cliff faces.

### Maidenhair Fern — The Star Plant

**Maidenhair Fern** (*Adiantum capillus-veneris* — Southern Maidenhair Fern, also sometimes called Venus'-Hair Fern) is the signature plant of hanging gardens in canyon country, including at Hanging Lake. It is one of the most visually distinctive plants in its environment: delicate, fan-shaped leaflets on thin, wiry, near-black stems that drape in curtains from wet cliff faces. The leaflets are bright, translucent green and catch light in a way that makes them look lit from within.

**Why it's rare here**: Adiantum capillus-veneris requires consistently moist conditions — the kind found at spring seeps, waterfall spray zones, and wet cliff faces. In Colorado's typically dry mountain climate, these conditions exist only in microhabitats like Hanging Lake's canyon walls. The species is near its northern elevation limit here. It cannot survive where the rock dries out seasonally.

**What to look for**: Drooping curtains of bright green, fan-shaped leaflets on thin black stems, growing directly on wet limestone and travertine rock faces. The stems are almost wire-thin. The leaflets are lobed, fan-shaped, and about fingernail-sized. When the sunlight hits a curtain of maidenhair fern growing on wet travertine, it glows.

The USGS documents Adiantum capillus-veneris as a characteristic species of hanging garden communities in canyon country, particularly growing on "the back and face walls" of seep environments, "clinging" to calcareous rock. These hanging garden communities on the Colorado Plateau are recognized by the USGS National Vegetation Classification System as a distinct ecological community.

**Status**: Do not pick or disturb. These plants are part of the protected National Natural Landmark ecosystem.

### Associated Hanging Garden Plants

Other moisture-loving plants you may see in the spray zone and on wet cliff faces:

- **Monkey Flower** (*Mimulus guttatus* or related species): Yellow, tubular flowers with spotted throats, growing directly in seeps and along stream margins. Often found alongside maidenhair fern in the same hanging garden communities.
- **Mosses**: Dense, bright green carpets on perpetually wet surfaces — travertine-depositing mosses that are themselves building the formations they grow on.
- **Sullivantia** (*Sullivantia hapemanii var. pupusii*): An extremely rare Colorado endemic plant identified specifically from the Hanging Lake National Natural Landmark evaluation. Ranked globally imperiled (G3T3) by NatureServe. The Hanging Lake population is described in the NNL evaluation as the largest population in the Southern Rocky Mountain Region with excellent viability.
- **Water cress** and other aquatic plants in the creek itself

### The Riparian Vegetation on the Trail

Along Dead Horse Creek below the lake:
- **Alder** (*Alnus* spp.) — nitrogen-fixing shrubby trees colonizing the creek banks
- **Willow** (*Salix* spp.) — low, moisture-loving shrubs along the creek's edge
- **Mosses** covering every wet rock surface
- **Various sedges** in the creek margins

## 6. Wildlife — American Dipper on Dead Horse Creek

### American Dipper (*Cinclus mexicanus*)

The American Dipper is the bird you are most likely to notice on the trail up to Hanging Lake, because its behavior is unlike any other bird you will encounter on a hike.

**What to look for**: A small (robin-sized), plump, uniformly slate-gray bird with a brownish head and a short, wren-like tail — standing on a rock in the middle of the rushing creek, repeatedly dipping its entire body up and down in a rapid bobbing motion. It will walk off the rock and into the water without hesitation, disappear underwater, and reappear downstream on another rock. This is normal behavior.

The Colorado Birding Trail documents American Dippers as residents in "the wet spray and near the falls" at Hanging Lake specifically, and at Veltus Park near Glenwood Springs in riparian habitat year-round.

**Why it walks underwater**: The American Dipper is North America's only truly aquatic songbird. It forages by walking along the stream bottom on submerged rocks and gravel, picking up aquatic insects, larvae, small fish, and fish eggs from under rocks and debris. Its adaptations for this:
- A transparent third eyelid (nictitating membrane) for underwater vision
- Nostrils that close when submerged
- Short, powerful wings that it uses for swimming (and flight)
- Unusually high hemoglobin levels for enhanced oxygen storage during dives
- Dense, waterproof feathering with secretions from an oversized preen gland
- Long, strong (unwebbed) toes that grip submerged rocks against the current

A typical dive lasts 5-15 seconds. The dipper walks against the current, gripping rocks to keep from being swept downstream.

**The bobbing**: The constant up-down body dip that gives the bird its name may serve multiple functions — improving vision into the water surface, communicating in noisy stream environments (the creek sound makes calls hard to hear), or simply maintaining muscle tension. It does this continuously and compulsively.

**Year-round resident**: Dippers do not migrate. They stay on their home streams through Colorado winters, moving only to find open (unfrozen) water. They sing year-round — their song is one of the most cheerful sounds in winter mountain terrain.

**In May**: Adults are nesting. Dipper nests are dome-shaped constructions of moss, grass, and leaves built into cliff faces, under bridges, or behind waterfalls — the spray zone is preferred, which keeps nests moist and somewhat inaccessible to predators. Females lay 4-5 white eggs; fledglings leave the nest at about 25 days.

## 7. Permit System — Practical Information for Today

Hanging Lake requires a timed-entry permit year-round, managed by the City of Glenwood Springs in conjunction with the U.S. Forest Service. The permit system was introduced in May 2019 to control visitor numbers after overcrowding threatened the travertine ecosystem.

**Seasonal pricing**:
- Peak season (May 1 – October 31 / December 31, sources vary): **$12 per person**
- Off-peak (November 1 – April 30): $10 per person

**Shuttle system** (operates May through October):
- Shuttle bus from Hanging Lake Welcome Center only during peak season
- Departs every 45 minutes, 6:45 a.m. to 4:30 p.m., seven days a week
- Accommodates 44 passengers per bus
- The Welcome Center has restrooms and parking

**Self-drive option**: I-70 Exit 125 (Hanging Lake Rest Area). Parking is limited.

**Reservations**: Through visitglenwood.com/hanginglake/ (Visit Glenwood's portal). Permits cover a 3-hour hiking window.

**No cell service** at the trailhead or on the trail.

**Restrictions at the lake**: No swimming, no fishing, no dogs, no drones, no direct contact with water or floating logs (protects travertine), stay on boardwalk.

## 8. History — Discovery, Name, and National Natural Landmark Designation

### Early History

Hanging Lake has been known to hikers and early Colorado settlers since the late 1800s, though the Ute people who used the Glenwood Canyon area certainly knew of the side canyon. The trail was developed in the early 20th century as the canyon became more accessible.

### National Natural Landmark Status

Hanging Lake received its **National Natural Landmark designation in June 2011** from the National Park Service. The designation cited the site as "an outstanding example of a lake formed by travertine deposition around the shoreline" and recognized the hanging garden plant community (particularly the globally imperiled Sullivantia population) as additional justification.

The 72-acre designated area is located entirely within White River National Forest under U.S. Forest Service jurisdiction.

**Note**: Some older sources cite a 1994 NNL date; the confirmed official designation per the NPS is 2011.

### The Permit System Crisis and Resolution

By the late 2010s, Hanging Lake was receiving approximately 1,000+ visitors per day in peak season — far beyond the ecological carrying capacity of the fragile travertine and hanging garden ecosystem. Footprints off the trail were compressing and destroying travertine formations that had taken thousands of years to form. The permit system introduced in May 2019 capped daily visitors and added the timed-entry structure now in place.
